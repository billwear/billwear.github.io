Motivating by thought

Laid back in my couch recliner, it's noble to spend time in mindfulness, but not particularly productive. There are many moments when I feel the restless urge to do something, anything, but I continue to sit, glued to the chair. There are more than enough real responsibilities, but none of them seem that urgent. In short, how does the mindful part of my brain override the self-indulgent, hedonistic parts?

Using mental self-observation – which is a horribly technical moniker – I can at least try to debug the problem.

First, let me set some levels: I have already established a lifelong habit of not attending to trivia. If it isn't a grand gesture, I'm not easily interested, and that's something about myself I find annoying and embarrassing. I'm quite sure that the urgent internal call to action is driven, at least in part, by that realization. But how to address it?

Brute force doesn't seem to work in a lasting way. Neither does reinforcement. If there is a way out of this box, it's through monitoring and discovering my thoughts. The challenge here, though, is to discover thoughts rooted in older, less articulate parts of my brain, specifically, the midbrain, which expresses thoughts in terms of emotions, and the brain stem, which expresses thoughts in physical states and sensations.

Taking stock of these (somewhat intertwined) secondary brains, I find:

  • I am groggy, even after about ten hours of sleep preceding this bank holiday, or perhaps because of the long sleep.
  • I feel satisfied; I'm a little hungry, but nonetheless comfortable, with no urgent physical needs, no expectations, and no imminent threats. What is the evolutionary logic of moving from this spot?
  • I feel put upon by expectations, which I can blame on society in general, the house I'm still renovating 11 months post-purchase, the undone laundry, some minor packing issues for my upcoming trip, and a host of things I would think that my partner would expect.
  • The air feels good, if a little cool on my bare feet. The seat is comfortably reclined, suggesting that yet another nap might be looming. The day is cloudy, the lamplight dim, and the noise level light. Ancient ancestors would have been well-satisfied.

It is this last observation that stirs up all three brains at once. Our forebears did not make the leap from stone knives and bearskins by sitting quietly in their caves. It was not in grand gestures, quite certainly, since I suspect that instincts don't carry one that far ar a leap. It was little things: cold feet, sickness from raw meat, rain blowing into the cave. Simple annoyances got them moving.

And here is my epiphany: rather than motivating myself, can I simply monitor my thoughts and focus on the smallest and slightest annoyance in sight?

There it is, minor angst over an empty soda can sitting next to me. Easy enough to lean forward and drop it in the trash. In motion, my thoughts and emotions turn to several similar problems: my daily meds, still not taken for today, within easy reach; empty cat food and soda cartons that got behind a chair; clean clothes on said chair and an overflowing laundry basket; the fact that some of these clothes could probably be hung up in my new bedroom without much ambition. None of these are conscious decisions, just the natural outflow of observed thoughts, feelings, and physical sensations.
