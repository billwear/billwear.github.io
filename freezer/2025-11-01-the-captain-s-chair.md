*All ahead full, Mr. Sulu.*

*Today is a personal reflection on my retirement after working for someone else for over fifty years. Bear with me!*

For years, I steered by other people’s stars. The routes were drawn, the ports assigned, and every course correction came stamped with a manager’s initials. When the wind turned, I didn’t tack, I filed a ticket. That’s how it goes when you work for someone else long enough: your own compass drifts below decks, wrapped in dust and salt.

Then one day, the orders stop coming. The horizon opens wider than it ever has — and instead of joy, I feel something quieter. Relief, yes. But also surprise. Because the silence of command leaves me with an unfamiliar sound: my own direction.

I wake to it slowly. Not a roar, but a pulse — steady, close, waiting to be heard again.

I realize the take-home is better than it’s been in years. The bills will be fine. The risk isn’t ruin anymore. It’s relevance. What does it mean, day after day, to wake up and steer your own ship? To build without orders, to act without permission, to know that no one’s expectations remain but your own?

At first, it feels like floating — relief without anchor. I lie on the bed too long. I wander my own house, half-owner, half-stranger. I find myself circling unfinished projects, each one whispering, “I’m waiting on you now.” That’s the new contract — between the worker and the self.

The app I  used to clock into is gone. The bench I build next will teach me a new song.

I go outside. The drain line still needs to go in while it’s warm. It’s not glamorous, but it’s gravity. The real work is learning how to re-enter a rhythm where I set the day’s weight. I begin to understand that retirement, aka self-employment, isn’t just freedom — it’s relationship. Between will and structure. Between rest and readiness.

And maybe, finally, between mind and motion.

At my desk, I open Emacs and realize it’s harder to use than it used to be. The config is tangled. Too many years of patches and accommodations. I feel it: the mirror of my life. So I clean. Line by line, layer by layer, removing what no longer fits. I stop when it feels light again — not empty, just mine.

And beneath that, a thought rises:

I'm not just the navigator now. I'm the ship, the wind, the tide, and the time.

That’s the truth of this season — not the sprint of career or the chase of recognition, but the long, deliberate art of steering my own days.

The compass didn’t break. It just waited for me to remember where North was. The undiscovered country awaits.

## For you

1. Clear the deck.

List every project that tugs at you, then cross out the ones that don’t carry your purpose. Life is short, but wide: use the extra space wisely.

2. Anchor one habit.

Choose a single daily cue — a time, a sound, a motion — to mark when you start creating. 

3. Build depth rituals.

Block two uninterrupted hours this week for work that demands full attention. Defend them fiercely.

4. Notice your defaults.

When you hesitate, ask if it’s protection or procrastination. Learn the pattern before you fight it. 

5. Aim at the next true task.

Everything else is noise. What one action moves the ship forward? 

6. Cut the false urgency.

Let most requests wait. Protect space for work that renews your sense of self.

7. Let your biology lead.

Rest when your body asks, not when the calendar allows. The organism knows the way home.

Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.

What are you steering toward, now that the wheel is finally yours?
