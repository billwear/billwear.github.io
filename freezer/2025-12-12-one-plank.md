This binary world loves polarized extremes. You are either disciplined or chaotic. Focused or scattered. In control or lost.

I lived inside that swing for most of my life.

Not a loop. A sine wave. Back and forth. Over and over. Every time I corrected, I overcorrected. Every time I found structure, I strangled myself with it. Every time I let go, I sank into the swamp.

From the outside it probably looked like progress. From the inside it felt like motion without arrival.

Here is the part I never said out loud.

I can meditate without effort. I usually don’t try. There is no wall between my conscious and subconscious mind. Images arrive fully formed. Structures stack themselves. Scenes render with logic I didn’t consciously choose.

I never touched drugs or alcohol. Not because I was afraid of losing control. Because I already live inside a continuous trip, and I was afraid medicating myself would make me normal.

I am always tuned in.

That gift came with a cost. If you already see too much, you learn to manage it by force. You oscillate. You clamp down. You release. You swing. You spend decades walking a winding path through mental swampland, waiting for something to resolve.

Then one day, I stopped trying to escape the swing.

I placed one plank.

Not a philosophy. Not a system.  
Not a reinvention. One small, deliberate structure. Just enough to keep my feet dry.

The world changed instantly.

The swamp disappeared. The ground resolved into a massive floor. Not a road. A hangar.

I finally understood what my mind had been doing my entire life. I was never meant to walk. I was meant to *pilot*.

The hangar was full of “readiness.” Tools. Fittings. Power lines. Not rules. Capacity.

Behind me stood a machine so large I couldn’t see it all at once. A land-moving aircraft built for heavy terrain *and* open sky. A ladder on the side. A cockpit high above the ground.

When I climbed in, the seatbelt closed around me. Not restraint. Integration.

I didn’t start the engine. The hangar opened on its own. The vehicle moved when alignment was right. Sailing through the clouds. And this craft is also meant for land, the perfect mix: head in the clouds, feet on the ground. 

This is the moment I escaped an entire life of oscillation. Whatever genetic mutation, birth defect, rare gift, or alien implant brought me to this, I’m thankful. 

Because now I can function. Not by choosing chaos. Not by choosing order. But by *choosing* function.

The images were never the problem. The visions were never the danger. The mistake was thinking I had to ***walk*** through them.

I don’t need to suppress the trip. I don’t need to indulge it either. It’s my normal mode. I need just enough structure to fly.

In his book, “A Writer’s Time,” literary agent Ken Atchity talks of “the continent of reason” and “the islands of creativity.” The trick to becoming productive, he intimates, is to birth your managing editor: learn to mediate between the two minds. 

Never understood what he was talking about because I don’t *have* islands. I’m hearing two, fully-formed beings: one rational, mathematical, and logical; the other creative, empathetic, and emotional. Fire and ice. Spock and McCoy. Structure and fancy.

And now, a third mind, the pilot. Let’s see what this Tardis can do. 

### For You
- 

Write down the one thing still bouncing in your head, then park it outside yourself.
- 

Make the smallest habit that keeps effort from pooling overnight.
- 

Protect one uninterrupted block where images can finish forming.
- 

Notice when you are correcting past center instead of choosing direction.
- 

Name the one function this season demands, not the ten that feel possible.
- 

Remove one obligation that exists only to prove discipline.
- 

Build the plank before you design the hangar.

I am not leaving the ground to escape life.  My father, whom I now realize was like me, ran to alcohol and nicotine to try and moderate the collisions. I am just leaving the swing and charting my own path.

Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.
