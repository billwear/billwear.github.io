Yesterday I re-introduced the Edge Ego — that fast, restless voice that generates your best ideas and your worst distractions. Today, let’s learn to recognize when it’s talking.

The first skill isn’t controlling your Edge Ego. It’s recognizing your current state. Not yesterday’s, not what you think it should be — what’s actually happening in your head right now.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ADMk!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7e9f8929-d998-4f8b-9b93-e870593a7cef_1301x1269.heic)

Here are six quick scenarios. Pick the response that feels closest to true. Don’t overthink it.

**1. Mood**

It’s Saturday morning, and you’re meeting a close friend for coffee. As you sit down in the booth, they say, “You know, your hair escaped the house kind of...unfinished.” If you had to respond right now, which would be closer to right?

A) What do you mean? (Red)  
B) Man, that’s a downer. (Yellow)  
C) I know, right? (with a laugh) (Green)  
D) Meh. Whatever. (Blue)

**2. Energy**

Your friend calls you at 10:30 PM. “My buddy is a little too drunk and he passed out in my front car seat. Can you come over and help me get him onto the couch?”

A) I’m not getting up at this hour. Deal with it. (Red)  
B) How often does this happen? Be there in a minute, though. (Yellow)  
C) I remember doing that! Be right there. (Green)  
D) Yeah, sure, but give me 20 minutes to wake up good. (Blue)

**3. Loop** *(What’s consuming your attention)*

You’re trying to work on your taxes, but you keep thinking about the argument you had with your brother last week.

A) This is pointless. I should just call him and hash it out now. (Red)  
B) Why can’t I just focus? This always happens. (Yellow)  
C) Okay, that’s looping. Let me acknowledge it and set it aside for later. (Green)  
D) Whatever. I’ll just let my mind wander until it stops. (Blue)

**4. Anchor** *(What actually matters vs. what should)*

You planned to spend your morning on your book manuscript, but you’re scrolling job postings instead.

A) The job search IS important. The manuscript can wait. (Red)  
B) I should be writing, but I just can’t get into it right now... (Yellow)  
C) Hmm. What I planned vs. what I’m doing don’t match. Let me check in. (Green)  
D) Eh, neither one feels important today. (Blue)

**5. Pivot** *(The one change that could help)*

You realize you’ve been stuck for an hour. What’s the ONE thing that could unstick you?

A) Nothing. This whole approach is wrong. Start over. (Red)  
B) Maybe if I just push through harder... (Yellow)  
C) I need a five-minute walk, then tackle the smallest piece. (Green)  
D) I don’t know. Maybe I’ll just work on something else. (Blue)

**6. Quest Pulse** *(Real-time course correction)*

Midday: Your plan was to write, but you’re actually enjoying organizing your workspace instead.

A) No. Stick to the plan. Organizing is procrastination. (Red)  
B) I’m off track again. Why does this keep happening? (Yellow)  
C) Interesting. Maybe organizing IS what I need right now. Check in: does this serve today? (Green)  
D) Plans change. Whatever happens, happens. (Blue)

**What Just Happened**

When you answered those questions, you weren’t deliberating. You just knew. That instant recognition happened because your Edge Ego operates at a completely different speed than your conscious mind.

Notice what happened when you read those scenarios. You didn’t weigh pros and cons or think about what a good person would choose. You just *knew* which answer was closest to true. That instant recognition — that’s not your Managing Editor talking. Your Managing Editor would have second-guessed, rationalized, maybe picked the “should” answer instead of the real one.

The Edge Ego doesn’t do that. It just responds. It knows your actual mood, your real energy level, what’s actually looping in your head versus what you tell yourself you should be thinking about.

**What The Colors Mean**

**Green** is where you want to be: aware, adaptive, responding to what’s actually happening rather than what you wish was happening or fear might happen. Green doesn’t mean everything’s perfect — it means you’re seeing clearly and working with reality.

**Yellow** is functional but deflated. You’re getting by, but there’s a questioning, second-guessing quality. You’re doing the thing while simultaneously doubting whether you should be doing the thing. Yellow gets stuff done, but it’s exhausting because you’re fighting yourself the whole time.

**Red** is reactive and rigid. You’re defending, resisting, or forcing. Something feels threatening or wrong, and you’re pushing back hard. Red can feel powerful in the moment, but it burns energy fast and usually escalates situations that didn’t need escalating.

**Blue** is disengaged. You’ve checked out. Not depressed necessarily, just... offline. Blue is what happens when your Edge Ego has given up trying to get your attention through the normal channels and just goes quiet.

Here’s the thing: **none of these are permanent states, and none of them are diagnoses.** They’re snapshots. You might wake up Green, hit Yellow by mid-morning when a project stalls, spike Red when someone criticizes your work, then drift Blue by evening when you’re too tired to care.

The skill isn’t staying Green all the time. The skill is *noticing* when you shift colors — and knowing what each color is trying to tell you.

But this six-question snapshot gives you the core skill: learning to recognize when your Edge Ego is talking, and what it’s trying to tell you.

Try this quiz again tomorrow. Notice if your colors shift. That awareness alone will start changing how you plan your days.
