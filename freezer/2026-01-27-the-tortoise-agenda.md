*Most* productivity advice assumes a fantasy human. 

They assume you wake up rested, eat clean by default, never get derailed by insurance paperwork, and feel morally neutral about your calendar. If that were true, none of this would be hard.

But today wasn’t a fantasy day. It was a real one.  I highlighted most, above, because Cal Newport is a huge exception.  You need to read him to find out why.  Google is your friend.

Anyway, I woke up foggy. My blood sugar had opinions. My head was crowded. I had too many domains that matter to me and not enough tolerance for pretending they’re all equal.

And that’s the point.

The week didn’t start working because I tried harder. It started working when I told the truth about **what actually pays**, **what compounds slowly**, and **what needs a container so it doesn’t eat everything else**.

Here’s the move most people miss:

You don’t schedule *tasks*. You schedule **lanes**, something like this:[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9pNY!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0952760c-7bb3-4d1c-bed3-e28a02a80454_3456x2234.jpeg)

The colors match the calendar list in the upper left.  This is extreme timeblocking, with the understanding that if something interrupts — say, a speech I’ve been asked to give, or a Friday family dinner outing — that block just doesn’t get serviced on that particular day.  No angst, no self-recrimination.  

This brings me to the most important point: A plan is just that: ***a plan***.  It *reflects *commitments you’ve made that you want to keep if possible, but if life intervenes (and it will), that’s just how the cookie crumbles, as my mom used to say.  A Navy SEAL friend of mine says it slightly differently: “The map is not the territory, but memorize the map anyway.”  It’s easier to deal with exceptions if they actually *have* something to be exceptions *to*. Otherwise, you’re just flopping around, following your nose, and that’s how people fail, more often than not

Sewing isn’t just “something I enjoy.” It’s a commitment to a craft, and based on the last few weeks, meaningful short-term income. That means it gets protected hours, daily touchpoints, and daylight energy. Not scraps.

Vibe coding isn’t a hustle. It’s public thinking, and something that may get me a part-time job if I need one.  It shows initiative, independent thinking, and devotion to important ideas that matter. That means bounded time, twice a week, with verification instead of performance.

Fiction isn’t urgent. It’s formative. That means steady, modest hours that don’t steal oxygen from rent.  And it can be formed into short stories, novellas, novels, and other forms that can be submitted (and get through their rejection cycles) really quickly, but only if you write regularly, and on a schedule.

Once I sorted the lanes, the calendar stopped feeling like a judge and started feeling like a relief.

Another thing that matters more than people admit: **polish time**.

Raw thinking and finished work are not the same cognitive mode. If you don’t explicitly reserve time to translate one into the other, you either never publish or you publish things you don’t stand behind.

So the structure became simple:
- 

early signal (often messy)
- 

morning polish (short, humane)
- 

real work (protected)
- 

afternoon translation (paid, explanatory)
- 

evening stop

Not optimal. Not perfect. Just *habitable*.

The last piece is the one nobody wants to hear:

If you miss a block, you don’t make it up.  
You log it and continue.

Making it up teaches your nervous system that structure is punishment. Logging it teaches your system that honesty is safe.

That’s how weeks start stacking instead of collapsing.

If you’re reading this and wondering whether you should copy my schedule: don’t.

Instead, ask yourself three questions:
- 

What actually moves the needle on your goals in the next 30–60 days?
- 

What will grow anyway, even if I touch it regularly but lightly?
- 

Where am I lying to myself about “flexibility” that’s really avoidance?

Answer those, then build lanes—not tasks.

Structure doesn’t give you discipline.  
It gives you permission to stop negotiating with yourself.

That’s what made today work.
