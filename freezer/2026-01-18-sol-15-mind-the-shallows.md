excerpt: "Deep work is not the same thing as hard work"

This morning I tried to play a “one-to-four player” D&D box game. What I expected was a dungeon — a place where choices matter, uncertainty is real, and careful thinking is rewarded — but what I actually encountered was something much closer to a bureaucracy in fantasy clothing.

Before I could make a single meaningful decision, I was shuffling, reshuffling, and trying to keep the pile straight.  Which card twisted what other card now?  Does this icon override that exception?  And what about that rule three pages back, mentioned once, in a paragraph that begins with “in rare cases?”

The cognitive load wasn’t coming from the dungeon. It was coming from the machinery. And somewhere around the third or fourth shuffle, I felt a familiar sensation creep in: fatigue that didn’t feel earned, the kind of tiredness that comes from servicing a system rather than inhabiting a problem.  It felt like today’s news or the career I retired from; certainly not fun, growth, or challenge.

That was the moment the connection snapped into focus.  What a mirror for our world right now: politics, the economy, work, tech — the whole thing.

A lot of what we call “hard work” today isn’t deep at all.  It’s petty, proverbial legalism. You know, “stay in your lane” vs. “think outside the box;” “win the game” vs. “play by the rules;” “might makes right” vs. the rights of man. It’s rote, shallow, and mechanical, and more often than not, the loudest and the fastest win.

It’s the work of tracking, complying, remembering, referencing, aligning, synchronizing, and maintaining coherence with a rule set that has grown not because reality demanded it, but because layers of other people’s decisions have accreted over time. And then just taking dangerous changes because nobody can figure out what’s right or wrong or fair.

Every rule once solved a problem. Every exception once felt necessary. Every treaty once kept the peace. But taken together, they turn thinking into bookkeeping. And bookkeeping is not deep work, and it’s very hard to do on the fly.

Deep work, at least the kind that actually changes you, is not about managing complexity — it’s about **meeting reality directly**, without unnecessary intermediaries. That’s what I was looking for in the dungeon. That’s the kind of hardness that Cal Newport is talking about when he describes deep work.  Mentally challenging, requiring you to directly address the main issues head on, and ignore all the little side-quests and checkboxes.

What I wanted was the hard kind of thinking that comes from moral and strategic choice: Do I press forward or retreat? Do I accept damage now to preserve options later? Do I act consistently with the values I claim to hold, even when it costs me an advantage? That kind of work is demanding, but it’s demanding in a way that feels clean. When you’re done, you’re *tired*, but you’re also *oriented*.

This box game made me tired without orienting me.  Just like watching the news, or working in a big corporation. The work wasn’t “what kind of person am I going to be in this situation?”  It’s “did I twist the right card in the right order?” That’s not deep work; that’s not even hard work. That’s just trying to minimize friction and walk away with a benefit, usually at the expense of someone else, often because you can’t see another way to survive.

It’s the same distinction I keep running into in the world outside the table.

So much modern effort goes into following procedures rather than exercising judgment. We mistake compliance for thinking, complexity for rigor, aggressiveness for bravery, and exhaustion for depth. We end the day depleted, not because we grappled with something meaningful, but because we carried too many rules in our head at once.

What struck me, sitting there with the cards, was how closely this mirrors the broader cultural confusion I’ve been circling all week. When systems become too complex to hold intuitively, people stop testing and start trusting. They cling to certainty, not because it’s true, but because it reduces cognitive load. They trade deep engagement for rule-following, and then defend the rules as if they were reality itself.

That’s the trap.

Deep work is not easier because it is simple. Deep work is easier because it is **honest and more direct**.

It asks fewer questions, but they matter more. It demands careful attention, but not constant supervision. It requires thinking — real thinking — but it doesn’t require you to keep forty-seven semi-rules active in working memory just to move forward one step.  

Most important, deep work, by its very nature, earns what it gets.  It doesn’t take it by force, bluster, bluff, or threat.  It doesn’t demand it as a religious or ethnic right.  Instead, it produce results that seem impossible in timeframes that feel unreal.  And yet in the long run, it’s actually simpler than anything else you could do. That’s why, paradoxically, deep work feels lighter once you’re inside it.

This is also why I’ve been gravitating toward solo D&D on Saturday mornings. When the dungeon is simple enough to disappear, what remains is choice. When the rules fade into the background, ethics come to the foreground. The work becomes about judgment, not administration. In a good dungeon, truth is never declared.  
It’s *tested*.

You act, the dungeon responds, and you live with the consequences. If your assumptions were wrong, the map corrects you. If your values were shallow, the cost becomes visible. There is no need to shout, justify, or appeal to authority. Reality does the talking. To me, *that* is deep work.

It’s slower, but it compounds. It’s harder to fake, but easier to sustain. And while it requires genuine effort, it does not grind you down in the way bureaucratic, mechanical hard work does.

Modern hard work exhausts you because it asks you to be a caretaker of systems. Deep work fulfills you because it asks you to be a thinker in the presence of reality.

The irony is that once you strip away all the bookkeeping and machinery, the work that remains is not only more effective, but far less tedious. You stop fighting the system and start engaging the problem. You stop shuffling and start deciding.

That’s the dungeon I want to build. That’s the kind of work I want to rehearse. And that’s the distinction I’m trying to protect this week.

Burn slow. Choose deeply. Let reality do the correcting.
