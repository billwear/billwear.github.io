You don’t need a better feeling. You need a better first stone. *One* stone.  In German, that would be “ein Stein.”  

We can stretch it a bit by bridging the two words (“Einstein”).  What’s very cool about this is that Albert Einstein was known for this kind of thinking: “Everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler.”

Most days die at the threshold. You stand there, scanning the mountain, waiting for a mood strong enough to lift it. That wait is the trap. Devotion doesn’t wait. It puts one rock in a bucket and tips the bucket toward gravity.

Tonight, let’s make that first stone obvious, repeatable, and hard to wriggle out of.

---

## The Einstein protocol 

How to start every day by moving one stone.

**1) Pre-select your stone the night before.**  
Pick the smallest visible snag that advances a real thread of your life. Put it in writing where you will see it at wake: a sticky note on the kettle, the laptop lid, or your phone lock screen.
- 

Writing example: “Replace the opener with a 2-line hook.”
- 

Code example: “Rename the misnamed function and re-run tests.”
- 

Sewing example: “Square and join 2 coffee squares; press seam.”
- 

Admin example: “Snap both sides of the check; initiate mobile deposit.”

**2) Give it a time box and a start verb.**  
Every stone starts with a friction-free verb you can do half-asleep. Set 10 minutes. The point is not to finish; it’s to tip momentum.
- 

Start verbs: open, rename, cut, pin, write, run, delete, file, send, stitch, commit, schedule.

**3) Reduce it one level further than you think you need.**  
If you can fail to start, it’s too big.  Try this process:
- 

Writing: Not “fix section 2.” Make it “Swap sentence 1 and 2; remove adverb.”
- 

Code: Not “debug auth.” Make it “Log request headers; confirm token presence.”
- 

Sewing: Not “assemble block.” Make it “Pin top left corner; check alignment.”
- 

Admin: Not “handle insurance.” Make it “Find policy email; star it.”

**4) Put your body on rails (micro-ritual).**  
Devotion is physical. No decisions.
- 

Sit, set a 10-minute timer, start verb, do not check anything else.
- 

When timer ends: stand, breathe, write one line in a notebook: “Stone: [verb + object]. Result: [fact]. Next: [micro-next].”

**5) Close with a visible win and a breadcrumb.**  
You want tomorrow’s you to arrive and see momentum, not chaos.
- 

Writing: Commit with message “tighten opener; cut filler.”
- 

Code: Push branch; open tiny PR with clear title.
- 

Sewing: Lay joined square on top of stack; clip a note “next: press seam.”
- 

Admin: Mark the task done and schedule the 2-minute follow-up.

**6) Protect the second stone (the rhythm, not the streak).**  
The temptation after a small win is to spray energy in all directions. Don’t. If you have another 20 minutes, do the **same thread** again. Two stones in one channel beat five stones across five valleys.

**7) Keep a “Friction Ledger.”**  
Each day, note the first resistance you felt. Don’t solve it big. Sand one edge.
- 

“Didn’t know where to start writing.” → Save one placeholder hook each night.
- 

“Git error again.” → Add the exact command that worked to a README.
- 

“Cutting takes forever.” → Pre-trim tomorrow’s two pieces before you stop.

## A concrete scene

6:03 AM. Kettle clicks. You’re not inspired. You pick up the sticky note: “Open draft. Replace opener.” Laptop wakes. You paste a two-line hook you wrote last night. Timer set to 10:00. You delete a fussy sentence, swap two lines, breathe. Timer dings. You type in your notebook: “Stone: replace opener. Result: reads cleaner. Next: cut one metaphor.” You stand, refill the mug, and—because the groove is warm—set one more 10:00 and cut the metaphor. Two stones, one channel. The mountain didn’t move. Your day did.

## When your world has five mountains

You have writing, code, a quilt, a household, and a body. You will feel torn. Here’s the rule: **one primary mountain per day, one maintenance stone for two others.** That looks like:
- 

Primary: writing (20–40 minutes, two stones).
- 

Maintenance: sewing (pin two seams), admin (send one form).
- 

Tomorrow, rotate primary to code. The others still get a single, tiny stone.

This keeps devotion wide enough to hold your life, but narrow enough to make real dents.

## For you 

Here are seven moves you can do tonight:
- 

Empty one inbox. Put every task into a simple list you actually open.
- 

Make the stone so small you’d feel silly not starting it.
- 

Block 25 minutes tomorrow for your one mountain. Guard it like a flight.
- 

Label the first snag aloud before touching the work. Then solve only that.
- 

Pick the one outcome that makes the day a win. Ignore the rest.
- 

Cut one active project. Free that energy for your core thread.
- 

Notice what your body resists first. Fix the environment, not your character.

Devotion isn’t louder than doubt. It’s just earlier, clearer, and closer to the ground.
