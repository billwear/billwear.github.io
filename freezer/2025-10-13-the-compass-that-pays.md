You want to earn a solid, reliable, waterproof living. You want to work in a way that feels right, not desperate.

But everywhere you look, someone’s shouting that you have to hustle harder; build funnels; pitch louder; find that dream job; or turn your craft into a top seller.

You don’t want to be that person. You don’t even want to *sound* like that person. You just want your work to matter — and to hold water.

The real trick isn’t selling more. It’s pointing true.

Every person has a quiet inner direction. Call it your **moral vector** — a line that runs through what you care about and what you can actually do. It’s your compass. And right now, when the ground feels soft and uncertain, you need it more than ever.

You can ignore that compass for a while. Many do. You can chase trends, pretend every gig is a brand, "know in your bones" that you'll get that promotion, convince yourself that “visibility” will turn into value someday.

But the longer you run off-course, the more you start to feel that faint drift inside you. The moments when you finish a project and still feel hollow. The uneasy way you talk about your work, as if you’re trying to sell yourself on it too.

Finding north again isn’t difficult, but it requires honesty. Look back at the things you’ve done that felt wrong, not because they failed, but because they bent you. Then look at the things that felt right, even when no one cared. Draw a line through those moments. That’s your moral vector; that’s your true direction.

### A Small Example

A friend of mine left a corporate job to design furniture full-time. At first, he copied the market: “modern minimal,” mass appeal, quick turnaround. He sold a few pieces but hated building them. They felt empty.

One day he built a solid oak table — heavy, overbuilt, the kind he grew up eating on. It took him two weeks and cost twice as much. He sold it in a day for twice what he was asking, because people could feel the care in it.

Now he only makes pieces that could survive seventy years. He’s not filthy rich, but he doesn't need anyone else to pay his bills, even when emergencies crop up. His waiting list stretches six months out. His compass points north, and his work holds water.

That’s how you earn a solid, reliable, waterproof living. You don’t fight the world. You navigate it. You build on your /own/ direction, not against it.

### For You

**1. Define north.**

Open a blank page. Answer three questions: What matters to you most in your work?  What you can do well without faking it?  What kind of result feels clean when you finish? That intersection is your vector.

**2. Audit your drift.**

Look at your last five projects or posts. For each, mark "+" if it aligned with your vector, "–" if it bent you. Patterns emerge fast.

**3. Build one waterproof offer.**

Design something you’d be proud to hand to someone you respect — a tool, a post, a product. Charge what it’s worth, even if it feels risky.

**4. Check your compass daily.**

Before opening email or social media, ask: What action today keeps me pointed north? Write it on a sticky note. That’s your true “priority list.”

**5. Use friction as feedback.**

If you feel resistance, ask whether it’s fear or misalignment. Fear passes. Misalignment festers. Learn to tell the difference.

**6. Anchor in evidence.**

When people thank you sincerely for something you made, copy their words into a “proof” file. That’s your compass confirming course when you doubt yourself.

**7. Protect the slow lane.**

Schedule one block of uninterrupted time each week for depth—no notifications, no performance. That’s where calibration happens.

This compass only works when you’re moving.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
