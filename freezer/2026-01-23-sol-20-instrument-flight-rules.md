In case you haven’t noticed by my skewed posting schedule, this week did not go as planned. Not because of distraction, but because other people needed meaningful help. Doctors’ offices. Hospital rooms. Family vigils. Elderly business owners trying to keep the lights on long enough to breathe.

I spent days carrying weight that wasn’t mine to schedule, but I was happy I could show up.

That kind of week strips away pretense, showing you what you still can do when your time is fragmented. It shows you, without mercy, what still moves forward and what does not. By the end of it, I had some real data about living through constraint. This week forced me to notice something uncomfortable and useful: when pressure is real, I default to the work that matters most — and quietly abandon the rest.

Amazing information. I have six active creative paths right now, and I like all six, but they can’t have equal leverage. Two of them can plausibly produce income in the near term — income that meaningfully improves my life beyond baseline retirement.  
The other four are slower burns: Worthy, deep, but long runs.

I can’t pretend they’re all equal *right now*, and that’s not pessimism, just honesty. Fortuitous, then, that while sitting in those doctor’s offices, waiting for the patient to get done, that I was reading Shane Parrish’s “Clear Thinking.” It’s a very good book and a slow, thoughtful read. I only got about 20% of the way through it, but he sets it up well enough that I could start using it right away.

When people get stuck, they usually aren’t lacking effort: they are trapped in one of four default systems:
- 

Emotion says, “I feel bad choosing. Everything matters.”
- 

Ego says, “I should be able to do all of this.”
- 

The crowd says, “People like me don’t do these kind of things.”
- 

Inertia says, “Keep the same allocation. Don’t disturb the structure.”

I watched all four try to take the controls this week. Emotion wanted me to treat every hobby as sacred, even when time was scarce. Ego wanted to prove I could keep everything spinning, even though compassion was the watchword of the week. The lemming instinct whispered that slowing anything meant quitting. Inertia wanted me to keep last week’s schedule anyway, simply because it existed.

None of those systems ask the right question, which is: *What does reality support right now?* Reality answered plainly, and I got enough clarity and distance to see that some of my hobbies are moonshots, and others will meaningfully engage others much sooner. So I re-allocated. Didn’t change my blogging schedule, but scaled up two hobbies and scaled down the other four. Everything’s respected, but the things with better payback get more time.

I didn’t lose a week; I gained a clearer dashboard, with steadier lights and better progress.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
