My dad was seventy when he loudly proclaimed, “I’m too old to learn new things.” 

I’ve never agreed with that sentiment. You’re never too old -- or too young -- to make a difference.

How many newborn babies give great-grandparents a new lease on life or rescue a struggling marriage?

For every teenager who’s a disappointment, there are probably five or six who are already working, helping to provide for their families while finishing high school.

So often do we hear the backstory of astronauts or generals or skilled surgeons, about growing up in poverty or managing alcoholic parents or even learning to famously make people laugh out of the sheer loneliness of privilege.

We all have good in us. We can all learn, grow, choose better, touch another’s heart. We can all promote understanding, peace, and unity. Every. Single. One of us.

## “Don’t generalize”

Like most parents -- and most *people* -- my dad’s advice ran the gamut from brilliant to utterly worthless. He could be wise, he could be mean, he could be smart and penny-pinching and literate all in the same sentence.  I used to blame him for all my failures, until I realized a couple of things.

First, he was *human. *

Second, he was unique, like every one of us who was ever born. Even twins can take very different paths, so different that their physical similarities are the only thing they have in common.

There were a couple of mantras my dad shared with me that have been helpful.  

He would often say, “Don’t generalize.”  Don’t stereotype. Don’t make assumptions based on limited evidence. Don’t jump to conclusions based on one or two facts. 

Good advice. 

Another mantra was a favorite verse of poetry:

> 

We think our fathers fools, so wise we grow. 

Our wiser sons, no doubt, will think us so.

I think Alexander Pope’s quote from *An Essay on Criticism* was intended to point out the dangers of neglecting communal wisdom. One of Pope’s contemporaries, Isaac Newton, took that to heart when he said he “stood on the shoulders of giants.”

## Building on wisdom

Living in the Global Village gives us access to the stored wisdom of the world. Granted, there are a *tremendous *number of bad ideas out there, but foolishness at scale can still be a good teacher. 

Someone watching that cat video might just become a veterinarian; someone reading the news may learn to better isolate facts from conjecture and innuendo; someone doomscrolling those fifteen-second vignettes might begin to understand more about human nature and decide to study psychology.

You can learn from anything, and you already know the question:

> 

What can we *learn *from this?

So, no, you’re never too old to learn, to care, to make a difference.

Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.
