### *** impedance matching

The idea of planning has always intrigued me.  I grew up in Rocket City, USA (Huntsville, Alabama) and learned about Von Braun and the Apollo 11 countdown that (I think) is still on the wall of a conference room there.  floor to ceiling, wall to wall greenbar with a gigantic flowchart on it, running the sequence from month to month, starting with the very first ideas and running through to the end of astronaut isolation upon their return.  All through it are built-in holds: sometimes a few minutes; more often some number of hours between 24 and 96, sometimes a couple of weeks.  These were built in so that they would have time to stop and reset.  The weird part for me is that they took the reset when came, even if nothing needed to reset.  I think I would’ve carried the time over to reset later, but it probably had something to do with the fact that not everything needed a reset in every line of the engineering PERT chart.  Anyway, I might be digressing.  The point is that planning is not automatically overthinking or procrastination, it’s what you make it and whether it helps.  For years, I used planning as a superstitious ritual (talked about yesterday) that got squarely in the way of me doing what I really needed to do.  I did so much planning and checklisting that it because my primary occupation, getting in the way of getting things done.  Glad I’ve figured that out now.

### *** idea pressure

I think that’s one reason I’m moving back to paper for my planning only.  Org-mode in Emacs is great, and has so many uses, but managing a fully-articulated agenda can take more time than the doing.  There are too many bells and whistles.  When you add the friction of doing it by hand, a lot of those marginal steps don’t have the same appeal because they’re too much work to add, track, and maintain.  In other words, there’s a certain blend of human plus computer that is more effective.  AI worries me in that way.  If we outsource too much of our reasoning and thinking to AI, we won’t grok the core issues as well, and we’ll make stupid mistakes.

### *** pivot

Pivoting on New Year’s Eve, though, means writing some meaningful resolutions for next year.  They need to be things I can actually figure out how to do, at least mostly, during next year.  Not pipe-dreams. Not self-help goals.  One of the better-respected goal books says something like write out your goals, read them every day, and you will achieve them.  No offense, but that’s pure bullshit.  Reading a list of things you’re supposed to be doing isn’t actually doing them, and there’s no guarantee you’ll get yourself out of bed and get moving on them.  You need to develop a devotion to them that goes well beyond discipline, commitment, dedication and all that.  You need to be doing them because you’d be doing them anyway.  For me, at least, if I’m not doing that, I’m not really happy with my life and I find ways to escape and avoid.  No way to live, IMHO.
