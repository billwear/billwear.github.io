You can tell gravity’s working because you feel the drag.

The pressure. The slump.

You can’t see it, but it’s always there — pulling you toward what you haven’t yet named.

Most people mistake this heaviness for failure. They think the weight means they’re stuck. 

But what if it’s the opposite? What if that weight is the proof that something solid still holds you — something that refuses to let you drift off into the endless doomscroll?

You don’t have to like gravity for it to work. You just have to stop fighting it.

### The pull before the plan

You’re feeling the pressure to “get a plan together.”

That’s not confusion — it’s the pull. Gravity doesn’t start with clarity; it starts with attraction.

It tugs on you long before you know where it’s trying to take you.

When your head’s thumping for a plan and your hands won’t move, that’s the body’s way of saying: the plan is already in motion. You just haven’t named it yet.

The trick is not to invent the plan out of panic, but to observe the pull until you see what it’s pointing to.

That’s gravity’s message: not “move faster,” but “pay attention here.”

### The Gravity Test

If you don’t know what to anchor to next, run this simple test:

1. List five things that quietly attract you. Not obligations — attractions. Could be a tool, a topic, a texture.

2. Ask which one makes time disappear. The one that erases the clock when you touch it.

3. Touch it daily for three days. Literally — hands on the material, instrument, or keyboard.

4. Watch what pulls back. If it gives energy, not just takes it, that’s your anchor.

5. Double the weight. Commit to one step that makes it harder to back out — an appointment, a draft, a cut of fabric.

That’s gravity in practice: identifying what already holds you, then giving it permission to pull harder.

When heaviness becomes lift

Without an anchor, gravity flattens you.

With an anchor, the same force gives you traction.

That’s the quiet miracle: the pressure doesn’t go away, but it starts working for you. Suddenly the weight becomes direction. The dark sky doesn’t mean storm—it means night is doing its job, covering you while the new shape forms underneath.

## For you

1. Capture one real pull. Don’t analyze it — just name what you’re drawn to today.

2. Clear your launch pad. Pick one surface or folder and make it empty.

3. Start with handwork. Type, sew, solder, draw — something that restores contact between mind and matter.

4. Measure in touches, not tasks. Count how many times you return, not how much you finish.

5. Slow your loop. When your brain spins, write down the loop’s headline once, then walk away.

6. Guard your anchor hours. One small block each day that nothing can invade.

7. End with gravity check. Ask: did today’s weight push me down — or hold me steady?
