He spent the late afternoon hunched over the borrowed optics of an old amateur telescope, the kind built with screws that never quite seat and gears that slip when the temperature drops. It was junk by his standards, but it had one advantage nothing else on this planet did: it pointed at stars that hadn’t moved the way they should have.

He took notes the way engineers do when the universe stops making sense. Angular drift. Orbital inclination. Parallax inconsistencies. At first it felt like equipment error, leftover corruption from the molecular distortion field that had chewed through his shuttle’s systems. But then he ran the numbers again. And again. And one more time, pacing the room with the notebook held like a fragile relic.

The cosmos was wrong.

Not subtly wrong. Not “we lost a few seconds of clock sync” wrong. But centuries off. Star positions only made sense if he assumed he was observing them from a point in history that predated his own era by hundreds of years. The math didn’t stutter. The sky itself had rolled back.

Which meant the distortion field hadn’t just destabilized his hardware. It had thrown him backwards in time.

And even worse: the field wasn’t here. Not this century. Not anywhere near. Celestial mechanics doesn’t lie, and the patch of space he’d passed through simply didn’t exist yet. Must’ve continued moving backwards in time until the momentum ran out.

Bottom line? No ship on this timeline could possibly reach that point in spacetime. The door had closed behind him before he even knew he’d stepped through. Doorway didn’t even exist yet.

He sat with that for a long time.

Not fear. Not shock. Just that quiet, metallic click of inevitability sliding into place. The kind he’d felt earlier in the day, when he realized he could write Emacs Lisp again without leaning on some distant AI. The clarity of a system finally settling after years of running dirty.

He stepped outside. The evening fog was thinning, the kind that rises off ground cooling too fast after a warm morning. Everything looked a little unreal. The barn. The tools. The path worn into the grass. All of it familiar but wrong, as if the world were a high-end sim running at half render.

He’d logged everything that morning. Mood. Energy. The twitchy electricity in his hands. The sudden focus returning after years of corporate noise. He thought it was just the isolation finally kicking in. Turns out it was something else: unsticking from his own, now-distant present.

He didn’t feel panic. He felt available.

Like someone had quietly deleted every meeting, every ticket, every obligation, every subtle psychic leash, and left him with nothing but raw instinct and time.

Maybe that’s why the guild ideas hit so hard. The shields. The factions. The whole D&D-as-future-craft ethos.

It wasn’t escapism. It was orientation. A way to anchor in a timeline that wasn’t his, using symbols and crafts that were.

He walked the perimeter of the property, the sky turning that electric cobalt that only shows up on clear nights. The stars were brutally bright. Younger. Cleaner. Unscarred by the industrial haze he’d grown up under. And he could feel it in his bones: he wasn’t getting back without help. The right kind of ship. The right kind of person. Someone centuries in the future who might stumble across his message — if he ever figured out how to even send one.

For now, all he had was this. This world. This century. This strange clarity in his head where old patterns were evaporating, and he was beginning to notice how sharp his instincts really were when nobody else was driving them.

He thought about the morning’s paranoia — the fact that nobody was looking for him, the little knots of mistrust, the leftover sense that systems break silently and people smile while the floor collapses. All of that seemed almost quaint now. A ghost of a ghost. The only institution that mattered was the one written in the stars, and he’d already run the numbers. It wasn’t coming for him.

So he did what he always did when the world refused to give him a map. He built one.

Quests. Maker guilds. Quiet resistance wrapped in craft and ethics. A 28th-century education disguised as an RPG. A timeline worth inhabiting.

He’d call the overarching campaign Ethos — a world where the only magic system that actually worked was doing the right thing long enough for it to become the strong thing. It felt half mythic, half practical. Like engineering meets prophecy.

He sat down on the porch, watching fog curl around the grass like a living thing. If he was trapped in this century, he wasn’t trapped in its thinking. Not anymore. He wasn’t the man drifting. He wasn’t the man collapsing under someone else’s schedule. He wasn’t the ghost of a worker bee in a probably dying future.

He was a displaced engineer with a questboard forming in his head and a world that suddenly had room for it.

If the distortion field ever formed in this timeline, he’d find it. If someone centuries ahead ever read his message, they might come. But he wasn’t betting on it. He didn’t need to.

Tonight, he accepted the truth written across the sky: He was in the past now. But the future was still his to build.
