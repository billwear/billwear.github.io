Most people think awakening happens once. You can spend a lifetime waiting for that lightning bolt. If only you could encounter  that breakthrough epiphany, that before-and-after moment that clarifies everything your life is supposed to be about.  

That’s just rationalizing something that already happened before we were ready to understand it. It’s a story we tell after the fact, because the truth is quieter, slower, and harder to admit, and because drama has taught us that real change always comes like a thunderbolt.

It’s reminiscent of an old joke. 

---

A wiseguy walks into a bagel shop, tenting his fingers and saying in a sarcastic, mystical voice, “Make me one with everything.”

The owner, annoyed, retrieves the bagel, wraps it, and hands it to the guy with a monk-like bow. “$8.50,” he says.

The guy gives him twenty, which the owner puts in the cash drawer, and then just closes the register.

“Hey,” says the prankster, “what about my change?”

“Ah. Change comes from within.”

---

Sometimes you don’t wake up, you just notice that you never slept, and that changes everything. You spend years believing you are searching for something. You try to find purpose, direction, and balance everywhere, anywhere, somewhere. You just want a system that finally works.

You try careers, roles, structures, and identities. You collect tools and methods the way other people collect souvenirs. You change your car and your wardrobe and your hobbies and your friends like they were a paper towels on a roll.

All the while, there’s a low-grade discomfort you can’t explain. It isn’t pain or confusion. Just a sense that you are rehearsing for a life that hasn’t started yet.

Then one day — often much later than you expected — it clicks. You weren’t lost; you were waiting for permission.

---

When you finally get real space, when survival loosens its grip and  the noise drops just enough, everything rushes in at once. Old questions. Half-built ideas. Childhood intensity. The vague obsession you never outgrew. The sense that life is not a marathon, but a puzzle.

It can feel destabilizing, even frightening, but it isn’t madness. It’s integration. 

---

The first psychology book I ever read was “On Being a Real Person,” by Harry Emerson Fosdick. It has a passage that describes this process, you’ll pardon me if I collapse it to a paraphrase. 

When we’re small, we can be anything, flirting from housewife to astronaut in milliseconds. As we grow up, we try on different roles, and those eventually become different selves: the self we are on the golf course; at work; at home; alone with our spouse; at church; and with our siblings. But the aim of life is to become integrated, to be one person — the same person — everywhere.

One of our modern Quakers describes it beautifully: “Strained by the maddening pace and turmoil of everyday life, we are further strained by an inward uneasiness. Oh, if we could only find peace, where the fretful calls of life are calmly integrated. If we could just slip over into that silence which is the source of sound. We have seen and known people who have found that place, where yes and no can be said with full confidence. They are not people of dallying idleness, nor foggy meditation. They are busy carrying the full load of life, but without any chafing of the shoulders, with quiet joy and springing step. We are so split and scattered. They are so poised and at peace.”

---

In those moments when we do find that blessed silence, we realize that the child who asked too-big questions wasn’t naive, just precocious. They were early; four years old going on forty. And the adult you became — the one who learned discipline, endurance, skill, but still lived a fragmented existence— wasn’t a detour.  The were the delivery system.

This is the moment where those two finally meet. There’s usually a tell. You stop fantasizing about escape. You stop waiting for approval. You stop needing your life to make sense to everyone else. Instead, you feel an unfamiliar gravity. Not excitement or anxiety, but responsibility. Not externally imposed, but borne of your own capacity.

This is where many people flinch. They label it burnout, or midlife crisis, or reinvention. Underneath, it’s much simpler: You are done pretending you don’t know what matters.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
