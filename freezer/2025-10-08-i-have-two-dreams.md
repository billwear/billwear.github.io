When we talk about productivity and success, the acronym DREAM can represent two very different philosophies. One is an internally guided approach based on tuning into one’s cognitive state and personal rhythms, captured by Devotion, Resonance, Energy, Awareness, and Mastery. The other is an externally imposed ideal of constant output – Discipline, Readiness, Enthusiasm, Action-orientation, and Mediocrity (as in “good enough is fine”).

These two dreams stand in stark contrast. One emphasizes personal autonomy, flow states, and doing the right work at the right time; the other emphasizes always being “on,” doing what’s required immediately (even if halfheartedly), and valuing quantity or speed over quality.

I am not a psychologist, so take this post in that light, but I’d like to peek into the psychological and scientific basis for each approach, examining how cognitive states, motivation, and work culture impact both personal fulfillment and productivity. I’d also like to consider who truly benefits from each work style – the individual or the system – and why deferring one’s own dreams in favor of duty can lead to long-term regret.

### The “DREAM” of cognitive states  
Devotion, Resonance, Energy, Awareness, Mastery

The first interpretation of DREAM highlights five distinct mental states or moods that one can leverage for more effective work: Devotion, Resonance, Energy, Awareness, and Mastery. Instead of expecting ourselves to be equally productive at all times, this model recognizes that our capacities fluctuate throughout the day and over time. In practice, it means tuning into your momentary capabilities – identifying which “mode” you are in – and aligning your tasks accordingly.Devotion (pull vs. push)

This state is about being drawn to a task out of interest or love, rather than forcing yourself through sheer discipline. Psychologically, this maps to intrinsic motivation – doing work because it matters to you, not because someone is cracking the whip. Research shows that intrinsic motivation is tightly linked to feelings of autonomy and produces more creative, committed effort[1][2].

In fact, external pressures like rewards or fear of punishment can undermine creativity and peak performance on all but the simplest tasks[1]. Devotion, by tapping into genuine interest, can help you enter a productive “zone” without as much willpower, since you feel a pull toward the work instead of needing to push. This doesn’t mean abandoning all discipline, but rather that discipline becomes easier when fueled by passion rather than obligation.Resonance (deep creative work)

Resonance refers to a state of creative flow – when you are deeply engaged in thinking, creating, or problem-solving and ideas are “in tune.” In positive psychology, flow is defined as “a state of deep engagement & enjoyment in activities,” where challenge matches skill and one loses track of time[3][4]. Experiencing flow not only makes work more enjoyable, it also boosts productivity and satisfaction by aligning the challenge of a task with one’s personal skills[3].

It’s exactly in these resonant moments that our best ideas and highest-quality output often emerge. However, such creative deep work usually requires uninterrupted focus and the right mental space, which we cannot summon on command at any arbitrary moment. Neuroscience findings show that when we are in a focused flow state, certain brain networks (the “task-positive” network for concentration) actually suppress the “default mode” network used for idle mind-wandering and self-reflection[5].

In other words, our brain has different modes: we can’t be brainstorming freely and analyzing meticulously at the same time. This supports the notion that states like Resonance (creative immersion) are distinct from other states and not always accessible – you might need to feel mentally fresh or inspired to enter it. Recognizing when you’re capable of deep creative thinking (and when you’re not) is key to producing quality work.Energy (physical and mental vigor)

Sometimes you find yourself in a high-energy state – mentally sharp and/or physically energized – which is ideal for tackling tough tasks that require endurance, intense focus, or willpower. Other times, you hit a lull. Far from flowing “smoothly” all day, human energy fluctuates in cycles.

Research on ultradian rhythms by sleep scientist Nathaniel Kleitman revealed that we naturally move through ~90-minute cycles of high alertness followed by ~20-minute periods of fatigue or lower energy[6][7]. This is often called the “basic rest-activity cycle”, and it means that even on a good day, our concentration and stamina will dip after about 1.5 hours of continuous work[8][9]. We can override these fatigue signals temporarily – for example, with caffeine or sheer willpower – but doing so triggers a stress response (fight-or-flight hormones) that exacts a cost in exhaustion or burnout later[10].

In short, we are not machines; we can’t run at high output continuously. This DREAM model acknowledges that when you have high energy, you should capitalize on it (do the hard things that require it), and when you’re in a low-energy trough, it may be more effective to rest or do lighter tasks rather than force productivity. Over time, working with your energy cycles instead of against them leads to greater long-term output and well-being[9][10].

Our alertness and cognitive ability naturally rise and fall in waves throughout the day. During a peak, one can achieve “top performance,” but pushing straight through the subsequent low period induces stress and diminishing returns. Strategic breaks and task-switching in line with these cycles allow sustainable productivity[7][9].Awareness (mindful observation)

This state is more reflective and observant – you might not be doing a lot externally, but your mind is open, noticing, and processing. It’s akin to mindfulness or a learning mode: observing without immediate judgment. In an awareness phase, you might absorb information (reading, watching, listening) or gain insights by letting your mind wander and make connections.

Psychologically, giving ourselves permission to sometimes step back and just observe or explore can spur creativity. Studies have found that periods of apparent “inactivity” – like being bored or daydreaming – are actually crucial for creativity and problem-solving because they allow the brain’s default mode network to incubate ideas[11][12]. The modern tendency to fill every spare moment with emails, tasks, or scrolling means we “have no time to sit still and just think,” depriving us of the fertile mental downtime that can lead to creative breakthroughs[11][12].

The Awareness state, then, is far from wasted time; it’s a necessary part of the creative cycle and personal growth (provided it doesn’t slide into chronic procrastination). By recognizing when you’re in a more contemplative mood, you can allow yourself to research, observe, and learn – sowing seeds that will later germinate during Resonance or Mastery.Mastery (skilled performance):

Mastery is the state of high skill and smooth execution. It’s when you can perform a task almost effortlessly due to extensive practice and expertise – think of a musician playing a familiar piece flawlessly or a coder writing efficient code from long experience. This is the zone of optimal performance where you move gracefully through work that you have mastered, often achieving excellent results.

Reaching mastery in any domain requires time and focused practice, which again can’t be rushed on a whim. Importantly, being in a Mastery state might require having gone through the other states first (you needed Resonance to develop the creative idea, Energy to put in the hard work, Awareness to learn from feedback, and Devotion to keep improving).

The DREAM framework’s emphasis on Mastery highlights quality: doing things well, not just quickly. It runs counter to the idea of “just get it done good enough.” Instead, it’s about timing your work so that when you do act, you can act with excellence and skill.

This aligns with research on intrinsic motivators: one of the pillars of motivation is the desire for Mastery – the urge to improve and excel at something that matters[13]. Environments that support mastery (through feedback, practice, and autonomy to refine one’s technique) tend to produce much higher engagement and expertise than those that push for quick, mediocre outputs[1][13].Mutual exclusivity of states

These five “modes” tend to be distinct and often mutually exclusive – a fact both intuitive and supported by cognitive science. You can likely recall moments when you were bursting with physical energy but not in a creative mood, or intellectually inspired but low in stamina, or diligently practicing skills but not in a daydreaming mindset.

Our brains and bodies shift gears; we can’t be in all modes at once. Neuroscientists have observed, for example, that the brain’s networks for focused attention vs. relaxed introspection work in a kind of push-pull dynamic – when one is active, the other quiets down[5]. This means certain capacities are heightened at the expense of others at any given time.

The DREAM states model implicitly encourages recognizing these trade-offs. Rather than expecting yourself to instantly do anything at 100% (the way an on-demand work culture might expect), you acknowledge that, say, today your mind is in deep-thinking Resonance mode but not in a detail-oriented Execution mode, so you honor that. By doing so, you set yourself up to produce work of much higher quality in the long run – you’re using the right tool (mental state) for the job.

Indeed, the concept of “fit” between task and mental state is crucial for achieving flow and peak performance[3]. When you harness Devotion, Resonance, Energy, Awareness, and Mastery at the appropriate times, you maximize both your output and your growth. You’re also practicing personal autonomy: you’re choosing how to work based on self-awareness, which is empowering in and of itself.Personal autonomy, intrinsic motivation, and flow

Central to this first DREAM paradigm is the idea of personal autonomy – having the freedom to organize your work according to your own rhythms and motivations. This approach is fundamentally person-centered: it trusts that an individual will be more productive, creative, and fulfilled when they have control over how and when they work.

Psychology research strongly backs this up. Giving people autonomy in their work (decision-making power over tasks, timing, or methods) consistently shows positive effects on productivity, job satisfaction, and well-being[14][15]. When you feel you can do things “my way,” you’re more likely to take ownership and pour your energy into the work.

For example, multiple studies have found that autonomy support increases intrinsic motivation, leading employees to willingly put in effort and even be more innovative[2]. Workers with higher autonomy also report lower stress – being micromanaged on not just what to do but how and when to do it is inherently stressful and demoralizing[15].

This autonomy-driven model aligns with what author Daniel Pink (drawing on self-determination theory) identified as key ingredients for high motivation: Autonomy, Mastery, and Purpose[13]. People are most engaged and persistent in their work when they feel they have freedom in how they do it, a chance to get better at it, and a sense that it’s meaningful. Notably, two of those – autonomy and mastery – are explicitly present in our DREAM-of-cognitive-states model.

We’ve already discussed mastery; autonomy is the thread running through the entire approach. By deciding to work on a creative project when you’re feeling Resonant, or choosing to rest when your Energy is low so you can recharge for later, you are exercising autonomy at a granular level. You’re not just doing work, you’re managing how you work based on inner signals rather than external pressures.

From a flow state perspective, autonomy is practically a prerequisite. To achieve flow, one often needs the latitude to set clear goals, immerse fully in the task, and minimize distractions[3][16] – conditions that are hard to come by if someone else dictates your every move or interrupts you constantly.

In a supportive environment, a person can organize their day to have, say, a protected 2-hour block in the morning when they tend to reach a creative flow (Resonance), followed by a break (Awareness or rest), then an hour of energized administrative work (Energy state), etc. This self-directed orchestration leads to far better results than a one-size-fits-all, rigid schedule[9][10].

In fact, an experiment on workplace autonomy found that when groups were allowed to self-manage timing and process, their productivity significantly increased, with improvements seen for both individuals and teams[14][15]. Workers who have control over where and when they work – even something as simple as choosing their workspace – show greater satisfaction and often performance[17]. All of this reinforces that honoring these “DREAM” states isn’t just touchy-feely advice; it has practical, measurable benefits.

It’s also worth noting that intrinsic motivation and creativity go hand in hand. When you are devoted (passionate) and working in flow, you’re more likely to take risks, explore novel ideas, and achieve original outcomes. Extrinsically forced work, by contrast, can lead to narrow thinking – “just tell me what to do and I’ll do the minimum”. A review in the Harvard Business Review famously warned managers: “Creativity gets killed much more often than it gets supported” by organizational practices that emphasize control and immediate results over exploration[18][19].

In the cognitive-state approach, you support creativity by giving yourself the mental space and the choice to follow your “music” when it’s playing. Devotion replaces pure discipline; you work with enthusiasm and interest (when available) rather than forcing enthusiasm 24/7. Resonance and Awareness legitimize the indirect but crucial aspects of creativity – daydreaming, pondering, observing – which yield insights that later become innovations. Energy and Mastery ensure you seize moments of strength to produce exceptional work, rather than grind out mediocre work when you’re depleted.

In short, this version of DREAM is individual-centric and long-term optimized: its beneficiary is primarily the individual’s growth, fulfillment, and excellence, which in a broader sense can also benefit employers or society through higher-quality contributions. But it does require a culture or personal discipline that respects timing – doing the right thing at the right time, rather than everything all the time. It acknowledges a reality that modern productivity gurus often stress: you can do anything, but not everything (and certainly not everything at once).

The science of productivity increasingly highlights working smarter, not just harder. As one productivity author put it, working in concert with your body’s natural rhythms unlocks “reservoirs of productivity” you didn’t know you had[20], whereas fighting those rhythms leads to burnout. This DREAM model is essentially about working smarter by respecting your cognitive states, thereby achieving Mastery and creative Resonance when conditions are right.

### The world’s “DREAM”:   
Discipline, Readiness, Enthusiasm, Action, Mediocrity

Against this backdrop of personal attenuation stands the more conventional interpretation of DREAM – a set of values often implicitly demanded by workplaces, schools, and society at large. In this paradigm, Discipline, Readiness, Enthusiasm, Action-Orientation, and Mediocrity (“good enough”) are prized. Let’s break down what this means and why it’s problematic when taken to the extreme.Discipline

In the traditional sense, discipline means doing what is required when it’s required, regardless of how you feel. It’s the “just do it” mentality – show up every day, grind through tasks, stick to the plan. Discipline is not inherently bad; in fact, it’s often necessary to achieve long-term goals. However, in this context it implies a push approach (as opposed to Devotion’s pull). It suggests forcing yourself into tasks on schedule even if your mind or body is resisting.

The world expects you to be disciplined so that its needs are met – your boss needs that report by 5, your client expects you to be available, your teacher demands homework by the deadline. High discipline is often glorified as the key to success, but the dark side is that it can lead to ignoring important internal signals.

If you are utterly exhausted or creatively blank one day but “with discipline” you make yourself do the complex task anyway, what is the quality of the result? Research indicates it will likely be subpar, and you might be draining your ability to perform in the following days[9][10]. Nonetheless, many environments insist on constant discipline – sometimes equating it with moral virtue (“hard worker”) – without regard for sustainability.Readiness

This is the expectation that you should always be ready to jump on any task or switch gears at a moment’s notice. It’s the ethos of being on-call mentally: always available, never saying “I can’t right now.” In a corporate setting, this might translate to answering emails immediately, taking on sudden requests, or multitasking across numerous projects. It’s basically an assumption that you are a consistently functioning, high-output unit with an on-switch that never goes off.

While it sounds like a recipe for productivity, constant readiness actually clashes with how our brains work best. Being perpetually ready often means chronic partial attention – you’re never fully immersed in one thing because you must keep an eye out for the next demand. This fragmented mode reduces deep work capacity and can increase stress.

Psychologists have observed that the “always-on” work culture (enabled by smartphones and remote access) is correlated with declines in creativity and innovation, partly because people no longer experience uninterrupted downtime or sustained focus[21][19]. Always being ready for the next task means you rarely devote full attention to the current one, nor do you allow your mind to recharge. It treats human cognition like a light switch (on/off) rather than a muscle that needs recovery. Over time, cognitive fatigue sets in, and both productivity and creativity suffer.Enthusiasm (on command)

The world often expects not just compliance, but cheerful compliance. Enthusiasm here means you should display a positive, can-do attitude toward any assignment, immediately. It’s the “smile and get to work” mandate.

Of course, genuine enthusiasm is wonderful – it’s essentially what we called Devotion in the first model. The difference is, society’s expected Enthusiasm is performative and constant, not necessarily genuine or situational. It doesn’t care if you actually feel enthusiastic; you just must act eager and avoid hesitation or critique.

This expectation can create a dissonance and emotional strain. For one, nobody is enthusiastic about everything all the time – we each have preferences. Forcing an appearance of enthusiasm (“Yes, absolutely, I’ll do that right now!”) when it’s not authentic can lead to burnout and cynicism. Psychologically, suppressing one’s true feelings and forcing a smile (termed “emotional labor” in organizational psychology) is associated with increased stress and even burnout in service jobs.

In knowledge work, always feigning enthusiasm for every trivial task can similarly erode one’s intrinsic motivation. It sends a message that your natural interests don’t matter – only your obedient participation does. Over time, people might lose touch with what genuinely excites them, making work feel hollow. The enthusiasm-on-demand ethos benefits those asking for the work (who wouldn’t want a team that says yes to everything cheerfully?) but can be dehumanizing for the individual, reducing them to a “positive productivity machine” rather than a person with ebbs, flows, and opinions.Action-orientation

This value dictates “Don’t overthink it, just do it.” It prizes swift execution and a bias for action in all situations. On the surface, this counters procrastination and analysis-paralysis, which is good. But when overemphasized, it can discourage thoughtful reflection, creativity, and learning – all of which sometimes require inaction or slower action.

If every moment’s mantra is “go, go, go,” then activities like brainstorming, researching, or simply pondering (Awareness state) can be seen as unproductive or indulgent. The result is that people may default to safe, shallow work that checks boxes (because it’s actionable quickly) rather than deeper, innovative work which might initially look like not much is happening.

Moreover, an extreme action-orientation doesn’t distinguish urgent vs. important. It may lead to tackling a lot of minor tasks (so you feel busy and active) at the expense of strategic work that requires patience and thought.

This is reminiscent of the difference between “deep work” and “shallow work,” as Cal Newport describes – shallow tasks (emails, minor to-dos) are easy to act on and give a sense of accomplishment, but real progress often comes from slower, more cognitively demanding deep work that our action-addicted culture often interrupts.

Constant action-orientation also ties to the “hustle culture” phenomenon: filling every minute with work, glorifying being busy. As noted, filling every waking moment with productive activity, without breaks, backfires by choking off creativity and increasing stress[19][12]. A mentality of perpetual action leaves no room for the mind’s essential recovery and incubation processes.Mediocrity (good enough is good enough)

It may sound odd to say the world expects mediocrity, but this refers to the idea behind the phrase “the quick gentleman’s B is better than a scholar’s A.” In other words, delivering something adequate right now is valued more than delivering something excellent later.

There is truth in the notion that “done is better than perfect” in many practical situations – you don’t always need an A+ effort when a B will suffice. However, elevating this to a guiding principle can create a culture of mediocrity where speed and quantity are rewarded over quality.

In work contexts, employees learn that cranking out many passable reports (even if each is mediocre) gains more praise than spending extra time to craft a brilliant report that exceeds expectations. Over time, this can stifle mastery and innovation: if you’re never allowed to strive for A-level quality (Mastery), you won’t develop peak skills, and truly breakthrough ideas (which often require more time, iteration, and yes, occasional “overthinking”) won’t emerge.

The “good enough” syndrome mainly serves short-term metrics – e.g. number of tasks closed, immediate output – which benefit managers or clients who want quick turnaround, which in turn, may increase this quarter’s profits, which looks good in the short term.  But it may not serve the individual’s growth nor even the long-term interests of the organization, which might miss out on truly excellent work. And inevitably, “thirty percent increase in profits per quarter from now on” is mathematically unsustainable.

In education, this is like praising students who reliably turn in average work on time over a student who, say, takes a risk on a creative project and hands in something late but brilliant. If mediocrity is institutionalized, people internalize that taking extra care or pursuing mastery isn’t worth it. They become comfortable with a plateau in skill.

Indeed, some organizations inadvertently create “cultures of mediocrity” by not allowing employees the autonomy or time to pursue excellence, instead inundating them with tasks and deadlines that only encourage doing the bare minimum[22][23]. In such cultures, high performers either conform and dial back their effort (since it’s not rewarded), or they burn out trying to do A+ work on a clock that only allows for B work, or they leave.Who really benefits from the world’s dream?

At first glance, the always-disciplined, always-ready worker seems like the ideal employee or student – highly reliable and obedient. But one must ask, who is this pattern really serving? Is it the person doing the disciplined thing, or the person who asked for it? The answer can be complex.  This paradigm usually benefits the requester to a greater extent than the individual, but the real beneficiary is the system itself.

When you drop everything to be ready and action-oriented for your employer, the employer gets their need met immediately, while you sacrifice your flow or the rhythm of your day. The employer then tries to translate that quick, second-class work into quick profits, which are then funneled into shareholder value, which fuels the vagaries of the stock market.  The honest answer is probably that no one individual at any level in this pyramid actually benefits greatly from this practice, but the system seems more stable to the naked eye.

If you maintain discipline to do tasks exactly when and how they demand, the organization gains efficiency, but you might be foregoing a better way you could have tackled the task or a time when you’d do it faster or more creatively.  Advances like the automobile, radio, the telephone, and the iPhone did not experience this kind of linear, on-demand development.  

In many traditional workplaces, the organization’s needs explicitly take precedence over individual preferences or strengths[23]. Employees are often expected to conform to set procedures and schedules (e.g. 9–5, or specific methodologies) even if it doesn’t align with their personal best practices or creative peaks. A vivid example comes from high-conformity jobs like policing, where “the push for conformity is powerful” and “the organization’s needs take precedence over your individual preferences and strengths”[24].

While there may be good reasons for that in certain fields, in general knowledge work this can be a recipe for wasted human potential. People end up feeling like interchangeable “cogs in a machine” when how they work is micromanaged and their own rhythms are ignored[22]. They are valued for compliance rather than creativity.

This certainly makes life easier for managers (who get predictability) and for the “askers” (who get what they want, when they want it), but it can leave the doers psychologically disengaged.

The beneficiaries of hustle culture are often the companies that get overtime out of employees or the culture at large that reaps economic output, whereas the individual may suffer mental and physical health consequences.

It’s telling that modern discussions on workplace well-being emphasize that constant busyness and availability are not truly productive in the long run – they lead to errors, burnout, and turnover. Yet, in the short run, a person who sacrifices their own pacing for the boss’s immediate need does make the boss’s life easier.

The trade-off is usually quantity over quality: e.g., an engineer pumping out code quickly (but with bugs) vs. carefully designing a robust solution; the former helps management check a box this sprint, the latter saves a lot of headache later but requires patience. If managers are only rewarded on short-term deliverables, they’ll implicitly encourage the quick B-grade work.

This highlights a misalignment of incentives: the person doing the work might actually care about doing it well (for pride or long-term maintenance reasons), but they yield to the pressure to just get it done, because that’s what is praised or expected. Thus, the “Mediocrity” in this DREAM is a systemic issue – the individual’s pursuit of mastery is subdued in order to meet someone else’s deadline or criteria, which are also B-grade goals based on the level above, and all the levels up to the top participate in this same “just get it done” mentality.  Over time, everyone may internalize that habit and stop pushing for excellence altogether, meeting the external expectations but losing their personal drive.

The real beneficiary of the always-disciplined approach is simply the avoidance of conflict or inconvenience. A disciplined person who never says “no” or “not now” makes things smoother for their team or family in the moment. For example, an always-ready parent might volunteer for every school activity (benefiting the school and kids short-term) at the expense of personal downtime that could have made them more relaxed and happy long-term. Or a student who always does what the teacher asks, exactly when asked, makes the teacher’s job easier (no extensions or special accommodations needed), but maybe that student never explores their own curiosity beyond the syllabus.

In essence, the world’s DREAM acronym fosters a kind of compliant efficiency – which has its place in keeping systems running, but often at the cost of individual creativity, autonomy, and ultimately satisfaction.  And worse, it’s based on self-deception, which is the most damaging kind of untruth.Cognitive resignation and deferred dreams:  
The cost of ignoring agency

One of the most insidious effects of living by the “world expects” model of DREAM is what might be called cognitive resignation. This is the mental state of giving up your agency over how you think and work, surrendering to the idea that you must fit into an externally imposed mold. You resign yourself to the routine: “It doesn’t matter if I’m feeling reflective or creative right now; I have to answer these emails and crank out this report because that’s what’s required.”

You start to ignore your own internal signals – fatigue, inspiration, boredom, curiosity – and just run the program that others set for you. People often justify this by saying, “I’ll get through this now, and later I’ll have the freedom to do what I really want.” This is the long-term hope that one day the resignation will be relieved – the classic “defer your life until retirement” or “sacrifice now for rewards later” mentality.

On a cognitive level, when you constantly override your natural states, you may become numb to them. For instance, you might stop noticing that you do your best thinking late at night or that you crash every afternoon, because you’ve trained yourself to plow through regardless. You essentially put your self-awareness on mute.

In psychology, this might be seen as a form of learned helplessness or at least learned suppression: you learn that your preferences don’t matter, so you stop having preferences. You just say “Yes, boss” or follow the expected acronym: be disciplined (even if it’s 3 AM and you’re brain-dead), be ready (even if you haven’t had time to think), act enthusiastic (even if you’re not), take action (even if it’s going in circles), and accept mediocrity (because there’s no time for better). This can lead to a hollowed-out feeling – you’re productive in a surface sense, but internally unfulfilled and disengaged.

Crucially, this state often comes with the promise of future relief: “If I do this for 10 years, I can retire and then pursue painting” or “Once I get this promotion, I’ll have more freedom to innovate”. Sometimes that future never comes (or when it comes, one might find they’ve lost the spark or the habit of following their own interests).

Studies on regret and life satisfaction provide a stark warning here: people overwhelmingly regret the things they didn’t do more than the things they did, especially when it comes to not living true to themselves.

In a set of studies by psychologists at Cornell University and the New School for Social Research, participants reported that their biggest life regrets centered on failures to fulfill their ideal self – i.e. not pursuing their dreams, talents, or passions – rather than failures to meet their “ought self” obligations to others[25][26].

When asked to list regrets, 72% were about not living up to their ideal self (the “woulda/coulda” paths not taken), versus only 28% about not meeting others’ expectations[25]. And fully 76% of participants said their single biggest regret involved not fulfilling their ideal self, far outnumbering those whose biggest regret was letting someone else down[26]. The message is loud: at the end of the day, you are more likely to lose sleep over the life you didn’t live for yourself than the duties you failed to perform[27][26].

This echoes the famous finding from palliative care nurse Bronnie Ware, who recorded the top regrets of people on their deathbeds. The #1 regret was “I wish I’d had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me.”[28]. The disciplined, always-obedient lifestyle is essentially living the life others expect of you. It’s telling that another of Ware’s top five regrets was “I wish I hadn’t worked so hard”, voiced especially by men who missed out on family and personal time[29]. These regrets underline how easy it is to fall into the trap of deferring one’s own dreams – working relentlessly in a mode of resignation, hoping that someday permission to be yourself will arrive. But for many, that permission comes too late or not at all.

Psychologically, deferring agency can lead to a sense of meaninglessness or depression over time. Humanistic psychologists like Viktor Frankl have noted that having a sense of meaning and purpose (which comes from within, not from external validation alone) is crucial to well-being. If you spend years not exercising your agency – not following “your music” – you might achieve external success yet feel internally empty or experience a midlife crisis. Many people in midlife suddenly realize they’ve climbed a ladder that was placed on the wrong wall, so to speak – they were disciplined and ready and did all the right things, but they’re not where they truly want to be.

There’s even a term in career literature: “dream job regret.” Surveys have shown a large percentage of workers regret not pursuing their dream career or childhood passion. For example, a ResumeLab survey found 72% of workers who haven’t achieved their dream job regret it, and 67% still hold onto that dream they had as a child[30]. Another poll reported the most common career regret was not pursuing one’s passion, cited by 28% of people – more than those who regretted not making more money or not getting promotions[31]. This paints a consistent picture: failing to claim your personal agency early in life can lead to profound regret later. People often think it’s safer to go with the flow of what’s expected – be disciplined, get the “good enough” job – but in reality, the emotional cost of abandoning one’s “music” is high.

Furthermore, operating in a state of cognitive resignation can dull the very skills you’d need to follow your dreams. Creativity, for instance, is like a muscle – if you never use it because you’re always following strict instructions, it atrophies. Self-directed people develop more self-efficacy – the confidence that they can influence outcomes – whereas overly controlled people may develop a dependency on directions. In a sense, always living by the second DREAM acronym can create a kind of self-imposed limitation where, even if given a chance later to “do your thing,” you might hesitate because you’ve long lost practice in guiding yourself.

It’s important to mention that resistance comes into play on both sides of this equation. In the context of deferring dreams, resistance might manifest as the fear of breaking away from the disciplined path. Steven Pressfield, in The War of Art, describes “Resistance” as a universal force that opposes creative endeavors – it’s the voice of fear, procrastination, distraction that stops you from doing what you truly long to do[32][33]. A person stuck in a corporate job might feel Resistance whenever they consider taking a sabbatical to write a novel (the mind throws up fears of failure, or rationalizations to stay the course). Paradoxically, the more meaningful a calling is to someone, the more Resistance they might feel, Pressfield argues, because the stakes feel so high[33].

Overcoming that Resistance requires reclaiming agency – essentially saying, “I will choose my own project and stick to it,” which is akin to switching from the externally dictated DREAM to the internal one. On the flip side, even within the personal DREAM approach, one can face resistance in the form of internal inertia or anxiety. For instance, just because you know you’re in a creative Resonance mood doesn’t mean you won’t procrastinate on starting the blank canvas – that’s Resistance too, in Pressfield’s sense, trying to keep you in your comfort zone[34][33].

This is where some discipline (of a different kind) is still useful: not discipline to do arbitrary duties, but discipline in service of your own Devotion. It’s the discipline of a craftsman who shows up each day to practice their art even if they don’t “feel” like it at that moment – but crucially, they have chosen this art themselves. Pressfield calls this being a “professional” in your creative life – treating your passion with the seriousness and consistency it deserves to overcome resistance.

So, a nuanced point emerges: The goal is not to abandon discipline entirely, but to apply discipline strategically to what truly matters to you (your music) rather than letting discipline be a tool others use to commandeer your time and mind. When you claim your agency, you might still work very hard – perhaps harder than ever – but it will be a labor of love (Devotion) and purpose, not a forced march. And all the science about flow, motivation, and performance suggests that this kind of self-directed hard work is both more satisfying and more effective. For example, people immersed in purposeful, self-chosen work often enter flow and lose track of time, even working longer hours but feeling energized rather than drained[35][4]. In contrast, working hard on someone else’s command with no personal meaning often feels exhausting and leaves one watching the clock.

### Finding a better balance:   
Autonomy over obedience

The stark contrast between these two DREAM frameworks doesn’t mean it’s an all-or-nothing choice. In reality, thriving in work and life requires a balance – some degree of discipline and reliability and a strong dose of autonomy and alignment to self. The analysis above suggests that the pendulum in modern culture has swung too far toward the “externally dictated” model, to the detriment of individuals’ creativity and well-being. Correcting that means bringing personal agency and cognitive state awareness back into the equation.

Here are a few takeaways and practical implications from this analysis:Respect your rhythms

Acknowledge that your mental and physical energy fluctuates. Whenever possible, schedule important work for when you’re in the right state. Do creative or deep work when you have Resonance and high Energy; do routine tasks when you’re in a lull or simply in execution mode. Take breaks when you hit a natural low – this isn’t laziness, it’s optimizing for the next peak[9][10]. Companies and individuals should recognize that an 8-hour workday of constant output is a myth – those who build in breaks and vary their work types actually produce more in less time (as studies on ultradian cycles and elite performers have shown, where working in intense bursts with rest yields top results[36][37]).Fight for autonomy

If you find yourself in a rigid environment, look for small ways to increase your autonomy. This could mean setting boundaries around certain hours for deep work (e.g. turning off email for a focus block), negotiating flexible hours, or choosing the method by which you deliver results. Managers should note that granting employees autonomy can boost not only happiness but output[14][15]. An autonomy-supportive climate leads to higher creativity and intrinsic motivation[2][1]. On an individual level, autonomy is also an attitude – taking ownership of your tasks rather than waiting to be told every step. Even within constraints, try to infuse your personal style or improvements (echoing that Devotion/passion). This way, you don’t feel like a cog; you feel like a creator, which sustains motivation[22].Use discipline wisely

Discipline is a tool – make sure you are wielding it, not others wielding it on you. Apply discipline to overcome Resistance in pursuing your own goals: for example, if you want to write that novel (Devotion), discipline might mean setting the same time aside each morning to write, even if you’re tempted to sleep in. The difference is you’re being disciplined in service of your ideal self rather than just to meet an external demand. This kind of discipline is what helps bridge the gap on days when motivation is low – it’s important, because even following your dreams has boring or hard days. However, avoid using discipline to suppress every natural impulse or need (like never resting or never exploring tangents). Remember the research: forcing yourself nonstop with stress hormones will eventually break you down[10]. So yes, be disciplined in pursuing mastery and purpose, but also be disciplined about recovery and reflection. It takes discipline to actually take a break when you know you need one, in a culture that glorifies busyness.Redefine productivity

Don’t measure your productivity solely by output per hour or tasks per day. Include metrics like quality, creativity, and learning. If one day you had a breakthrough idea after an hour of staring out the window (Awareness state), that’s productive – perhaps more so than 8 hours of mindlessly answering emails. Many forward-thinking companies are realizing that outcomes matter more than face-time. As an individual, you can track accomplishments in terms of both efficient execution and meaningful progress.

This guards against the trap of equating “busy-ness” with success. It also highlights who benefits: if you did fewer things but they were high-impact, you benefit (pride, perhaps profit or recognition) and so does your organization in the long run. The person asking for 10 trivial things might not like that you only did 5, but if those 5 were truly valuable, it will show. Where possible, communicate and educate those around you about this – sometimes bosses need to learn that more is not better, better is better[38]. For example, one source pointed out that true productivity is about quality, not quantity – one brilliant idea or high-quality output can be worth far more than ten mediocre ones, meaning that creativity and thoughtfulness drive real productivity[38].Avoid the mediocrity trap

Don’t let “good enough” always be good enough, at least not for the things that matter. It’s fine to be mediocre at unimportant tasks (not every email needs to be poetry), but identify areas where you want to excel and give yourself permission to occasionally strive for an A. This may involve pushing back on unrealistic time expectations or carving out extra time to refine something.

It’s here that following the first DREAM approach pays dividends – if you work in Mastery mode when it naturally occurs, you will produce some A-level work. Protect those opportunities. It can help to share with stakeholders that a bit more time or a different process will lead to a better result – often, people will understand if the value is clear. The pride and mastery you cultivate by sometimes doing things excellently will fuel your motivation far more than years of churning out mediocrity. Over the long run, excellence can become a personal brand that benefits your career and self-esteem – and that never comes from always taking the path of least resistance or fastest delivery.Listen to your “youthful music”

Finally, reconnect with what you truly care about – those dreams or interests that have been whispering in the background. Perhaps when you were younger you loved drawing, or coding, or helping people, but you took a different path because it seemed more responsible. It’s never too late to start integrating that “music” into your life. It might not mean quitting your job tomorrow, but it could mean starting a project on the side, taking a class, or seeking a role that aligns better with your passions. Research shows that people who engage their core interests and strengths at work tend to be more engaged and perform better. And as we saw, you are much less likely to regret giving your ideal self a chance than you are to regret staying safely in your cocoon of obligation[25][26].

Claim your agency now, rather than hoping for a future allowance. The sooner you do, the more time you have to build a life that resonates with you. Even within a current job, that might mean asserting a bit of independence – proposing a creative initiative, or simply doing a task in a new way that you find more fulfilling. It might ruffle some feathers if it challenges the status quo, but often results will speak louder: when people work from passion and strength, they produce outstanding work, which can win others over.

### Two dreams, one choice

The two expansions of DREAM represent a fundamental tension in how we approach work and personal growth. On one side, Cognitive States + Personal Autonomy offer a path of engagement, quality, and self-actualization – the path of long-term hope realized in the present, rather than forever deferred. On the other side, Discipline + On-Demand Duty offer predictability and short-term compliance, but at the risk of personal stagnation and future regret – a path of quiet resignation masked as responsibility.

The science of flow, motivation, and creativity overwhelmingly suggests that individuals and organizations stand to gain more when people are psychologically free and attuned – when they are in their Devotion or Resonance or Mastery – than when they are merely present and accounted for under strict schedules[3][14]. Of course, society will always need a bit of discipline and timeliness; the trains must run on time to an extent. The key is not letting the means (discipline) obliterate the ends (purposeful, excellent work and a fulfilling life).

The invitation is to see discipline not as blind obedience, but as devotion to one’s own meaningful goals. By doing so, we transform from being users of our own mind on someone else’s terms to being true agents of our lives. And as the evidence shows, those who take that agency – who follow their music – tend to achieve not only greater personal happiness but often greater contributions to the world as well. After all, most great art, innovation, or progress didn’t come from people mindlessly following orders; it came from individuals in a state of passionate flow, daring to do things differently.
