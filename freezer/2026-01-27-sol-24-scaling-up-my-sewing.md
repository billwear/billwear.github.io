I re-calibrated my rusty sewing skills by making “mug rugs,” like this one:[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ndWi!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff4427887-a79d-4917-bfeb-a44e3241b7e0_785x785.jpeg)

Sold a basket full for good money.  Now I can make those with leftover scraps, so nothing goes to waste.  

Next up? Phone purses, which seem to be fairly popular locally.  At the moment, I’m only doing local sales.  I found a pattern I bought maybe ten or fifteen years ago.  The pattern was too small for modern cell phones, hence the first version was too small.  
Not flawed, just cramped.

Still, I found all kinds of things I’d do better.  Thats how creative work speaks to you: Not in criticism, but in constraint.

Today, I listened. I took the same basic phone-purse idea and scaled it up by one and a half times. Same shape. Same intent. More breathing room. And the piece changed character immediately:[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!y0jV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdd7558fe-be0e-4d89-8a6e-adbc96fe3ac4_3112x1754.jpeg)

You could feel it in the fabric before you ever put a phone inside. This one wanted to be used, in spite of the fact that it didn’t have a strap yet.

### What I changed

The core move was simple: **scale everything 1.5x**. Height. Width. Flap depth. Seam allowances stayed sane, but the body grew.

Instead of treating the flap as an afterthought, I built it into the back panel as a single piece. Back and flap were cut together, then sewn **reversed**, so when the whole thing turned right-side out, the flap rolled cleanly over the top without a visible seam break. I missed the edge of the seam on one side, but for a muslin, this was doggone good work.

From first to second muslin, I did a lot of design work:
- 

fewer visible seams
- 

cleaner exterior lines
- 

a flap that feels intentional instead of attached

For the front panel, I went oversized on purpose. I knew I could take fabric away later, but I couldn’t add it back. That extra material gave me freedom at the machine:
- 

I could square the sides under tension
- 

I could correct drift as I went
- 

I could decide *where* the seams wanted to land instead of forcing them

Once front and back were prepped, I stitched the body together and turned it.  
No heroics. Just steady work.

### What didn’t work (and why that matters)

I also tried to make a strap from a narrow strip of the same black polka-dot fabric.

On paper, it should have worked. In practice, it was too narrow to turn cleanly. That’s not a failure. That’s information. Some fabrics forgive narrow tubes. This one doesn’t.

Knowing *which* materials turn and which ones fight you is the kind of knowledge you only earn by trying. I stopped before forcing it, set the strap aside, and moved on.

That restraint matters.

### What I see now

This is my **second muslin**, and it’s already flirting with sellable. The scale feels right.  
The contrast between the black exterior and the dotted interior feels modern and quiet. The structure is soft where it should be, firm where it counts.

What still needs work is the inside:
- 

seam finishes need to be hidden or celebrated, not left raw
- 

the flap geometry needs to land more precisely on the side seams
- 

edge bulk needs to be managed, not ignored

Those aren’t problems. They’re *next decisions*.

This one didn’t get perfect. It got *clear*. And that’s how things become real.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
