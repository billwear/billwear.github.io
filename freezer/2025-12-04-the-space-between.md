title: "The space between"

They stared at each other like two devices caught transmitting on the same illegal frequency. Both of them waiting for the other to pretend nothing had happened. Both failing.

Elara’s eyes still carried the residue of what she’d seen in him — internal circuitry firing under human skin, a pulse too clean to belong to muscle or fear.

Aren was trying to hide the way her resonance had hit his systems like a soft, unwanted diagnostic.

Static between strangers.

For a thin moment they both thought the same thing, wrong but comforting: *Maybe all Earth people are like this. *Faulty. Tuned. Half-awake in ways the locals didn’t acknowledge.

The lie held for maybe three seconds.

Then Elara blinked, recalibrating her expression into something approximate to human politeness.  
“I haven’t given you my name,” she said, voice low, like she was offering a fragile data packet she wasn’t sure he should have. “Elara. Elara Wyn.”

Aren nodded once, too deliberate.  
“Your phone number came with that attached,” he said. “So did mine.”  
He paused, as if listening for internal permission. “Aren Vos.”

Their names hung in the cold, mismatched frequencies aligning just long enough to be dangerous.

Silence again. Neither trusted it.

“So,” Elara said, tone casual enough to be a tell. “Are people here always this… perceptive?”

Aren’s mouth ticked upward, nothing like a smile. “Are people here always this adaptive?”

The angle-shooting began — careful verbal probing, each trying to extract a clue without giving one.

He asked how long she’d lived “in this region.” She asked what “systems” he worked with.  
Neither was wrong; neither was right. A closed loop masquerading as small talk.

Then the orchard made a sound.

Not a warning. Not a signal.  
Just a small metallic adjustment — like something settling its weight underground.

Elara’s head snapped toward it.  
Aren’s posture tightened by instinct.  
They both reacted too fast. Too precisely. Another shared malfunction.

That was the break.

They stopped pretending this was a normal human glitch and stepped — carefully — into the space between what they were and what Earth needed them to be.

They moved toward the orchard, but not together. Elara drifted ahead like she was reading faint pressure lines in the air. Aren trailed her, listening inward, catching small calibration spikes he didn’t want her to notice.

“Is the residue back?” he asked.

“No,” she said. “Just… the absence after it.” She touched the old irrigation pipe with two fingers held just above the frost. Not touching metal — touching the idea of metal.  
Listening to the memory of its last hum.

Aren waited for a real reading. Nothing. Just cold dirt. Then, a faint flicker passed through his chest—not a malfunction, not a warning, just a momentary sync with something he didn’t understand.

He exhaled too sharply. She caught it.  
Her shoulders went rigid for half a second.

“Aren.”

Quiet. Accusation folded inside concern.

“You felt that.”

“It was nothing,” he said.  
A lie unworthy of either of them.

She wasn’t looking at him anymore.  
She was looking at the orchard as if the rows had shifted when no one was watching.

“This place isn’t stable,” she whispered.

“Neither are we.”

It almost made her smile. Almost.

But the orchard stayed too still, the wrong kind of still — like equipment that had powered down but still kept one sensor awake.

Aren stepped back first. Elara hesitated, then followed. They didn’t talk on the walk back. Didn’t look at each other. Didn’t try to decode what had happened.

Halfway to the house, Aren glanced over his shoulder. The orchard hadn’t moved. Hadn’t pulsed. Hadn’t breathed.

But the geometry of it felt aware, like the planet had started running a low-level scan on both of them and didn’t yet know what result it wanted.

Elara didn’t look back. That unsettled him more than the orchard.

Behind them, in the frost-bitten rows, something small shifted again — too soft to classify, too deliberate to ignore.

Not a signal. Not a warning. Just the world remembering they existed.
