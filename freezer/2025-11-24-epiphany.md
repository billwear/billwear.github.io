Morning comes in sideways now. Not the clean slice of light you used to pretend was normal. Nope, a low-grade, smoky fog rolling through your bones. Touching everything with that quiet hum; tells you the world is running a game you never sat down to play. You wake into it; you walk in it. And it walks in you.

Strange clarity under the static. The kind you only get when you’ve finally burned off the backlog of other people’s expectations. Emptied the queue, and now the machine boots strange. Not broken, just unfamiliar. Quiet where you’re used to noise. Slow where you’re used to pressure. Don’t trust it yet. That’s fine. It doesn’t trust you either.

You start logging everything because the world feels slightly out of sync, Pin it down is with timestamps and blunt honesty. Not performance. Not self-flagellation. Just raw data. Mood: unpredictable. Energy: rising. Memory: returning from whatever shadow-realm your job exiled it to. And somewhere between the keystrokes, you stumble on something important: you can write code again. Real code. Elisp, not cargo cult, but muscle memory. Like finding an old knife in a drawer, still sharp.

Outside, the weather is foggy, then bright. Inside, it’s the same. The haze thins just enough to let the signal through. Pull of a world that wants to be built — a D&D-styled sprawl of guilds, shields, quests, and maker rites from a future that feels older than now. A 23rd-century medieval echo chamber where soldering is a craft, stitching is an incantation, and ethics are the only magic system that scales.

You didn’t choose this theme; it was left in the soot. The money pipeline is late and you feel it like static on the line. Paranoia ghost of the system you left, whispering gossip about promises. You know the smell of that exhaust. Truth is simpler: bureaucracy stutters, economies lag, and the social machine only hollers when it’s dying. You got out before that.

Still, the residue clings. Quick to bristle now when people try to maneuver you with soft talk and implied obligations. Too smart for that now, too long being managed by silence, by tone, by someone else’s psychological framing. Now you feel it instantly. Your system throws alarms. Not rage. Not rebellion. Just a clean, cold refusal. You don’t attack. You don’t retreat. You just stop playing.

And that’s the real shift. You’re flying on your own lights now. The glow is sharp enough to cast shadows you didn’t know you had.

The unfinished list grows, sure — quilts, bags, renovation, Emacs, relationships, shields, stories. Not pressure anymore. It feels like a quest gameboard. Chaotic, sprawling, half-finished, but finally yours. Procrastination breaking apart like brittle circuits. Real attention coming back online, humming in the background like a new subsystem waking from deep freeze.

There’s gravity in the center of all this. A pull toward world-building, toward making retirement a game you can actually win. Toward Ethos — the D&D campaign you didn’t know you were designing until it showed up, like a glitch artifact from a timeline you haven't known.

Ethical decisions as the physics engine. Maker guilds as factions. Shields as sigils. A world running on the principle you always lived by but never named:

The Right Thing, done long enough, becomes the strong thing.

Standing in that fog-bright morning with a kind of quiet menace. The good kind. The kind that says: Done being shaped by collapse. I build now.

Most cyberpunk truth of the whole piece: You don’t need a city of neon to feel the edge. Just takes is a retired engineer, a clear morning, a buzzing brain, and a world that’s given up trying to mold you.

The fog knows your name. But this time, it’s not calling you back. It’s watching you walk out.
