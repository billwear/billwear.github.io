title: "This borrowed earth"

The machine arrived early.

Not by much — an hour, maybe ninety minutes — but enough that he felt the shift before the knock. Like some part of the world had stepped half a frame out of sync so it could put the box on his doorstep before he had time to brace for it.

Rain slicked the pavement, softening the cardboard at the edges. He touched the seam, feeling that familiar internal alignment — the fire tightening, not brightening. A tool approaching orbit.

He brought it inside.

Placed it on the table. Peeled the tape. Lifted through the layers of foam. Metal. Weight. Balance.

Earth-made, but with a presence that didn’t belong only to Earth.

Then his phone buzzed.

> 

*Did it come?*  
*I don’t know why I’m this excited for you.*

He stared at the words. Her excitement threaded differently through the world — quieter, truer. She wasn’t reacting to the machine. She was reacting to *him* choosing something again.

He lifted the machine from the box. It hummed faintly — not sound, not heat, just recognition. As if the tool had already mapped his intent and was waiting for the rest of him to catch up.

Another buzz.

> 

*You okay?*  
*Want me to come see it?*

He hesitated, thumb hovering.  
He didn’t know if he wanted company or if the fire wanted witness. Those weren’t the same thing.

This world he lived in — this borrowed Earth — didn’t always translate intention cleanly. But tonight there was no static in the signal.

He typed:

> 

*Door’s unlocked. If you want.*

He set the phone down like it was fragile.

Rain ticked against the front window.  
The machine sat on the table like the beginning of a map.

Then her footsteps — soft, quick, unpracticed for this kind of moment.  
A knock that wasn’t really a knock, more a courtesy signal as she pushed the door open and stepped inside.

She stopped when she saw it.

Not him — *it*.

The machine, the cleared space, the way the air felt slightly denser, like an engine warming in a sealed bay.

“You weren’t kidding,” she said, her voice catching on something she didn’t name. She closed the door behind her, shaking rain from her hair. “It looks… I don’t know. Like it belongs here.”

He watched her cross the room, drawn not to him but to the tool, fingers hovering just above the housing the way you hover a hand over a sleeping animal.

“Can I?” she asked.

He nodded.

Her fingertips touched the metal.  
The fire inside him flared — not heat, not light. **Calibration.**

She looked up at him, realizing something had shifted in the room.

“This is a big step for you,” she said quietly. Not a question. Not commentary. Recognition.

He didn’t answer. Couldn’t. The moment had that gravity — the kind where the wrong word could bend the wrong beam and collapse the whole structure.

Instead he stepped closer, close enough that the warmth from her soaked through the cool machine shell. She didn’t pull back. She didn’t overplay it either. Just stood there, steady, grounded, as if she’d been waiting at the threshold of this moment longer than he had.

“You’re building again,” she whispered.

And something in his chest — the fire, the circuitry, the part of him that still remembered a ship that no longer existed — aligned to a single point.

Not warmth. Direction.

He reached past her and rested his hand on the machine beside hers.

“Yeah,” he said softly. “It’s time.”

She smiled, and it wasn’t loud. It was a vector.

The fire didn’t warm him.

It triangulated.

And he wasn’t alone in the room when it did.
