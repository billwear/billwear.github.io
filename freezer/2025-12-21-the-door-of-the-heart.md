title: "The door of the heart"
date: 2025-12-21
---

There is a door inside you that does not open for convenience. You meet it quietly, often without naming it: a pause in your body, a slight inward lean, a hesitation that isn’t fear so much as recognition. You are standing in front of something that matters, and the fact that it matters is precisely why your hand hovers.

This door appears before work you can’t fake; before choices that would bind you to your own values; before actions that would cost you comfort in exchange for integrity. It shows up before devotion. Most days, you don’t think of it as a door at all. You just feel yourself slow. Your hand reaches for the handle and stops, not because you doubt what’s on the other side, but because opening it would make something real.

---

The world misreads this moment. It says that if something is right, it should feel easy, energizing, certain, free of resistance. So when the door doesn’t open smoothly, you assume something is wrong — with the timing, with the plan, with you. You step back. You tidy the hallway, polish intentions, prepare to prepare. You tell yourself you’ll come back when you feel clearer, stronger, more worthy.

But the door doesn’t go anywhere, because it is not merely guarding opportunity. It is guarding devotion.

Devotion is not enthusiasm, not passion, not motivation. Devotion is the willingness to let something matter enough that it can change you. That is why the door resists. Behind it are the things you say you care about when no one is listening: honesty that costs you shortcuts; ethics that complicate easy wins; work you can stand behind without explanation; beliefs you don’t abandon just because abandoning them would be convenient.

Your gifts live there too — the ones you didn’t ask for but are responsible for anyway; the skills that only you can offer in the particular way you offer them. Rare and valuable things live there, and they do not accept casual entry.

---

This is why prayer is so difficult for people, even for people who believe in it. Not because they doubt it works, but because prayer is an act of devotion. When you pray, you are not just thinking quietly; you are asking, or admitting, or offering something that matters.

You are naming what you care about; risking disappointment; exposing hope; committing to the idea that what you are about to say is worth saying out loud, even if nothing changes. Prayer requires opening the door, and once you do that, you can’t pretend you didn’t mean it.

The same resistance shows up before your best work — not the busy work, not the safe work, but the work that uses your real gifts; the work that would make it obvious what you believe in; the work that would reveal what you are actually capable of, not just what you can perform.

That hesitation is not reluctance to act. It is reluctance to commit. Because once you open that door, you are no longer browsing, no longer experimenting, no longer protected by irony or distance. You are saying yes to a standard you will be measured against, mostly by yourself.

This is why devotion is scarier than failure. Failure at least gives you an explanation. Devotion removes the alibi.

---

There is a moment like this that happens far from any audience, with no applause and no witnesses. You stand alone, aware that you could walk away and nothing visible would break; you would still be reasonable, sensible, unremarkable.

Instead, you rest your hand on the handle. You don’t throw the door open. You don’t need to. You turn it just enough to feel the mechanism engage, enough to know you’re no longer pretending the door isn’t there. That is the turn.

The door of your heart does not open to confidence. It opens to consent. When you allow yourself to be devoted, something clarifies. You stop asking whether you feel like doing the thing and start asking whether the thing is worthy of you: Does it align with who you are at your most honest?  Does it deserve your attention, your patience, your care?

Devotion simplifies without making life small. It says: these are the things I will show up for even when I’m tired; these are the standards I will not abandon when they become inconvenient; these are the gifts I will use, not because they guarantee success, but because withholding them would be a form of dishonesty.

---

You don’t need to devote yourself to everything. You shouldn’t. The world is full of noise that wants the appearance of devotion without the cost — hustle, optimization, performance, endless motion without commitment. But the door inside you knows the difference. It only resists when what’s behind it would ask for your whole heart instead of your compliance.

That resistance is not an obstacle. It is the threshold. Lives with depth are not built by flinging doors open in moments of inspiration. They are built by returning to the same door, day after day, and opening it just enough to step through again — slowly, deliberately, without spectacle. That is how devotion takes root. That is how rare value is grown. That is how integrity becomes embodied instead of claimed.

You don’t have to open every door. But the ones your heart keeps will not stay closed forever. And when you are ready, the work is not to force them, but to place your hand on the handle and consent to what you already know matters.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
