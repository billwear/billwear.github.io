I’m in good health, in a good season, and I’m not in a rush. My wife and I will go to town later. There will be cake. Maybe a doctor visit. An oil change. Probably a couple of lottery tickets. I’m not confused about the math, but I’m also not allergic to hope.

So this is not a heroic workday, and that matters.

I retired on Halloween last year, two and a half months ago. Since then, I haven’t touched a line of code, not even casually. That surprised me, because programming is what I trained for. It’s what I did for decades — first as a student, then as an engineer and physicist, and eventually as a computer scientist. Unix has been the background radiation of my adult life.

Working for money is important. If I need to, I’ll do it again. But five decades of building other people’s ideas for other people’s margins leaves a residue. Not anger, just staleness.

Last weekend, I made a small but important decision: I’m reclaiming programming as a hobby. Not a side hustle or a backup plan. A craft.

When I sat down today to do it, I immediately recognized the pattern. The old end-of-career behaviors showed up right on cue:
- 

Maybe I should get a snack.
- 

It’s a little chilly — sweatshirt?
- 

Who just texted me?
- 

Let me check email.
- 

Facebook.
- 

Oh look, cat videos.

This is what “I’m done with this career” looks like in the body. The problem? I *am* done with the career, so the behaviors have to go too. That means approaching the work differently and deliberately.

Different operating system. Different language. Different shell. Still Emacs, mind you, but on my own terms.

I’m on a Mac now, working in zsh on Darwin, learning Rust. Not because it’s trendy, and not because I need it. But because it’s unfamiliar enough to break the grooves, and disciplined enough to reward attention.

I’m not opening the Rust book I bought years ago. It feels too academic and corporate, like being trained for a *role* instead of discovering a *craft*.

Instead, I’m letting an old friend in India teach me, one failure, one program at a time. No objectives except the ones I stumble into, and no one assigning me outcomes. This is slower than the way I used to work, and that’s the point.

There’s angst here. The avoidance reflex is strong, but at least I recognize it now. I know what it feels like when deep work is trying to re-enter a life that’s been optimized for shallow progress.

So today isn’t *about* progress, but re-entry. Sitting still long enough to remember that I already know how to do this — just not this way, and not for these reasons.

That feels worth documenting. Oh, wait, I just did.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
