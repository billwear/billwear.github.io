Every few decades, a new tool arrives and the world reacts the same way. First awe. Then excess. Then fear. And finally, a story line about how this time, humanity itself is at risk.

Here we go again.

Right now the noise says AI is a bubble, a fraud, a ticking bomb. That it will collapse and take whole sectors of society with it. That it will hollow out meaning, replace judgment, and leave us with something efficient and empty — devolving the internet into a cesspool of slop.

It sounds dramatic. It always does.

What’s happening feels new *only* because we forgot the pattern. We’ve seen this before.

You may not remember the sermons, but they happened. Automobiles were said to sever moral order. Radio was called demonic influence entering the home. Movies were accused of hypnotizing the soul. Telegraphy was feared because presence no longer required proximity. Television would rot the mind. The internet would end truth. Smartphones would end attention. Social media would end democracy.

Again and again, the same fear appears wearing new clothes: that change itself is a force that will destroy mankind. Not because the tool worshipped anything dark, but because power moved somewhere unfamiliar. Somewhere unseen — and more importantly, somewhere beyond the control of the current order of self-appointed moral police.

Every time, the accusation was really about loss. Loss of status. Loss of prestige. Loss of certainty.

Brittle roles built on imperfect ideas do not age well. A much better translation of these objections is probably:

“Stop playing God. (That’s *my* job.)”

Here is the quieter truth.

AI is not a god. It is not an artificial mind. And it is not even new in the way people think it is. It is a tool in a long, boring, deeply human lineage.

If you have ever piped text from one command to another, you already understand it. If you have ever trusted a script you did not fully read, you already felt its danger. If you have ever watched a clean, fast output be subtly wrong, you already know where responsibility lives.

AI is a filter. A compressor. A pipe. A cousin to awk, sed, regex, and Perl. A child of ELIZA and the Emacs doctor. A grandchild of mathematics, semantics, and logic.

It moves symbols, not meaning.  
It accelerates patterns, not judgment.

Like sed, awk, Perl, or a shell script, it does astonishing things without knowing *why* it did them. And like every powerful Unix tool before it, it will **happily automate mistakes at scale**.

That does not make it evil. It just makes it a bigger crowbar.

The bubble everyone is arguing about is not technical. It is economic and psychological.

Bubbles form when stories outrun literacy. When abstraction outruns understanding. When people stop reading the output and start trusting the tool as if it were an authority.

The crash, if it comes, will not be about AI failing. It will be about shortcuts failing. Crashes always are. Weak ideas wrapped in powerful tools do not become strong. They just collapse faster.

What breaks in a correction is always the same: confidence without comprehension; speed without inspection; automation without ownership.

Here is a small, ordinary scene.

You run a command. It finishes instantly. The output looks clean. Convincing. Complete.

Something feels off.

You pause. You read it again. You realize the error is not in the tool. It is in the assumption you fed it. You fix it. Problem solved.

The responsibility never left you. It never does.

This is where the fear narrative gets things exactly backward.

**Change does not destroy humanity. Abdication does.**

The danger is not that tools get stronger. It is that people forget how to slow them down. Those who survive any correction are not the fastest adopters. They are the ones who understand the primitives. The ones who can step into the pipeline mid-stream. The ones who know when to stop the command and think.

AI does not replace judgment.  
It exposes where judgment was missing all along.

### For you
- 

Read the output before trusting it. Every. Single. Freakin. Time.
- 

Break one automated flow into visible steps this week.
- 

Insert a pause where speed currently feels impressive.
- 

Keep one important task manual on purpose. (Hint: my phone interface is a command line.)
- 

Learn one tool deeply instead of ten superficially.
- 

Treat assistance as leverage, *not* authority.
- 

Own the final decision, even when the tool feels confident.

This moment is not a rupture. It is a continuation.

A louder pipe, yes. A faster filter, most definitely. Still blind to meaning. Still waiting for a human to decide what matters.

If the noise stopped tomorrow, the people who endure are the ones who can explain their tools, slow them down, and take responsibility for what flows through them.

This is why I always say:

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
