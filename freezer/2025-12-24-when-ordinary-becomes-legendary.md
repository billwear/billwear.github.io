We don’t actually come with instructions, but sometimes we really need them.  Moments arrive with no edges, no map, and uncertain permission. You’re not tired, not rested, not lost, not found: just there. The room is dim. The cat wants your constant attention. The tea cools too fast. Outside, the dense fog erases the view.  Hard to get oriented in those situations.

You move anyway, laying things out and removing later friction. Set the pieces where your hands will find them later, not to create discipline, but because you know how easily the day can collapse back into bed if you don’t lower the activation energy just enough.  Then you’re ready in thirty minutes, but you still don’t know where the day is supposed to go.

---

Typical systems don’t handle this well. Believe me, I’ve tried all the day planners, and none of them can handle this. The assumptions about clarity, effort, direction and powering through all sound great, but I just don’t work that way. And neither do a lot more people than anyone wants to admit.

Mental fog is a condition, not an obstacle. Your mind doesn’t sequence cleanly. There’s no polite queue where thoughts wait their turn. Ideas, impulses, images, emotions, and plans arrive together, like weather fronts colliding over warm water. There is no hard wall between reason and intuition. Not even a thin one.

Yet, somehow, you function. You build things. You ship work. You make decisions that hold, not by force, but by alignment.  There’s that word “devotion” again.  Used to think this was my superpower. John Gott disagrees; says that you are probably not living at a special point in anything. So this pattern isn’t rare, just under-described.

---

To be fair, what’s actually happening is bandwidth, not chaos. The edge ego, that fast layer in your head that operates before language, before judgment, before narration? It recognizes patterns the way radar recognizes terrain: not by detail, but by gradient and shape and pull and direction. When conditions are right, this layer integrates enormous amounts of information faster than you can think.

And that edge ego is what I refer to when I say, “inner fire.”[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!db0l!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fada7c631-73bc-4b55-876b-02467ec3c0ea_1024x1536.png)

Sometimes it’s overwhelming.  Okay, a lot of time.  You assume the slower layer is in charge, and apply pressure and discipline. More force equals more output, right?  But when the edge ego is dominant, pressure just makes it worse.  Like trying to tune a weak signal while standing under live power lines. All you get is noise. The harder you try, the worse the interference becomes. Fragmentation replaces coherence. You don’t just slow down. You become unable to really function.

So you learn, slowly, to work differently. You stop trying to hold the fog open for hours. You stop mistaking endurance for depth. You stop apologizing for needing clean surfaces and short passes. Instead, you work in pulses.

Ten minutes. Fifteen. Sometimes twenty. Enter clean; translate what arrives; exit before the signal smears. From the outside, it looks shallow. From the inside, it’s surgical. This is journalistic deep work and it’s not a compromise, and it’s not a fallback.  It’s a way of capturing the creative output of a mind that has already gone through fifteen million possible meanings and settled on the very best one, and it is going to tell you about it, ready or not.

In this case, the goal isn’t to maintain focus. It’s to respect bandwidth. Short, aligned bursts outperform long forced sessions because they match the tempo of integration. Not stamina, but tempo.

---

This is why you remove friction instead of adding pressure. Why you simplify where others optimize. Why you build slowly where others rush. The output isn’t volume of work, it’s coherent results. You make things that hold together because they come from one mind, one aesthetic, one signal path. When you give value freely, it’s not bait. It’s proof. What you sell isn’t rare because it’s scarce, but because it has such a complete gestalt.

This isn’t hustle or grind. It’s work that depends on signal integrity. And on days like this — Christmas Eve, family everywhere, plans arriving last-minute, fog outside and inside — the task is not to cut through the fog. The task is to work *with it*: short passes; clean surfaces. Enter. Translate. Exit.

That’s how this kind of work not only survives the weather, but makes it rain.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
