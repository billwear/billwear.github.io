Some nights the fire inside him didn’t offer warmth so much as a pulse — a low, directional flicker, like something buried in the hull of a ship that no longer existed. It was never dramatic. Just persistent. A reminder that even a displaced engineer still carried circuitry the world couldn’t overwrite.

The fast world wanted distraction.  
But the fire wanted direction.  
And direction was beginning to take shape.

He’d been stranded long enough to understand that Earth wasn’t a temporary layover. The molecular distortion field that shredded his shuttle had taken that illusion with it. All he had left was gravity, makeshift tools, an unfamiliar body, and the quiet sense that if he didn’t start building soon, the fire would eat through the floor of whatever life he thought he was adapting to.

Earlier that evening he’d been sitting across from the neighbor — the only human who treated him like he wasn’t a ghost trying to pass. She wore warmth like armor, leaning forward when she talked, always smiling a half-beat too long. The kind of person who acted like she didn’t know she had a crush, even though the whole room surely did.

The restaurant lighting was harsh, tungsten and amber, the kind that revealed every scratch on the tabletops and every second thought in his mind. She asked about his day. He gave her the safe version. She laughed too softly, eyes holding longer than they should.

But underneath her voice he felt the hum — the fire waking, the engineering subroutines coming online again. He let his hand drift to his phone, thumb sliding across the glass like he was touching an interface panel from home.

“I’ve been thinking about making things again,” he said, and it sounded too simple, like a decoy for what the fire was really whispering.

He turned the screen toward her.  
A sewing machine — industrial enough to survive war, precise enough to coax a living thread from dead fabric. A primitive device by the standards of the place he came from, but it carried the same promise: *you can build again.*

She leaned in, brushing his hand without meaning to. Or maybe meaning to.

“Oh,” she said, voice thinning into something bright. “That looks… right for you.”

The fire flexed in his chest — small, directional, inevitable.

He watched the machine’s product page reflect in her eyes, the neon edge of potential bending across her pupils. The world felt strangely quiet in that moment. Outside, rain traced nervous lines down the windows, but none of it reached him. The restaurant noise flattened to a hum, like static on a failing comms channel.

He tapped the order button.

No plan, no caution. Just the fire choosing for him.

For a heartbeat he could almost hear his shuttle again — not breaking this time, not burning — but resonating, like old systems clearing dust and realigning in the dark.

A new tool was coming.  
And with it, the first shape of the life he hadn’t dared build yet.

The fire didn’t warm him.  
It rebuilt him.

And it wasn’t done.
