Sometime in the night, a certainty arrived. It didn’t come with trumpets or visions, just a quiet recognition that felt like a ground wire snapping into place. I had spent my whole life searching for the message, the way, the meaning. And it turns out, my gift was the message all along.

I should have seen it earlier. In my first college English class, an exam required a full paper written on the spot. I barely skimmed the play; instead I read the character list, sat down, and wrote. My professor, a revered critic, told me it was excellent and worth publishing. I ignored her.

Again and again, I ignored it. Whenever life pressed me into writing — drafting a manual overnight, sketching a course that a colleague said was already a book, explaining what others struggled to explain — I treated it like an accident, not a calling. I told myself it was too easy, too natural, too obvious to be rare and valuable. Surely everyone could do it. Surely this couldn’t be the gift. So I chased other futures, other jobs, other securities.

And yet, no matter where I went — the military industrial complex, the tech world, teaching, defense contracts — writing was the signal that kept cutting through the static. Every pivot bent me back toward words, toward explanation, toward carrying a message.

For years I kept standing in my own way, convinced that what came most naturally couldn’t possibly be my true work. I looked everywhere for provision: new careers, new industries, new schemes. But every road led me back to the same thing: me, sitting with words, finding the signal, hearing the incoming message carried on the wave beneath everything else.

The irony is sharp now. I thought writing was mundane, too common to be a gift. But it was the ground plane I had been looking for, the reference signal humming underneath my life. The thing I thought too small turned out to be the only thing large enough to hold me.

We all wait for a message from God, from the universe, from someone we trust. But often, our gift *is* the message. Ignore it, and you wind up lost in jobs you dislike, lives that don’t fit. Honor it, and the way opens.

So here I am, at last stepping aside, no longer resisting the signal. My gift is the message. It always was.

## For You

If you’ve read this far, maybe you feel that tug too, that quiet thing you’ve always dismissed as too easy, too obvious, too ordinary. Pay attention. That ease might be the very sign of your gift.

Here’s how to lean into it:
- 

Notice what keeps pulling you back, no matter how many times you try to leave it.
- 

Listen for what others see in you — the compliments you brush off because “anyone could do that.”
- 

Stop treating your gift as an accident. Give it the dignity of your time and practice.
- 

Trust that what feels natural to you is rare to others. That’s what makes it valuable.

Your gift is already sending out a signal. The only question is: *will you tune in and listen?*
