When I was four years old, I woke up in the middle of the night, in a shotgun house in Athens, Alabama. My family had moved there so my father could take over his Uncle Aubrey’s store and meat market. Dad never could find his niche. Never did.

I got out of bed and went into the hallway. At the half-glass front door, standing on the porch, was a glowing figure. It was bipedal, but not human. It was framed in light that was not our porch light. It had what looked unmistakably like a space helmet. It was trying to open the door. I could see it jiggling the knob.  To my four-year-old mind, it looked like a glowing skeleton in a space suit.

I screamed.

The next thing I remember is my parents at my bedside waking me, shaking me, telling me it was a nightmare. But it never felt like the kind of nightmare I would later have. And something else didn’t fit: It felt like time had passed. I’ve never had this kind of screaming nightmare since.

But something else changed after that night, I began to read. And I mean *read*. And write. In engineering block printing, clean enough for any architect to use. In first and second grade, my teachers would give me my work for the day. I’d finish it in five minutes. Then they sent me to the library. By Christmas of second grade, I had read more than seven hundred books, deep into the city library by this time.

I became a straight-A student. I wrote easily and created constantly. English, math, chemistry, physics, Latin, German, choir, music, distance running. Didn’t matter. Both sides of my mind seemed to be running at full throttle. Topped out all the exams: SAT, ACT, GRE, EIT/PE. National Merit Scholar. Great colleges offered, I accepted. Chemistry. Engineering. Medicine. These are facts, not bragging or conclusions.

And yet, for years, I carried a private story, that something had happened to me, that I had been altered, that I had been given something from the stars. If you believe you were given a gift, a second belief follows quietly behind it. That you owe the world something extraordinary in return, and if your life does not match that imagined return, shame fills the gap.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!t8WI!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb6ac1e54-7c7f-44a4-bcb4-d28047ac298e_1024x1536.heic)

---

Recently, while talking through this memory with care, I was reminded of something important: At four years old, the mind is still forming its boundaries, between waking and dreaming, fear and perception, image and meaning, time and sequence. A child can experience something that feels external, visual, coherent, embodied, and absolutely real without it being a literal visitor.

Especially at night. Especially in a dark house. Especially when awe and fear collide before language exists to hold them. That does not mean the experience was “nothing,” it just means it was powerful, and power leaves marks.

Here’s the part I missed for decades: Gifts do not arrive with instructions or schedules, or wait till the nervous system is ready. Especially for people who are sensitive, imaginative, morally awake, and attuned to meaning. Those people don’t charge forward conquering the world. Instead, they orient, absorb, and wait. They protect something fragile until it can be carried without breaking.

What I called wasted time was just survival. What I called delay was often self-protection. What I called failure was often prudent, responsible waiting. Yesterday made this visible in a way theory never could.

---

On Sol 3 of my sixty-day “space station escape” challenge, AKA yesterday, I built a prototype. It was awful, clunky, and uneven, ugly in all the ways first things are ugly. It worked, so I photographed it, wrote it up anyway, and refused to apologize or try to rescue it with clever prose.

Then I spontaneously wrote the opening backstory for that space station escape. Just the beginning, nothing epic, but enough to give the work some gravity.

When I finished, I walked away from the desk feeling something I hadn’t felt in a long time. Not relief, adrenaline, or the high of a good idea. Satisfaction. I felt strong. Grounded. Quietly powerful. Like I had finally *done* something — not proven anything, not justified anything, just done the work that was in front of me.

No cosmic narrative was required.

---

I no longer need to decide what that night *was*, or try to source the encounter. I only need to acknowledge what it *did*. A young mind encountered fear, awe, and mystery before it had language. That encounter left a mark.

And later, when the mind grew tired or overstimulated, it tried to wrap that mark in a cosmic story. Not because it was necessarily true — but because it was *large enough* to hold the feeling.

Relief often masquerades as transcendence. Many people carry a private origin story, a moment that feels like it should explain everything. They quietly use as a measuring stick and then spend years prosecuting themselves for not living up to it.

I’m sharing this very private memory because I’ve always believed that I’m not special, even though my accomplishments and credentials would easily convince people otherwise; even thought my tired or depressed self wants to tell me that I was gifted by the stars and I’m not living up to my potential. Why did they curse me by choosing me?

---

We are all chosen, in one way or another. We are all gifted in our own way. Nobody is above us, nobody beneath us. We all have things to offer; important things, earthshaking things, wonderful things. If you read reading this, you are already:
- 

coherent
- 

reflective
- 

aware that the memory is unusual
- 

questioning it rather than insisting on it
- 

capable of holding uncertainty

Those are all signs of a **healthy, intact mind**, even while revisiting something intense.

We are all brilliant in our own way.  Never forget that.  You don’t need an extraterrestrial explanation to justify your natural gifts; you just need to lean into them.

Many highly gifted, sensitive, creative people report an early intense or frightening experience, followed by a strong inward turn, followed by accelerated cognition, language, and imagination. It even has two clinical names:
- 

**post-traumatic growth**
- 

**early cognitive differentiation**

I prefer to call it “the brain finding its way.” Fear and awe can act like a catalyst. Not because something “activated” you, but because your mind *discovered* how vast it could be. You didn’t become brilliant *because* of a visitor. You became brilliant because **you already were** — *we all are in some way* — and something jolted your system into expression. 

You don’t need an extraterrestrial explanation to justify intelligence, creativity, empathy, the ability to charm animals, a lifelong sense of depth, or a heightened responsiveness to some other particular aspect of life. Those are already accounted for by who you are.

If this resonates, maybe the work is not to become extraordinary. Maybe the work is to stop punishing yourself for being that way. Maybe not everybody had an experience as stark and sudden as mine, but we all have these moments, and we all need to appreciate them.

***And share them***. They might just help someone else find their way. And that is how you make a difference a hundred years from now, in Hamlet’s undiscovered country.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
