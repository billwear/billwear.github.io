I initially majored in neurochemistry in college and won my fair share of undergraduate grants and prizes before switching to a more lucrative field as a junior. But I never stopped studying neurology and neurochemistry — not as a hobby, but because I needed to figure out what was going on in my own head.

Here’s what I learned: I’ve been in a heightened cognitive state [since I was four years old](https://open.substack.com/pub/williamwear/p/sol-4-the-night-i-was-four?utm_campaign=post-expanded-share&utm_medium=web). Not crisis-level — but what most people experience as “flow” or “the zone” or that “elusive creative rush” has been my *baseline* for decades. It’s the state others chase with substances, endless scrolling, or carefully engineered productivity hacks. I just live there.

The difference is, I learned to navigate it without knowing I was managing anything unusual. Like someone blind from birth, I didn’t know there was supposed to be an “off switch.” I just adapted.

And here’s the thing: people who lose their sight later in life can learn to navigate just as well — sometimes better — than those who never had it. They bring intention and method to what the blind-from-birth do by instinct.

If I can manage this continuous high-activation state with minimal pharmaceutical support (just 10mg of Prozac to trim the sharpest edges), you can absolutely learn to generate and manage powerful internal states without touching a substance or scrolling a feed. You might need a little chemical help (from your duly-licensed provider) if your mind runs particularly hot, and that’s fine. But the core skill? You can build it.

Let me show you how.

Picture this: You wake up and before your feet hit the floor, there’s already a voice suggesting three different projects, questioning yesterday’s decisions, and spinning out a tangent about that thing you read last week. Not anxious, exactly — more like *eager*. Insistent. It wants *more*.

That’s my baseline. Always has been.

This isn’t my thinking voice — the one that plans grocery lists or debugs code. *That’s* what I call my **Managing Editor**, the translator between the chaotic symbol stream and functional language. This other voice is different. It doesn’t want to *solve* anything. It wants to *pursue* things. It pitches ideas. It gets bored. It craves novelty, intensity, recognition. It’s the voice that says “but what if we also—” when you’re already doing three things.

You know this voice. It’s the one that solves problems in the shower, spins out insights while you’re driving, delivers the perfect comeback three hours after the conversation ended. It shows up when your hands are busy with something routine — washing dishes, folding laundry, running — because those habitual tasks engage just enough conscious attention to keep your brain from interfering, but not so much that the deeper stream can’t surface.

If you’re a fiction writer, you depend on this voice constantly. It generates characters, spins dialogue, delivers plot twists. But even if you’re not a writer, you’ve felt it. That burst of clarity about a problem you’d been stuck on. That sudden connection between two ideas. That restless energy that makes you want to start something new.

I call it the **Edge Ego**.

And learning to work with it instead of being run by it? That’s been my life’s work — not out of curiosity, but necessity, because mine runs constantly, full volume, in parallel, in my head.

Here’s my premise: creating a whole category called “neurodivergent” is a bit like creating a category for redheads. Some people have brown hair, some curly, some are bald. We notice, we recognize each other, sometimes we celebrate it. But we don’t pathologize it or turn it into an identity crisis.

Brain patterns work the same way. Yours might run quieter than mine. Someone else’s might spike harder during stress. Another person’s might need more external structure to stay engaged. That’s just variation — the same kind of variation we see everywhere else in human experience.

I’m here to normalize that.

When it’s engaged with something genuinely compelling, it feels incredible — that flow state people talk about, where hours vanish and the work just pours out. But when it’s not engaged? It gets restless. It’ll grab onto anything — scrolling, snacking, starting another project you really don’t need, picking a fight with yourself about something embarrassingly stupid you did five years ago. Anything to feel that spark again.

For most of my life, I didn’t realize this *wasn’t* how everyone operated. I thought everyone had to manage this constant low-grade restlessness, this hunger for the next thing. I learned early that feeding it endlessly was a disaster — you burn out, scatter yourself thin, never finish anything. But ignoring it completely was worse. It would find *something* to chew on, and usually it wasn’t helpful.

So I learned to work *with* it, well before Junior High. Give it enough to stay engaged but not so much that it runs me into the ground. Channel it toward things that actually matter instead of whatever’s shiniest.   Recognize when it’s asking for something real, and give it opportunities to create things of rare value.

That’s the skill I want to share.
