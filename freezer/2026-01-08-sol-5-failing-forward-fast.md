Today, I learned by what we used to call the “fail forward fast” approach, which I think has been re-coined as “move fast, break things.” It fits, anyway.

I spent a great deal of time carefully cutting out pieces, based on my lists, but I was worried that my pieces would show raw seams, so I doubled the material. I first carefully ironed the canvas material, slowly, to get it nice and flat. Even with water sprayed from a squirt bottle every few inches, it still took a long time to get this canvas flat and wrinkle-free.

Next, I carefully cut pieces. You’ll note that I had zig-zagged the edges before washing to minimize fraying. That worked pretty well, and it also made me learn how to zig-zag over the edge of the fabric. That will be important in a minute, trust me.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GuuN!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F707cf79b-457e-4290-978e-99eebd3d5998.heic)

When I got done cutting, I had double pieces for everything, along with a heavy-weave lining for each piece. Here’s a shot of the heavy-weave, so you’ll know what I’m talking about. This isn’t fusible, so it has to be embedded in the seams.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Q75u!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9500aa55-baef-4467-a59f-1d40f5b14233.heic)

Here are all the pieces I cut before starting to sew:[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!k7VQ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F517b180b-a024-4f9b-8bb1-a606d23b7710.heic)

From left-to-right, top-to-bottom, it’s the whip pocket, the main pocket fronts, the pocket gussets, and the base that it all gets sewn onto. I started with the pockets, sewing the first pocket front like a normal piece: two pieces of fabric sandwiching the heavy-weave, turned. That didn’t work out very well. First of all, I forgot to take into account the *second* seam allowance needed to sew the pocket front to the gusset, so the pocket front is already too small. Second, it’s unnecessarily thick and hard to sew, because the very place where you’ll be joining is already a heavy seam with five layers of material. Clearly, there’s a reason why most pockets are not doubled.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!hsM8!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fccd81c11-7639-4b4c-a9df-4d9d48a807ad.heic)

This led me on the next try to just sew the outer pieces together. This got me a much better fit (although it’s still a little too small). The zig-zagging worked out okay, though. I did forget to prep the pocket opening in any way, so I had to turn and zig-zag those edges on the inside, which of course produces a zig-zag line on the outside of the pocked. Honestly, at least with shirt pockets, which seam-line a half-inch down from the top of the pocket is perfectly normal.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SQqe!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fca9dd5aa-5801-4e83-ad3e-7679fe7b29e8.heic)

This pocket would probably do, if I could figure out how to sew it to the base! I don’t have a long-arm machine, so it feels like I’d need to turn it out and sew it that way, but that seems like it would look weird.

**Why was it good to do it this way?**

I deliberately didn’t do too much research, since it’s a “fail forward fast” operation. Basically, I tried my best with what knowledge I had, and I made some halfway decent progress. Now I know a lot more about what I need to learn, which will make it easier to do the research for tomorrow.  And, of course, tomorrow ill probably be all about research and lame attempts to figure out what that research is saying.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
