Today I spent four hours listening to shortwave, using WebSDR, without the ability to talk back. Forty-meter band, the Early Birds net based in the Pacific Northwest. No feed, no profile, no likes. Just voices and callsigns, spaced out by propagation and politeness. When I finally stepped away, nothing in my life had technically changed, except me.

Shortwave listening (SWL for short) is not a passive switch-flick. You have to hunt for an intelligible signal, tuning slowly, adjusting bandwidth and sideband to filter out just enough noise and distortion to hear a clear voice. And the strangest thing is, the most obvious part of the signal — the big “streak” in the graphical output — is never the important part.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!FGN6!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2a3be9d0-b1ba-48c6-913e-59f16f42f45d_1354x424.jpeg)

In radio, there’s something called the **carrier**, a strong, steady spike at the center frequency. It holds the signal in place, but it actually carries no information. The real meaning lives in the **sidebands** — the subtle variations just above and below the center frequency. Sidebands mirror the information content that’s in the carrier, but without the intense signal.  That’s where the voice is, all the inflection and humor and intent and humanity. Strip those away and all you have left is empty loudness. You don’t hear sidebands by accident. You have to tune for them.

### **Listening with no agenda**

On the net (*a group of hams that get together at the same time every day on the same frequency*), people talk about weather, dogs, hay, insulation, a bowl turned from walnut with a popsicle stick as a burnishing tool, or a hot water heater hauled out of a church balcony. Everyday conversation, no shiny content or pictures of food. Nobody trying to scale or get likes.

Everybody spoke once across the virtual kitchen table without any pressure to be relevant or trendy. When they were done, they said their “73” (*best regards*) and cleared the frequency. What came across wasn’t performance, it was humility and presence.

### **Why this felt familiar**

As I listened, I realized I already knew this pattern. I learned it just last night as I was struggling to start a novel. A story isn’t really about the facts; they just carry the plot along. The meaning and interest is all about how the characters *react* to those facts by the things they *do* and the things they probably *don’t* say out loud. You don’t explain, you let it leak out, and the reader feels smart and special.

I’ve known this with people, too. Empathy isn’t about hearing what someone actually announces. It’s about tuning for the human sideband — hesitation, repetition, expression and body language. The little details that show up if you pay attention and listen to what they’re *not* saying. 

### **Social media obscures the truth**

Most modern platforms reward carriers: Big statements, bold pictures, fast reactions, clean conclusions. Sidebands take time to tune in, and they don’t spike. They require patience from both sender and receiver.

Shortwave radio, arguably the first social media, refuses the bland economy of social media. You wait your turn, you listen through the noise, and you accept that not every message gets through clearly. And in doing so, you calm down.

### **What stayed with me after the radio went quiet**

When everyone had said their 73, I didn’t feel smarter, but I did feel *less compelled*. No need to comment, chant my point like a mantra, or make sure to show my good side to the camera. Instead, I felt tuned and connected.

Most of what we call engagement today is just our nervous systems reacting to loud carrier waves. Listening is not passive reaction, but active reflection and connection, with genuine appreciation for everyone’s story. It’s skilled, selective, and learned. It takes out the bumps when someone else’s ideas don’t match your personal universe. Shortwave teaches it quietly, every time you’re willing to sit and tune instead of demanding instant dopamine.

I didn’t transmit today, intentionally; that’s why I used WebSDR and not my Yaesu (I knew I wouldn’t keep quiet and listen). The signal that stayed with me wasn’t the strongest one. It was the one off-center, in the realm of normal, everyday people just sharing mundane things that happened recently. 

And it’s truly nice to know that many places in the world, people aren’t reacting to the news or politics or the economy or their friend’s lunch. They’re just calmly sharing  everyday chores and little ambitions, as if to say, “Don’t worry about all that other stuff.  Just take care of what matters to you, and let the rest of it go by.”

**Burn slow. *****Listen***** deeply. Be the proof.**
