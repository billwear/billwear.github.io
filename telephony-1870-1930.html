Why This Era Matters to Networking

Before packets, there were circuits. Early telephony gave us the first at-scale lesson in connecting endpoints under real constraints: human time, copper losses, switching complexity, and money. Most of the problems we still solve today showed up here first, just with cloth-insulated wire and a person on a cord board.

Timeline, Simplified

Key Shifts

  • 1870s: Local manual switchboards. Operator sets up a dedicated circuit for each call.
  • 1890s-1910s: Magneto to common-battery power, multi-office dialing plans, toll lines between cities.
  • 1910s-1930s: Electromechanical automation: step-by-step (Strowger), then crossbar, to remove the human bottleneck.

Vocabulary

  • Circuit Switching: Hold a physical path for the duration of a call.
  • Toll: Long-distance trunk with higher cost and limited capacity.
  • Switch: A device (or person) that connects one line to another under control.

The Two Core Problems

1. Scale

Manual boards do not scale linearly with subscribers. Each new line increases the number of potential connections combinatorially. Operators, cords, and jacks become the bottleneck. Misplugs and busy tone are symptoms of a saturated control plane.

2. Long-Distance Cost

Long copper loops attenuate and pick up noise. Repeaters and loading coils help, but city-to-city trunks are scarce and expensive. Routing a call across multiple towns consumes a toll circuit end to end, even if the far end never picks up. Utilization is poor by design because the path is locked for the whole call.

From Human Control Plane to Automated Control Plane

Strowger's step-by-step switch replaced fingers and cords with relays and selectors. Later, crossbar used a grid of horizontal and vertical bars to make faster, denser connections. In both cases, the control plane (dial pulses, then more sophisticated signaling) set up a temporary dedicated path.

Automation solved operator labor and error rate, but it did not solve the fundamental cost of holding a path. That would require packet switching many decades later.

Dial Plans, Addressing, and Early Routing

As cities added exchanges, numbers became structured: area code, exchange, line. Calls between exchanges traversed trunks, and traffic engineering meant provisioning enough trunks between popular pairs while avoiding blocking. For us, this maps cleanly to modern addressing and routing: you need hierarchy to aggregate, and you need capacity planning to avoid hot spots.

Field Notes for the Modern Reader

Design for Failure First

Circuit-era networks failed hard when any segment saturated. Packet networks succeed by having many cheap paths and fast reroute. Default to repetition over hero boxes.

Measure What Matters

Telephony had Erlangs and grade of service. We have latency distributions, drop counters, and saturation times. Track the few signals that actually drive user experience.

Summary

1870s-1930s telephony built the muscle memory for scale, addressing, and control. Automation (step-by-step, crossbar) removed the human bottleneck, but long-distance cost and path reservation kept utilization low. The next era will trade circuits for packets to fix that.
