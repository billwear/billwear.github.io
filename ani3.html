MAAS

MAAS (Metal as a Service) provisions bare metal servers and virtual machines. It creates a single point of control that removes a lot of the logistical errors associated with manual hookup, configuration, and discovery. MAAS also makes it very easy to run your racks -- or even your whole datacenter -- remotely. This document explains how MAAS does all this.

The BMC

Machines that MAAS can provision have a baseboard management controller or BMC. A BMC is a separate CPU often included on the motherboard of servers and devices. Its capabilities are limited to managing and monitoring the health of the device. The BMC has sensors for physical characteristics like temperature and power supply voltage, and controls for rebooting or power-cycling tje device. MAAS uses the BMC to turn remote devices on or off, and reboot them at will. A BMC can also monitor BIOS or UEFI, provide a serial console for the devive, and do a few other things with the hardware -- but most of them are uninteresting from a MAAS perspective. For the purposes of this discussion, our maim interest in the BMC is the ability to power cycle and reboot machines.

Network discovery

One of the first ways that MAAS cuts down on manual errors is called network discovery. MAAS listens passively to IP traffic on any network it can see. As it does so, it observes other devices receiving and responding to TCP packets, and it captures each device's IP address. MAAS also captures any other identifying information that's available. For example, it also uses mDNS to collect and present the hostname, if available. All of the captured information is summarized in a discovery dashboard.

Enlistment

Administrators can use the dashboard to enlist machines that need to be remotely provisioned. MAAS doesn't do anything with devices that aren't enlisted, but it doesn't forget them.

PXE booting

MAAS depends on connected machines being in network-boot mode. This means the Baseboard Management Controller (BMC) has been set up to listen on the network for a valid PXE packet. When it receives a valid packet, the first thing it does is to boot the machine. Note that the machine won't have an IP address, and won't be able to boot, but it's waiting for one.
