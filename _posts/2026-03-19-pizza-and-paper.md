I’m a guy who’s had a Unix system in hand since 1974, who’s built his whole life philosophy around simple truth and plain speaking.  Been using emacs since I first discovered it in the eighties.  Today, I’m voluntarily picking up a big honkin’ moleskin and a pocket notebook and making them part of my repertoire.  I want to explain why that’s actually not a regression, but actually an extension of the Unix philosophy. 

My personal principles, which include “use what you have” and “hack” and “distrust all claims for the one true way” really support that decision.  Use the tool that fits the job, not the tool that fits the *implied ideology*.  

Computers were supposed to make our lives faster.  What they’ve actually done is made them broader, which improves the immediacy of networking, but doesn’t necessarily make things easier in every situation.  You can learn to thumb-type very fast, and you can practice the compact dialect of IRC-speak (“chix strips 4 u?”), but you can still probably write it faster, unless that skill has atrophied over the years.  

Nobody who stands at a retail counter waiting on the computers to catch up would be too evangelistic about the faster part. The authorization process becomes more convoluted at regular intervals, and it’s really annoying to have to follow different directions from the little POS terminal (POS in both senses, TBH). Some want the card out before the auth; some hold the card until a receipt is printed; it’s unpredictable.  It’s like everybody has an opinion about the “one true way” to pay, and we’re the ones suffering debit-card whiplash as a result.

## Paper is harder to fantasize about

It started with my budget.  I have a fixed number of months to get a business going to maintain our lifestyle in retirement, or I’ll need to go back to work. Aside from the obvious re-entry barrier for a late sixties person, I’m very much enjoying my freedom, so I want to do just enough to maintain our lifestyle.  Not buying lottery tickets, or letting AI invest my twenty bucks, or surfing the “one weird trick” scammers.  Just good, old-fashioned, here’s-something-you-can-buy for not much money that helps us both. The rare and valuable and not too expensive route.

The paper money tracker helped.  First, my actual numbers weren’t online in a way that someone could figure out my actual budget.  I mean, bank balances are out there, and there’s not much you can do about that now.  But the spending profile, the projections, the run rate is all private now, and that feels very good.  It’s also a physical object.  Somehow the bitwise spreadsheets feel more ephemeral, so I keep re-computing them in my head.  Silly, but a very natural thing to do.  Once you put it on paper, it becomes something else: more solid, a page-flip away; easier to push back on panic and doubt.  

Also my vital signs.  I’m in relatively good health for my age, if I could just get in the habit of avoiding the modern diet.  Processed foods do not help you get healthier and probably send you the other way.  It’s important to find your key numbers: heart rate, blood glucose/A1c, blood pressure, ketones, whatever.  Once you find the kingpin vital, you need to watch it and do things that drive it in the right direction, the fewer meds the better.  In the last couple of years, I’ve gotten off of so many meds it’s insane.  If my blood sugar is high, I know how to bring it down 100 points or more in thirty minutes with a brisk walk and a tall glass of water.  That’s plain text finesse, and that’s what I want.

It’s easy to fantasize about numbers in a glucometer program that aren’t tabulated, that instead give you somebody’s opinion about how you’re doing.  Put those numbers in a makeshift table in a moleskine notebook, and you’ll find yourself adjusting much faster, and having a less difficult time saying “no” to heavy carbs and sweets.  It seems counterintuitive, but I can tell you it’s true.

## The notebook is a Unix pipe

It sounds completely bonkers at first, but paper is the ultimate Unix tool.  No dependencies, no sync issues, no lock files, and certainly no social media distractions when you open the cover.  The moleskine doesn't have a feed algorithm.  Your pocket notebook doesn’t need xterm-mouse-mode. Your arguments appear in your own voice, written by your own hand.

I think that proof-of-ownership might be what makes it sell better.  When the notebook is open and I’m scanning it, my subconscious is adding weight because *I’m the one who wrote this*.  It’s not diluted or filtered to look like everything else in the interface.  There’s no fancy font that only kinda looks like something I typed in, no weird emojis or colors or other embellishments that create inappropriate spin.  It’s just the output of my pen in my hand, scrappy and sloppy as that may be.

In a way, that scrappiness is like confidence.  This isn’t something that an algorithm or an app filtered and cleaned.  It’s just my mind to my paper, to misquote the Vulcan mind meld. I like that, but more importantly, I find it to give me more solid resolve to be who I am, and not the revenue-generating product that someone else wants me to be.

We can have the argument about using Unix at the terminal, but even that has a limited number of filters, a fixed line-length, and capabilities that are limited in some ways.  For me, it’s best to escape that as I can.  Yes, I know emacs will do a lot of things; after all, it’s an operating system desperately searching for a decent text editor, as we all know.  But even that venerable bastion of open source has its quirky ways that are not mine, and when I immerse myself, I start to carelessly make emacs my one true way.  Not great.

## Just pizza[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qwem!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa7745ce1-d521-4d67-8885-f8068265eba0_1024x1024.heic)

My moral principle about religion is drawn from Unix: “Distrust all claims for the one true way; except for pizza, pizza is always the answer.”  Note that I didn’t say my moral principle about *faith*. Having faith is a good thing, and you have to choose some of the objects of your faith.  I mean, it’s good to have faith in gravity, since it’s going to be there anyway, and you can see gravity as a divine promise or a quirk of physics — that’s up to you, and I don’t stick my nose in that unless you ask me (my prime directive principle).

Religion is a different story.  I know great evangelistic preachers who decry religion in almost every sermon, urging you to not let someone else tell you want matters, and encouraging you to find that for yourself.  “If God is what he says he is, you don’t need me to prove it do you,” is the mantra of people of faith that I respect. I’m still a little iffy about the AA method of making, say, an ink pen your god.  What happens when it runs out of ink, for example?

Don’t crowdsource what you believe. Pick things that make you feel comfortable and confident.  For me, pizza happens to be one of those things.  If you want to know where else I place my faith, you’ll have to get to know me first, and then ask me.  And yes, I’m now choosing Caulipower pizza because bread is the supervillain in my health comic.  Distrust all claims for the one true way, even this one.

After all, I don’t treat my own credo as gospel, but more as a diagnostic tool.  And lately, it’s diagnosing that I need a pocket notebook and a paper journal to keep some things personal, simple, and private.  You gotta find your own angle on authentic living, and they might include a piece of typing paper and a number 2, or an SSH to the supercomputer at MIT.  The choice is yours; I’m just saying that you need to consciously make that choice. Just saying.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
