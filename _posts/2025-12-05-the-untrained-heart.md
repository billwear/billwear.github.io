Ever feel like your inner world is running its own physics experiment?

No notice. No warning. Just the sudden realization that your life has become a three-body problem. The math won’t settle. Nobody can solve it in general. You’re living in the particular.

Corporations pretend the universe runs on linear progress that magically turns into exponential growth. Say it out loud and the futility becomes obvious. Reality runs on ten minutes of controlled chaos: a flicker of clarity, a chain of tiny improvements, stealth progress that only looks random from the outside.

If you watch long enough, you start to see it.

There is such a thing as a stable pattern of unstable orbits.

Never the same. Never fully predictable. But open to influence.

## From me

Right now I’ve got three forces in motion: the maker who’s waking up, the environment that no longer fits but keeps talking to me, and the freedom I stepped into without instructions.

None of them want to move politely. None of them want to take turns. They pull in different directions, smashing old assumptions but leaving new paths in the gaps.

I’m not lost. I’m in the settling period — the strange interval where a new system is figuring out how to hold itself together.

These last two weeks helped. They reminded me that capability comes in bursts, not marathons. Ten minutes done with intention is stronger than a whole day done reluctantly. Stack enough of those bursts and “exponential growth” stops being a slide-deck fantasy and starts being the normal curve of your actual life.

The dust devils in my mental weather are still there, but I recognize them now. I don’t mistake them for storms. They can spin themselves out without dragging me with them.

Around me, the quests are forming: sewing, rearranging, writing. They aren’t competing. They’re interacting. They’re teaching me how to navigate this three-body dance without demanding symmetry or predictability. The system doesn’t need to be solved; it needs to be inhabited.

## For you

This works even if you still have a day job. You can build your own Lagrange point — a place where the pull of past, present, and future balances just enough for you to stand there and do real work. Enough chaos to allow surprise. Enough stability that you don’t live in fear.

But you can’t keep pretending you’re in complete control.

Those nagging ideas? Don’t slam the door on them, but don’t immediately obey either. Let them circle you. Let them gather mass. When one of them refuses to leave and keeps tugging at both your logic and your gut, that’s your sign. That’s the orbit that matters.

The untrained heart has its own little solar system: the solid home planet of reason, the bright, fast-moving moon of creativity, and then you — the observer and participant, pulled by both. The result is a system that can only be lived, not fully predicted.

You can try to outthink it, move by move.

You can let the orbits run wild and hope for the best.

Or you can start looking for the stable spots that emerge on their own — the Lagrange points, where something obviously wants doing.

Those points show up as clear overlap between logic and emotion. For me, one of them was embarrassingly simple: my work desk was hardly used anymore (I have Emacs on my phone), but it took days for my head and my gut to agree on what that meant.

Last night, they finally did.

“Move the work desk into the corner. Put the sewing table in front of the window.”

That’s it. A tiny move. But it came from a place where thought and feeling lined up, where the orbit felt right. That’s what you’re listening for.

Start watching for those overlaps in your own life. They won’t announce themselves with trumpets. They’ll feel like small, obvious truths that keep returning until you act on them.

Heed them when they show up.

***Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.***
