The world likes clean borders and dichotomous, binary thinking: Awake or asleep; rational or creative; working or dreaming. Just pick a side and stay there.

But some minds cannot work that way. They are stuck in the portal, permanently holding it open. This is about one of those minds, and what happens when you finally give up looking for the door.

We are all taught early to be fully awake. Distrust that morning fog and use psychoactive drugs (caffeine, nicotine) to hasten the clearing. Move quickly through the half-lit places and get to something solid, or bring a flashlight. We are taught to reject the moments when images arrive before words, when emotion forms sound before meaning, or when ideas surface already assembled.

But what if that open portal is not a defect, but a gift? Timothy Leary and at least a couple of the Beatles pioneered the use of LSD to get to “transcendent states.” Drug-induced hypnagogic states aren’t reliable or fully usable, though. It’s like going into a TARDIS-style closet, with no idea where you’ll walk out or be suddenly flashed back in.

But what if you lived there from birth and learned how to function normally in an altered state? I think I can tell you. I’m realizing that’s actually how I live.

Hear me out.

---

I walk most days with one foot on either side of that line between waking and dreaming. The hypnagogic state most people brush once at night is my default mode. 

Thoughts do not arrive step by step. They appear spatially. When I want to, I can hear colors (blue is a beautiful, clear A chord, in case you were wondering). I can taste and smell weather, hours ahead; bad storms coming make me dizzy and nauseous, hence my handle, “stormrider.” Sounds cause immediate physical reactions; death metal causes severe pain, but Pink Floyd’s “Dark Side of the Moon” album will slowly cure my migraines. 

On the inside, conversations with my own mind happen a few steps ahead of conscious thought, and I catch up by listening. I’ve tried a few times to catch the train, but the spectrum is too dense. The streaming output of my subconscious carries so many sidebands that it’s impossible to read directly. I have to let it drop across my nervous system and sort itself into the various parts of my brain.

I have wondered whether this was trained, or simply given, but recently finding my mother’s journals in an old suitcase of my grandmother’s gave me a clue. This drove me to a crash course in neurology, which leads me to suspect that I was born with a very liberal prefrontal cortex and a single network serving default mode, executive control, and salience. And a very lazy thalamus. Or we can dispense with the scientific jargon and just call it what it is: LSD mind without the LSD.

Except that I trained on it, instead of being thrown into it. In Star Trek: The Next Generation, Geordi LaForge, blind since birth, wears a visor to see. In one episode, he connects his visor to a monitor so everyone can see through his eyes. No one else can make sense of his view, but it’s quite natural for him, and more precise than what normal humans can muster. 

Emotion sometimes comes out of me as well-formed nonsense syllables, grammatical but untranslated. I can inhabit a role so completely that people forget it is a role. Music lives in me photographically; a minor segue from an old radio play can surface decades later intact. I read people and rooms the way others read text. Space is never abstract: organic chemistry, circuitry, sewing patterns, equations in motion, projects not yet begun all appear fully dimensional. Anything can be translated to anything else with zero effort and little error.

This permeability does not stop with ideas, by the way.

---

Animals respond to stillness long before they respond to intention. I have learned that if I place my hand near a recently-exposed hornet’s nest without urgency or threat, the hive will climb onto me and allow itself to be moved somewhere safer. Not because I control them, but because nothing in me signals danger. Sometime I still get stung a bit, but that’s an acceptable trade-off for saving the hive.

I have sat by campfires while bobcats pressed close, accepting touch the way they might accept warmth. Trust me, I was still nervous, but I knew how to mask it. I notice the heaviness in a tree before I prune its lower limbs, and I take the time to do it gently, as if loss could be eased by respect.

When I’m hungry, I can smell the food I’d like to have as if it were cooking on the stove. This particular synesthesia, sadly, is calibrated by a terrible childhood diet, and I’m only now teaching it to program better choices. But all the other synesthesias are fully active:
- 

Grapheme–color synesthesia – letters or numbers have intrinsic colors.
- 

Lexical–gustatory synesthesia – words evoke tastes.
- 

Spatial–sequence synesthesia – numbers, days, and months exist in fixed spatial layouts that I can see like little boxes in my brain, and they all have their own colors.
- 

Mirror–touch synesthesia – feeling sensations you see happening to others.

There are many more of these (Google is your friend). These moments do not feel mystical, supernatural, spiritual, new-age, or extraterrestrial in any way. They feel relational and pedestian. I just didn’t realize until recently that not everyone thinks this way.

Even grief lives differently on the threshold. When my little sister died after two years of illness, I watched my parents break open with sorrow. I did not feel empty. I felt complete. Those two years had been spent in quiet bonding, and somewhere below language I knew her spirit was not gone, only resting. That knowing did not cancel grief, but it made mourning feel natural and right.

This is also why mind maps work for me in a way linear notes never could.

---

A mind map is not organization, but instrumentation. It is fighter-jet radar for a threshold mind. When ideas arrive simultaneously, from multiple vectors, a list collapses them into false sequence. A map lets them stay airborne. Relationships light up. Gaps announce themselves. Patterns in data reveal themselves the way trajectories do, not as numbers but as motion.

I do not use mind maps to think. I use them to see.

Direction, on the other hand, rarely sticks. North and south feel arbitrary when the world presents itself as relational rather than absolute. I navigate by landmarks, by structure, by internal geometry, not by compass points. The map matters more than the arrow. North is just a figment. “Turn left,” makes no intrinsic sense.

And don’t get me started on sleeping. I have quite literally spent entire nights fully awake and fully asleep at the same time, and gotten up well-rested. And there was one night in my thirties when I tried transcendental meditation as I was falling asleep. I dreamed continuously for about four hours and woke up refreshed, but I couldn’t quite shake the dream.  I was doing the dream and reality all day, fully functional and without getting confused between the imagined and the concrete. It’s iumpossible to describe, but I won’t be doing that again.

In fact, that one event sent me down a self-help rathole that lasted nearly a decade.

---

For seven years, I tried to shut that door. I tried to separate engineer from artist, reason from intuition, waking from dream. I tried every charlatan in the bookstore. I thought discipline meant narrowing, re-educating, affirming, getting in touch with my shakras, transcending to the next level…. All that did was distort the signal and cost me years of distraction. Trust me when I tell you to choose your gurus wisely.

The truth is simpler. My best thinking happens when the dreaming mind speaks first and the rational mind translates second. When the connection is honored instead of policed. When I stop trying to close a door that, physically, doesn’t actually exist in my mind. Maybe it’s a birth defect. Maybe I’m an red-headed extraterrestrial stepchild, put out of the flying saucer on a rural planet like one too many kittens. Maybe I’m the only X-man who escaped from the comic book. I don’t have a good explanation. 

I’ve just come to accept that this is how I live and work.

---

This weekend, I saw it clearly at the sewing table. A muslin that was ugly, stubborn, and poorly designed told me everything at once. I had faithfully built it from a pattern, but every flaw announced itself before the seam was finished. Not frustration, only recognition. And a note to self to always treat a purchased pattern as more of an untested hypothesis.

*Recognition*. That quiet moment when you realize you have crossed from amateur to professional, not because the work is perfect, but because the diagnosis is instant and the stream of kaizen is irresistible. You see what is right and what is wrong (although in my mind, right is red and wrong is a weird shade of purple-green, if you can imagine that).

That is threshold work: Seeing before finishing; knowing before understanding. The true meaning of the word “grok.” Zeitgeist as a way of perceiving and navigating the world. Some people sharpen by narrowing their focus. I sharpen by listening at the doorway, but as you’ve read, I have no other choice.

The world will always reward speed, certainty, and clean edges. Discipline, loyalty, logic, and a well-maintained weapon. But there is a different kind of power that lives in the upside-down. It does not shout. It waits. And if you stop trying to seal it off, it will carry you further than anything else ever could.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
