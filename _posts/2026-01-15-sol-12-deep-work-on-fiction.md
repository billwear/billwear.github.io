Deep work feels different from the inside: quiet, slower, almost boring at first.  But if you stay, things that have resisted you forever suddenly clarify and stop pushing back.

Today was one of those days.

I woke early, as usual. Logged only a couple of lines, but stayed awake anyway. No heroic morning ritual or prepared outline. Just a long, protected window centered on noon, and a firm decision to stay inside it; not to produce, but to work.

What surprised me wasn’t how much I wrote. It was what finally *gave way*.

## Sci-fi novel

I’m drafting a novel right now, science fiction apparently. That still makes me smile. Alongside the drafting, I’ve been deliberately learning one hard thing: *show, don’t tell*.

Not by reading about it or rewriting old pages, but by writing forward and noticing where explanation tried to sneak back in. Deep work made that possible.

In shallow time, I would have:
- 

polished sentences
- 

fixed pronouns
- 

optimized paragraphs
- 

revised the same scene again

In deep time, I did something harder. I let scenes run long enough to find their shape. I resisted revising early, even when I could feel the craft issues. I trusted behavior over explanation. I held ambiguity without resolving it.

Those are not small skills. They don’t gel quickly. They require staying put while your instincts argue with you. And today, at least some of them stopped arguing.

## Let characters be characters

Here’s what fell, specifically:
- 

The urge to explain emotion instead of letting characters act.
- 

The need to know exactly what a scene “was” before it finished becoming itself.
- 

The impulse to fix problems that only exist because the story isn’t far enough along yet.
- 

The anxiety around structure — replaced by confidence that structure emerges if you keep moving.
- 

The fear that learning craft and producing work are competing goals.

I learned *show, don’t tell* by catching myself mid-sentence and choosing not to explain. I learned pacing by stopping scenes when something changed, not when they felt “done.” I learned structure by writing forward and realizing, afterward, that the beats were already there.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!AYF2!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3df712e3-83b2-4aef-aff2-945789900af3_784x1182.jpeg)

The big lesson was not to draw conclusions for the reader.  You want them to experience the situation through the character’s eyes, not hear you narrate it.  It’s not easy, even once you learn to watch out for it, but it’s worth the effort.

No fireworks. Just fewer things fighting me at once.

---

Last night I wrote about the deep work circuit — slow accumulation, delayed payoff, heat that’s still there in the morning. Today was proof. The circuit isn’t about discipline for its own sake. It’s about creating conditions where **hard things can finally complete themselves**.

Deep work didn’t make writing easier today. It made the *right* difficulties show up, in ways that couldn’t be ignored, and then soften under scrutiny and practice. That’s the signal I trust now.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
