There comes a point when you realize no one’s coming to save you. No boss with a plan, no family member with a lifeline. The next chapter won’t be handed to you — you have to take it. Write it with shaking hands if you have to.

You didn’t plan to be here. You thought life would slow down gently, not come apart at the seams. But now it feels like being pushed out of something that once felt steady — work, identity, purpose. The world starts calling you “retired” like it’s a finish line. But you don’t feel finished. Just winded.

The anger sits there, quiet but alive. Not rage, exactly — more like a steady pulse that keeps you awake at three in the morning. A voice that says, *how dare they decide when you’re done?* You’ve given years — decades — of time, of skill, of heart. You’ve earned the right to your own damn ending.

So you lie there, listening to the noise — the house, the memories, the restless thoughts — and you wonder if you’ve already spent your last burst of energy. Then, without warning, something flickers. A spark. A whisper that says: *get up.*

So you do.

You tell yourself: damn the torpedoes. Straight ahead. Flank speed.

Maybe there’s no paycheck waiting. Maybe no one’s clapping. But you move anyway. Because forward is the only direction that still feels like life.

You make something small — a plan, a paragraph, a start. You move even when you’re scared. Especially when you’re scared.

The seas won’t calm just because you want them to. You steer through with what you’ve got — tired hands, stubborn heart, half a map, full faith.

And if you crash, at least you’ll crash moving.

Because momentum is life. And no one gets to call the end of your story but you.

Damn the torpedoes. Straight ahead. Flank speed anyway.
