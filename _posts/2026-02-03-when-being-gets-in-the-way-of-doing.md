title: "When 'being' gets in the way of 'doing'"

Imagine trying to get started on a daunting new project. Success will create much better odds that your lifestyle will improve. Failure will all but guarantee that your mode of living will degrade. It’s not “life or death” from a personal perspective — there’s space and time to make things work out — so desperate measures aren’t required.

When you think about starting, though, there are all sorts of invisible obstacles that get in the way. You want to do the project, but you know, deep down, that you’re not actually doing what’s needed to move it forward.

For one thing, you’re suddenly not in the mood. You’re gun-shy and afraid of mistakes; hungry, but afraid to eat. Convinced the little lingering pain at the bottom of your left Eustachian tube must be the flu. Besides, you’re tired of hearing the heater run for three days straight. Maybe a nap will help.

Eventually, you push past that, but then you notice how many competing obligations stand in your way. People who matter in your life have schedules and needs that require you. After all, shouldn’t they be first in your life? But is it spouse, then kids, then parents, or some other priority scheme?

Finally, in spite of your protestations, you end up with some uninterrupted time to work. Great time to read that basic tutorial about the project you’re considering. Oh, and you’ll need to order some tools and parts, and drive to town for a day to get more books and supplies. You’re certain that once you do this, you’ll have what you need.

At some point, you have to consider yourself prepared because you simply don’t have any more room to store anything else. That’s when you switch to detailed planning. And if you do get a plan together, it’s clear that it needs modified tools to pull it off, especially if you work with software or anything else that can be rewritten or programmed.

Then one day you look up, and the deadline’s tomorrow, and you decide to try and pull an all-nighter to make some kind of showing that will allow you to convincingly ask for more time.

We talk about this procrastination as if it’s overcome with better discipline. Make your bed first thing in the morning. Set a schedule and keep it. Power through; after all, determination wins the day.

In reality, the problem is more fundamental — and easier to solve — than anything in the spin cycle above could lead you to believe. Sometimes, when you’re not finding the answer, the problem isn’t you, but the fact that you’re asking the wrong question altogether.

Think about all these situations I’ve mentioned above. Every one of them is based on the idea of pre-existing competence, driven by identity issues. Specifically, we misunderstand personal identity altogether.

Identity is not who you are. It’s the mental model that answers the question, “Who am I?” [](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!wxl4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffdbf8a07-32da-495a-aae4-94a29d3a7bea_910x1024.heic)

When you set identity-based goals (“I want to be a millionaire by age 30”), you’re creating a mental model. You’ll try very hard to fit into what *you* think that mental model means, but you don’t actually know.

Your identity has nothing to do with your goals. Changing your identity doesn’t endow you with skill. You can call yourself a firefighter, watch instructional videos, and hang out at the fire station every night, but that doesn’t mean you can suit up and run into burning buildings.

Belief in identity as the pivot point is so common that it’s virtually become invisible, the way that marionette wires disappear into the background. It’s one of the default assumptions of modern life, and the main assumption holding up social media. Most people operate under this hidden rule: **Be → then do → then maybe have.**

Get the label, badge, degree, certification, training, or title, and you’re done. The concept is built into our schooling, our work, our self-help streams, and even our religion. Sadly, identity doesn’t work that way; not once in the history of mankind has identity conferred anything other than an ID card.

Identity is only a model, not a reality. To break that spell, stop asking what you should be; start finding out what you can do, and how you can get better at it. People who do this aren’t superhuman, but they think differently. They’re focused on building their capabilities until they can consistently produce rare and valuable results.

Once you shift from an identity focus to a capability focus, the question stops being

“What should I be?” and becomes instead, “What small, real action can I take right now to make this a little better?” That’s the question that breaks the spell.

For example, the fundamental principle of medicine as a practice is built on this idea: “First, do no harm.” That principle *is* the key to the whole mindset shift. You stand a significant chance of gaining mastery just by constantly asking yourself, “How can I *not* make this worse?”

Burn slow. Build strong. *Be *the proof.
