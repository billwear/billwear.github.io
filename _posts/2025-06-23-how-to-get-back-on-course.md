Every long-haul flight is off-course the majority of the time.  It’s not because the pilot’s careless., or the plane is broken, but because flight is **dynamic**.

Wind shifts; currents rise; clouds obscure the path. Still, planes generally land exactly at their intended destination. The secret isn’t staying perfectly on course, the secret is *correction*.

Gentle, consistent, quiet correction.

---

Some days, your internal instruments start blinking as soon as you wake up. You feel off, sluggish, fogged in. You’re not where you meant to be, and not in a useful mental and emotional state.

We’ve all been there.  Here’s how to realign mid-flight, especially on heavy Mondays or in post-spiral dips.

## 1. Admit it

Okay, first, just admit it to yourself that your spiral has lost momentum. The fire’s dimmed and the autopilot seems to have gone rogue.

Guilt? Regret? Whatever. Look past all that and admit that what you *really *need is just a **course correction **— not a push, just a re-anchoring.

---

## **2. Sit and breathe for five minutes**

You don’t need a meditation mat. Just sit on the edge of your bed, a chair, or even the floor. Let your hands rest and breathe slowly and fully. Say this to yourself like a gentle liturgy:

> 

*I forgive myself for the drift.  
Yes, I chose to wander, and yes, it’s getting uncomfortable,  
but today, I choose clarity.  
I choose ease.  
I choose small, sacred action.*

This is your moment to check your instruments before re-engaging the throttle.

---

## **3. Ground your body**

Stretch. Roll your shoulders. Shake out your arms. Touch the ground. Literally, if you can: Step outside if weather allows, and let the breeze remind you that you're still airborne and alive and moving forward. You're *not stuck*, just off *your* intended heading.

---

## **4. Sip something kind for as long as you can**

Make a drink that signals **care**, not command: tea, warm water, black coffee, broth — something gentle. Drink it with both hands. No screens. No rushing. Let this be your first real act of reentry.

---

## **5. Write down *****only***** three things**

These are not obligations, just personal *VFR *beacons: radio signals that put you on an established and safe flight path. Write down *three truths or intentions* that could guide you back today.  For example?
- 

I want to feel lighter by evening.
- 

I want to complete one thing I’ve been avoiding.
- 

I want to end the day feeling a little more like myself.

Put this list somewhere where it will be visible all day. Let it steer you without pressure.

---

### **6. Bonus: The 7-minute tidy-up**

Pick the space where you’ll spend most your time today.  It might be at the office; don’t forget that commuting is a great place to just focus carefully and mindfully on your surroundings, so bring some of that forgiving, warm drink.

When you can get to your cockpit:
- 

Set a timer.
- 

Clear it. 
- 

Reset it. 
- 

Post the little sign with the three things from Step 5.
- 

Breathe again.

The cleaner the space, the clearer the trajectory.  It matters

---

## You’re still flying

You didn’t fall, you drifted, and like every good pilot, you noticed. The spiral will pick up again. The fire still glows in your core. You are in the sky, adjusting, and if  you keep adjusting, you’ll still land exactly where you meant to.
