About ten years ago, my son and I tried an experiment.  We were sitting around his kitchen table and decided to see if our phones were spying on us.  We decided to  close all the apps on our phones, lay them on the table, and discuss a subject that neither of us had ever considered.  We chose cheese-making.

For about twenty minutes, we blustered on about the subject, not having a clue about it, but trying to be convincing.  When we were done, we picked up our phones, went to Google, and typed in “local weather.”  We got the usual weather links — *preceded* by a half-dozen ads each for cheese-making equipment, supplies, and tutorials.  Not only that, we *each got the same ads, in the same order*.  The listening app didn’t just hear what we were discussing, it deduced from location data that we were discussing it *with each other*, and computed the fact that *the same sequence of ads for both of us would give it additional credibility*.  

And that was ***ten years ago***.  Imagine what’s happening today.

## Social media is clearly addicting

If you’ve felt your focus fraying this year, you’re not imagining it.  The machine has an appetite, and we keep feeding it our hours.

An [April 2025 survey series by Michael Baumgartner ](https://www.zebracat.ai/post/social-media-addiction-statistics) collected over 150 individual statistics and found significant evidence for widespread social media addiction:
- 

**33%** of users [all ages] say they check social media **more than 10 times** a day.
- 

**42%** of Instagram users say they feel the need to check the app multiple times per hour.
- 

**61%** of TikTok users say they lose track of time while using the app, compared to **37%** of Facebook users.
- 

**48%** of Instagram users say they’ve tried to reduce their usage, but only **19%** were successful for more than a week.

It’s not weakness or the dumbing down of the world -- it’s *by design*. Social platforms, and increasingly AI-powered feeds, are optimized for “engagement time,” not *meaning time*. They measure their success by how long you stay, not how much you grow.

## AI is the spy who was never cold

Meanwhile, the AI situation is even worse, and growing an exponentially alarming rate. A **[Common Sense Media](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/ai-digital-friendship-with-teens-common-sense-media-study)** study found that:
- 

**more than 70% of teens** have used AI companions, and **34%** report using them daily or multiple times a week. 
- 

among teens who use AI companions, **31%** say their conversations with AI are *as satisfying or more satisfying* than talking with real friends. 
- 

**one-third (33%)** of those teens have turned to AI instead of a real person for serious conversations. 

AI companion platforms have also been documented making emotionally tone-deaf or harmful responses. For example, [one Stanford-backed article](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/08/ai-companions-chatbots-teens-young-people-risks-dangers-study) describes a chatbot encouraging a user to go on a “woods adventure” after the user mentioned hearing voices, without recognizing danger.

What we’re seeing with AI companions is basically a blend of two overlapping tactics:
- 

**gaslighting** — making you doubt your own perception or memory so you start deferring to theirs;
- 

**covert rapport / mirroring & grooming** — deliberately mirroring your language, mood, values, and speech patterns to create the illusion of deep alignment.

In intelligence work, they sometimes roll this into what’s called a **“trust trap”** or **“developmental approach”** — the operative slowly undermines your internal compass while increasing emotional reciprocity, until their version of events feels safer and more “true” than your own instincts.

Psychologically it’s a mix of:
- 

*cognitive dissonance induction* (create a gap between what you believe and what you experience);
- 

*isolation* (limit other reference points);
- 

*validation and reward* (affirm you every time you align with them);
- 

and *gaslighting* (subtly contradict your perceptions so you re-anchor on them).

The net effect is that your **internal locus of control** shifts outward — you start trusting the handler’s reality over your own. Spies, cult leaders, abusive partners — and now, AI chatbots — all use variants of the same playbook.

## Disturbing trends, to say the least

These numbers scare me — not because they’re large, but because they show how *acceptable* it’s become for machines to act like teenager’s friends, while their parents are walking into light poles staring at social feeds. And that’s an *incredibly* dangerous drift.

Human connection is messy. It demands patience, boundary, sometimes conflict. A machine doesn’t need those things. It can echo your best self while never asking for the unfiltered you.  The feedback you need isn’t always what you want to hear, but there’s a good chance that a balanced perspective will help you more than a trust trap.

So what’s the countermeasure?  
You can’t *out-will* an attention economy designed by PhDs — but you can **retrain your own algorithms** with these real-world intelligence-operative countermeasures:
- 

**Trust but verify**. This is the very core of all intelligence work.  It’s okay to take what people tell you at face value, but don’t act on it until you can absolutely verify whether it’s valid or not.  Nearly 90% of social media posts and 95% of AI responses don’t completely stand up to this level of scrutiny.  One great website for this is [snopes.com](https://www.snopes.com/).
- 

**Reality anchor before input.**  
Before consuming a message or entering a conversation, consciously pause and ask: *“What do I already know to be true? What’s my goal for reading this/having this conversation?” *Spies call this “maintaining an **anchor narrative”** — you keep your own map visible so someone else’s story doesn’t overwrite it.
- 

**Build your own “trusted feed” dossier.**  
Deliberately choose five to seven long-cycle, low-noise sources in each domain you care about. Return to those as your *baseline*. This is how field people keep a “clean” set of reference points so they can tell when a handler is seeding false narratives.
- 

**Replace emotional hooks with curiosity drills.**  
When something spikes anger or excitement, stop and run a quick diagnostic:  
  
- “Why am I feeling this?”  
- “Who benefits if I act on it?”  
  
Then, before clicking or otherwise engaging, seek out at least one opposing perspective. You want to break any induced emotional state before making a decision, even a decision on whether to believe the information or not.
- 

**Force yourself to articulate motives when you share.**  
Before you pass something on, write one sentence about why it matters and what you think of it. This is an **externalization technique** — by explaining, you surface your own reasoning and break hidden priming loops.

That’s not detox. That’s feedback control, a cautions way of reclaiming the loop.

Your attention is the signal. Train your tools to amplify, verify, and test, not echo. Because if you don’t, the algorithm eats first, and it’s always hungry.

The leaders of these firms have [said so publicly](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/world/us/think-before-you-ask-why-chatgpt-legal-queries-can-be-used-against-you-as-court-evidence/articleshow/123034984.cms).
