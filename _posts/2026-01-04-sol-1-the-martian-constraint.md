I watched *The Martian* again recently, not for the drama, but for the structure.

What struck me this time wasn’t the crisis. It was the method. One person, limited resources, a fixed environment, and a simple rule: **log reality, solve the next problem, repeat**.

I realized I could put myself under a version of those same constraints.

Not because I’m in danger, but because I’m on a limited budget, and I want to get to the point where I can **ship and sell real things**: things I sew and simple circuits I build. Quickly enough to matter, carefully enough to last.

So I’m doing an experiment. I’m giving myself **60 days**. Not because the number is magical, but because it’s long enough to expose self-deception, and short enough to prevent drift.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JJ_U!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4ea434ae-4593-4f01-95a0-78e3563d301e_1024x1536.heic)

Here’s the setup:
- 

I need to learn sewing well enough to produce **professional, sellable tote bags** that are *well articulated*: pockets, structure, load distribution, possibly embedded electronics, and thoughtful features that make it easy to carry a lot **within the weight limit**, without hunting for things or dumping everything into one loose compartment.
- 

I need to learn electronics well enough to build **simple, useful circuits people can actually use right now**: things that belong on a desk, in a kitchen, or by a bed. Practical devices with clear purpose, not demos or novelty builds.
- 

I need to document the process publicly **as a real, unfolding story**: written clearly, honestly, and well enough that people want to keep reading—not because it’s optimized or motivational, but because it’s grounded, precise, and shows what actually happens when someone tries to learn and build under constraint.
- 

And I need to write clearly enough to use the tool I already have — **Scrivener** — to produce a **complete draft of a book by the end of 60 days**, something real I can revise, refine, and possibly attempt to publish afterward.

I’ll also be doing the ordinary work of living. Cleaning. Maintenance. Admin. No retreat. No monk mode. Just real days.

The rules are simple:
- 

One integrated system. No app sprawl.
- 

Daily logs. No skipping bad days.
- 

Build first, write second.
- 

No premature lessons. Only evidence.

I’m assuming 60 days is enough. Not because I’m certain it is.  
But because assuming it *isn’t* would be an excuse to go soft.

If I fall short, there may be space afterward. But I’m not planning on that.

Each day, I’ll publish a short mission log: what I attempted, what failed, what worked, and what tomorrow requires. Calm. Factual. No theatrics.

This isn’t about hustle. It’s about whether a person can still design a learning system that holds under constraint.

**Sol 1 objective:**  
Set up the system. Prepare the first build. Identify the first real failure point.

At first, I thought that failure point would be time. I was wrong. Before I cut fabric or touched a circuit, I discovered a more basic problem. One that, if I don’t solve it early, will quietly undermine everything that follows.

Tomorrow, I find out whether that problem is structural — or whether it’s me.
