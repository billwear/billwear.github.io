Getting Things Done (GTD) is a powerhouse for clearing mental clutter. It excels at giving you a trusted system—a way to empty your mind, so you don't have to keep everything spinning in your head. The inbox, the Someday/Maybe list, the tickler file—these are brilliant tools for capturing potential energy.

But here's the rub: GTD helps you know what you *can* do, but it punts on the deeper question of what you *should* do next. Its prioritization model—Context, Time, Energy, Priority (CTEP)—feels more like a filter than a compass. It tells you what you're capable of doing right now, not what actually matters most.

This leads to decision-making that can be out of step with your values or consequences. You might fix a dripping sink because it's convenient, even though your grandmother is dying in the hospital. The model over-indexes on what's controllable and present, instead of what carries real-world weight.

So I started layering my own solution on top of GTD.

---

**The Origins of "Better To"**

"Better To" didn’t start as a productivity method. It started as a personal philosophy—a way to resolve decision fatigue and moral confusion when everything seemed equally urgent, or equally meaningless. I noticed that when I was overwhelmed, I could almost always resolve indecision by asking myself, "What's better to do, right now?"

Not "what's perfect," not "what's ideal," but *better.*

At first, this was just a mindfulness tool. If I was caught between rest and work, between helping someone and protecting my boundaries, between fear and action—I could just compare two options:

> 

Better to text my friend back, or rest for five minutes?  
Better to clean the kitchen, or fix that thing on the car?

It was never about optimizing life. It was about freeing myself from the emotional fog of too many choices. And it worked—every single time.

Then one day, I found myself doing a GTD inbox sweep at home—collecting everything that was pressing, lingering, undone. And I thought, what if I used "Better To" to triage the pile?

Suddenly, two tools snapped together in my mind.

---

**The Collision of GTD and Better To**

GTD gave me a trusted inventory of everything on my plate.  
Better To gave me the compass to navigate it.

Where GTD asks, *"What can I do now?"*, Better To asks, *"What should I do now, given the consequences?"

And that was the key: **consequences.** Not convenience. Not location. Not available time. But real-world outcomes.

This immediately revealed a critical flaw in the GTD CTEP model: it favors action based on what’s easy to do in the moment, rather than what matters most to your life. It makes your choices feel opportunistic, not strategic.

Take a typical GTD decision:
- 

You’re at home.
- 

You have 15 minutes.
- 

You’re a little tired.
- 

You see a task: "Tighten leaking sink."

GTD says: Perfect. You have the tools, the time, the context. Do it.

But Better To says: Wait. Isn't your grandmother dying in the hospital? Isn’t it *better* to call her, or pack and leave now?

This is where the CTEP system breaks down. It makes what you *can* do the driving factor. Better To restores the weight of what you *should* do.

---

**Refining Priority With Consequences**

I realized that my intuition was already evaluating tasks based on their **consequence profile**:
- 

Will someone suffer?
- 

Will a system break down?
- 

Will this affect my job, income, health, or relationships?

So I started assigning an A/B/C structure based on the level of consequence:
- 

**A**: Major consequences if not addressed soon (health, relationships, finances)
- 

**B**: Noticeable impact, but not catastrophic (delays, frustrations, minor financial or emotional cost)
- 

**C**: No real consequence, just low-level mess or friction

I layered a 1–3 severity scale inside each letter to reflect urgency or pressure:
- 

A1: Immediate and severe
- 

A3: Serious, but with some buffer
- 

C1: Low consequence, but useful
- 

C3: Entirely optional

Now I could walk my GTD inbox as a decision tree. And more importantly, I could choose not just *what to do*, but what would strategically *buy me time* by reducing the severity of a project.

---

**Redefining Next Actions: Pressure Relief First**

Here's the real unlock: not every Next Action needs to push a project forward. Some actions serve best as **mitigators**. They reduce the tension, shrink the risk, or buy you breathing room.

Here’s what that looks like:
- 

**Project**: Fix van oil leak (A1)
- 

**Reality**: Leak is slow. Repair cost is too high right now.
- 

**Insight**: I can move this to A2 or A3 by checking oil daily.
- 

**Obstacle**: No oil in the house.
- 

**Next Action**: Buy the right oil (first check the sticker on the windshield)

Suddenly, I’m up, dressed, and walking to the store. Not because it was a high-effort action. But because it was the *correct* next move—the one that defused pressure and let me re-focus.

This kind of logic brings a deep sense of peace. Not just clarity of thought, but **clarity of purpose.**

---

**Mindfulness as Tactical Awareness**

Better To + GTD becomes a living mindfulness practice. Not in a fluffy, incense kind of way. In a hard-nosed, truth-facing, reality-navigating way.
- 

You trust your system (GTD) to hold everything.
- 

You trust your judgment (Better To) to choose wisely.
- 

You practice seeing consequences clearly.
- 

You take action to reduce pressure, not just "get things done."

This is a logic of **relative good**. It’s not perfect. It’s just better. And that’s enough to move.

---

**The Big Picture**

I'm not reinventing GTD. I'm *completing* it.

GTD gives you the tools to gather your life.  
Better To gives you the tools to live it with purpose.

Together, they form a powerful pair:
- 

Inventory and Insight
- 

Capture and Comparison
- 

System and Sense

If GTD is the stage, Better To is the spotlight.  
If GTD is the map, Better To is the compass.

And the journey? That's mine. One better decision at a time.

This practice—this lens—is turning out to be one of the most reliable tools I've ever built for navigating a messy, unpredictable, sometimes overwhelming life.

And I believe it can work for others, too.
