This really feels like one of those days. The cat is lying patiently across my feet, trying to console me. She still believes in me, and that matters.

My head hurts a little. My throat is irritated and my voice is husky. Sleep really didn’t land. The sky can’t decide what it wants to do.

To be honest, neither can I.

On days like this, I tell myself the old story, the story of my life:  Something must be wrong with my direction. Maybe I should change everything.

Sometimes that works; converting to the Mac and an iPhone turned out to be life-changing. The transition made everything in my life much easier, letting me focus on the things that I do best, while purpose-built tools handle the mechanics seamlessly. No more tweaking Emacs lisp half the day to make an amateurish version of bad tool output.

I have listened to that voice long enough to know when it’s misdirecting me. When the pressure drops and doubt turns urgent, and I feel the push for dramatic solutions — a pivot, a new plan, a different identity — I now know that voice is lying.

If something doesn’t show signs of life quickly, I used to assume it never would. Always looking for an Instagram moment. Legendary doesn’t work that way. Great things don’t come to pass like that.

If the seeds don’t bloom immediately, don’t question the soil. For a long time, though, I did. I thought every slow result meant I lacked discipline. Now I think I was listening to bad weather and bad ideas.

Now, I know that my instruments are temporarily unreliable:  Congestion blurs my judgment; sleep debt bends my conclusions. Medication, irritation, and noise amplify the pessimist in me.

That doesn’t mean the work isn’t working. It means the forecast is distorted.

The work isn’t wrong. It *is* working. Yesterday something important happened. I designed a pouch, cleanly, without strain. The design virtually documented itself. Every step had a tool that fit my hand like a custom glove and produced credible output quickly. Results felt easy and obvious. And when I put the work down, it pulled me back.

That wasn’t willpower. That was gravity, the gravity of work well done. The gravity of devotion to excellence.

When work creates its own gravity, pushing is the wrong move. Instead, stay close; orbit; let the field strengthen and pull you in.

*This* is why discipline always felt hollow to me. Discipline is how you move against conditions that you didn’t inherit to do what someone else wants, often to enrich only them. The quiet truth is they don’t want to do that work either. That’s why they want you to do it.

Devotion is different. Devotion is what happens when everything lines up around the work itself. When you can’t resist doing it because it is beautiful, right, and aligned with how you think.

The world prefers discipline because it wants your output for a pittance.  It calls grinding through bad weather a virtue, but the people who praise that grind don’t stand in the storm. They hide under the tarp.

Besides, weather lies.

So today I am resisting the urge to force momentum. I am staying in this bed until my readings make sense again. This isn’t avoidance, it’s calibration. My signal boost hasn’t appeared yet, and that’s fine. This isn’t a broadcast day. I’m tuning and tending frequencies.

Now I know that pivot I’ve been looking for isn’t a change of direction, but a change of listening. Attraction over urgency. Stay with what pulls. Let the storm move through.

And if any of this sounds familiar to you, If your own weather has ever told you the work was wrong — when what was really wrong was the sky and all the people telling you what you that it’s great weather — then maybe today isn’t about fixing your life either.

Maybe it’s about waiting out the front, keeping the flame lit, and not making permanent decisions in under a gale flag.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**

And the cat agrees.
