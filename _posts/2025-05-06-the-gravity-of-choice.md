If the Parabolic Consequences model is the shape of meaning, then clarity is how we steer within it. In a world drowning in options, not choosing is itself a high-consequence choice. Let's consider the emotional and cognitive weight of decision inertia, and how refusing to choose keeps us stuck in the center of the parabola, where everything is low-consequence and low-meaning.

### The false safety of indecision

It's been said that there are only two problems in life:

1. You know what you want, but you don't know how to get it, or

2. You don't know what you want.

While this sounds profound and intuitive, there's actually a spectrum here:

1. You know what you want, but don't know how to get it.

2. You know what you want, but realize that you can't have it — or shouldn't try.

3. You know what you want, but getting it would compromise other things.

4. You think you know what you want, but haven’t looked deeply enough at why you’re chasing it — so you end up with things that don’t meet the need.

5. You don't know what you want, so you stop caring. Or you decide not to decide.

There are probably more variations. Those middle three are moral dilemmas in their own right; we'll come back to them later. But what particularly interests me here is the last one: the choice not to choose. Because not choosing often feels safe, when it's usually just drift in disguise. In the absence of clarity, drift becomes destiny. Our modern, phone-centric culture makes it ever easier to commit to drift and avoid substance.

### The hidden cost of ambivalence

Holding too many open paths is psychic taxation, not freedom. Too many unresolved decisions chew up memory, attention, and energy. You feel busy, but nothing moves.

Making too many choices can lead to decision fatigue, but so can postponing what matters. Ambivalence accumulates like clutter, building an opaque wall of “maybe later.”  Every yes is a no to something else, but every no clears the path a little and sets us on our way.

The gravity of consequences is sneaky. Over time, as we fail to move forward, that parabola expands in dark directions: Status quo gives way to scraping by.

One of the most uncomfortable truths about clarity is that it requires loss. Every honest “yes” means a hundred quiet “no’s” — to paths, people, and possibilities you might have explored. This is why we delay, why we circle instead of commit.  We're not sure exactly what we want, so we don't want to take any options off the table.

We love to say, "When God closes a door, he opens a window."  Doors don't work that way; they can open and close a hundred times a day or more.  What they can’t do is take you anywhere if you don't walk through them.  The worst choice is probably standing still: an ambivalent life becomes a series of low-stakes diversions.

### Direction is better than perfection

We say we delay action because we want the right answer, the perfect path, the certain outcome.  Often, though, we're just comfortable and want to maintain that comfort.  Clarity will push us to do something, and doing something usually means change, and change is scary -- and everything's just fine right here, right now.

The gravity of consequences is sneaky. Over time, as we fail to move forward, that parabola expands in dark directions: Status quo gives way to scraping by.  You spent so much time on your phone that you lost your job.  If all you do is keep playing with the phone, eventually the service will be turned off, and you find that you've slid from that "safe" center into crisis, or at the very least, scraping by.

Granted, clarity isn’t certainty either, just direction. But unlike the slide into collapse, clarity is a slope you can feel early on, a nudge toward alignment that feels right long before you can see a summit. It's less about where you’ll end up and more about which way is toward meaning. You don’t need to see the finish line, you just need to move where things feel weighty and real. Perfection doesn't live at the center, but progress lives at the edge.

### The clarity compass

Here’s one question I come back to again and again: *If I were being honest with myself, what choice would move me closer to meaning—even if it’s uncomfortable?*

This question doesn’t ask what’s easiest, most urgent, or most expected. It asks what would ripple outward with meaning.  It’s how you detect which direction tips you out of inertia and into significance.

There’s a recording out there — low fidelity, almost forgotten. A phone tech was setting up a line and had to record a placeholder message. He didn’t know what to say, but he had to say something. So he hit record and spoke:

> 

“This is the DMS-100 line. Someone said to put a message here. I’m not sure exactly what it’s supposed to say. So if you get this message… I guess you can just ignore it.”

It’s a bit awkward, and kind of funny, but also quietly heroic, because he showed up. He didn’t let not-knowing stop him. He didn’t wait for perfect certainty, he did something — and in that, he did the assignment. Maybe not how he imagined, or how anyone expected; but truthfully, authentically, and without pretension.

And that’s the point: Most meaningful decisions aren’t made from a place of certainty. They’re made from honesty. You don’t need to be sure; just care enough to try. Name the truth as you know it, and leave a record of your attempt.

### Choice is courage

Clarity is a kind of courage. It says, "I don’t have all the answers, but I’m willing to move toward something that matters." When you live this way, you don’t always get it right, but you get it *real*. In the long arc of consequence, that makes all the difference.

So if you’re staring down a decision today — personal, creative, relational — don’t ask, "What’s easiest?" or even "What’s most impressive?" Ask, "What will happen if I don't, and what will ripple forward if i do?" Then take one small step in that direction. That’s how you move from the middle to the edge — from static to story.

And that’s how you reclaim the gravity of your own life.
