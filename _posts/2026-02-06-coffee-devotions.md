I miss George. 

He was my first mentor, fresh out of college. Brilliant. A PhD in Electrical Engineering. But what really drew me was his style. 

Everybody else on executive row at this test lab wore ties. As a freshly-minted BSEE, I did, too, even though my desk was an electronics bench in a mainframe computer room in a metal building a half-mile away. 

George, a staff engineer reporting directly to the president, did not. He wore perpetually wrinkled dress shirts with a brown corduroy coat, summer or winter. At six foot two, you could see the unkempt mass of curly hair coming a quarter mile away. 

That was the first thing different about him. At a company that was largely retired military, with a strict dress code and tight discipline, everyone liked George just the way he was.

His rare and valuable gift was being completely disarming. He frequently spoke in a very relaxed way, sprinkling his conversations with “maybe,” “kinda,” “sorta,” and other words to soften opinions and teaching that were always sharp, brilliant, and inevitably correct. And his delivery went with his look. 

When George got on a crowded elevator, he quickly had everyone laughing and talking. He carried a lively cocktail party with him everywhere without touching a drop of liquor. In fact, when he died, he’d left instructions for his kids to rent the biggest ballroom they could find and throw a massive indoor picnic, using the money he’d saved. His address book was the guest list. 

No funeral. No graveside (he donated his body to science). Just four-thousand of us packed into the floor of the arena, with cards that had 50 names apiece on them. The first person to meet all fifty got his Volkswagen. I didn’t win the car, but I met 400 people that day. 

Oh, and there was coffee. No liquor, just a Starbucks crew with a bunch of espresso machines and custom mugs you could take home. They brought the full menu, and he’d had signs made with “the nine billion names of coffee,” as he liked to say.

Coffee was his negotiating tool. When he wanted you to do something, he invited you somewhere to have coffee. Our coffees were usually in his office with the door closed. 

I remember a coffee we had the afternoon of my last day at the place we both worked. He shared some stories, and then said, “I bet you’re wondering why I called this meeting.”

He went on to tell me that my engineering mind was unique, and my math skills over the top and otherworldly. “I’ve never met anyone who speaks fluent Fourier transform. I’ll bet your sock drawer follows Fibonacci. You sure your parents didn’t dump you out here when their saucer flew by?” 

Then he got very serious. I’d never seen that before. He talked at length about how much of a shame it would be if I didn’t go to grad school. I left feeling like a million bucks, and started back to school the very next day.

We connected briefly here and there. One day I was out for a walk. He passed in his VW, saw me, and pulled into the next driveway. We talked for a while, but I remember that he asked me about my continuing education. Was I working on a master’s? Had I applied for the Professional Engineer certification yet? Would I consider MIT if he could get me in?

While we were talking, the homeowner came out to see what we were doing, parked on the end of the driveway. Ten minutes later, we were sitting around the guy’s kitchen table, laughing and drinking coffee and bonding. That was George: empathy, charm, and wisdom wrapped up in a threadbare corduroy coat.

---

Coffee gets many people up in the morning and makes them feel like they can at least get their day started. Good, bad, or indifferent, you’re awake. 

Now imagine a rustic mug rug that comes with a nostalgic story that fits on a laminated postcard, about people who care for each other, enjoying coffee together. That should also make you feel better. 

And what should go with that? Carefully woven advice about how to use and manage our emotions, good or bad, to lift our mood, make our days better, bring us closer to others, move us toward solving our problems, and get us busy improving the lives of others and building a legacy to leave for humanity.

In short, how to be like George.

Hold that thought.
