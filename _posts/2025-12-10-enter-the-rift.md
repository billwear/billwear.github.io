It’s possible to live in a world full of noisy distractions and still inhabit a conversation with your whole heart. You already have the built-ins, but if you’re human, you usually let the dopamine win.

There’s some talk about the “new” weaponization of empathy. That’s not new (it’s at least as old as used car salesmen) and easy to spot, usually. “That happened to me, too.” “Oh, you poor thing.” Correction: as old as Southern Belles: “Bless your heart.”

I believe there’s a genuine way to create empathy for others.

First, you have to tell your brain to focus. What’s inconsistent about your conversation partner? Maybe a bit of hair akimbo, a clenched fist, a tense, confused expression? This alone will cause the background to blur into a white fog. The room falls away.

More importantly, so does your competitive nature. You stop trying to think of better stories. All you notice is the person in front of you, trying to make sense of something they can’t quite name.

If you commit to this focus, a moment opens, subtle, vertical, a thin seam of possibility, If you keep paying attention, you can feel the shift.

There’s a rift between what they say and what they mean. Between how they look and how they feel. Between what they show and what they can’t yet articulate.

And that’s where everything changes, because that rift isn’t distance. It’s a communal direction. You’re getting caught up in what they’re saying.

You do it naturally, you just respect it. It appears when someone pauses a beat too long. When their eyes track something you can’t see. When their voice flattens. When they clench a fist without realizing it. These little things (that you overlook when you’re rushing to figure out “what’s in it for me”) are the actual coordinates of empathy.

You don’t bridge the rift by assuming. You cross it by noticing. And that’s where you come in.

You don’t have to fix anything. You don’t have to be brilliant. You don’t even have to understand the whole story. You just have to turn your head a little, the way you’d rotate inside a strange room, and look at the world from their angle.

You’d be amazed what becomes visible when you do.

You start to see the haze behind their words. You start to hear the meaning under the explanation. You start to feel the pressure they haven’t found language for yet.

And when you finally step through that rift gently, slowly, without forcing anything,  you’re not invading their space. You’re joining their scene.

That’s the quiet heart of empathy: not solving, not saving, not scanning for the right response or trying to top their stories. Just crossing the small distance between you

and the person who can’t cross it alone right now.

And once you’ve crossed it, together, you discover something simple and strange: connection doesn’t happen in the words. It happens in the noticing. In the patience. In the angle shift.

In the brief, fragile walk into someone else’s daylight.

For you

1. Pause before responding. Let your attention settle long enough for small signals to surface.

2. When something feels off, name the tiny detail to yourself; don’t rush to interpret it.

3. Clear one mental distraction so you can stay present for the next beat of the moment.

4. Ask a single clarifying question instead of guessing at hidden meaning; let the answer shape the next step. It will also thrill your conversation partner to know that you’re paying attention.

5. Focus on one thing you can attend to fully; depth, not breadth, builds trust.

6. Strip away the inessential response and the “me-too” stories. Keep only what moves the connection forward.

7. When someone’s emotion rises, remember it’s a physiological wave. Stay steady until it crests.

Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.
