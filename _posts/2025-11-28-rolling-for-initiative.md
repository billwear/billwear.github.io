*Running your life like a D&D campaign.*

Tonight feels like a weirdly detached Thanksgiving. I showed up, ate, half-socialized, watched a three-year-old argue with reality.

Everything was “No, I don’t wanna.” And, honestly, I get it. Good for you kid: Find your own path.

And my own mind is clear tonight. Decisions land softly. The usual pull toward over-structure is faint, like an echo from another life. All those systems built for corporate survival feel brittle now. They belonged to the person who had to defend every hour, justify every plan, and stay ahead of someone else’s expectations.

That’s not who I am anymore.

What I keep returning to is this: life works better when I treat it like a long campaign through a dark and dangerous universe.

That’s why I hang caps behind the door. That’s why the emergency kits stay packed. That’s why the tools, the radios, the sewing gear, the notebooks, the little rituals — all of it — live within arm’s reach. 

It’s not clutter. It’s preparation. It’s the utility belt that keeps a wandering character alive and useful. It’s the physical reminder that the world is unpredictable, but worth exploring anyway.

The seven books I read over the summer were never about “productivity” for me. They were the early manuals — the field guides, the character-builders, the mindset modules you collect as you level up. Getting Things Done taught me how to clear the fog. Atomic Habits showed me how to light small torches in dark tunnels. Deep Work taught me how to hear myself think. The others filled in the rest: how to choose, how to focus, how to cut the noise, how to understand the creature I am.

All of that became the backbone of my “utility belt” long before I called it that.

So the D&D life isn’t cosplay. It’s not nostalgia. It’s the most accurate description of how I actually move through the world.

Medieval sewing. Maker quests. Ren faire aesthetics. Weird tools that do exactly one clever thing. A house that feels more like a wizard’s study than an office. A daily rhythm built around a morning fire and a night ember. It’s the life I’ve been building piece by piece, even when I didn’t have the words for it.

And lately I’ve been thinking about how many of the numbers that define our universe are irrational: pi, e, phi. The fundamentals don’t simplify. The world doesn’t round off neatly. So why would my days?

Maybe the point was *never* to march straight.

Maybe the point is to pick a quest, enter the dungeon, learn something, and come home with a story — again and again — until the character sheet finally looks like somebody I recognize.

The regimented bullshit can fall away.

The adventure can stay.

After all, life is either a daring adventure in a dark and dangerous universe, or nothing.
