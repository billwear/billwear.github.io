title: "Sadness has its place"
excerpt: "Being sad tells us the object of memory is still important to us"

Lying here in the early morning, waking up, feeling the immense and delicious weight of sadness for all the ones who’ve gone before. Silent, welcome spirits who visit in the shimmering corners of twilight, flooding the morning with joy and heartache and meaning. Strange how memory sings to me, how love, worn thin by years, softens and blossoms again in this quiet return to breath.

Paradoxical to relish sadness?  Perhaps, but worthwhile.  How else will we appreciate how much those memories mean to us?  Every person gone from our daily life, remembered in the quiet moments, is a person who matters to us.  Every memorable situation we left behind, every beloved possession — a favorite car, a toy we loved, a 

place where we wish we still lived — all these things add up to create the color and the character of our inner fire.  

Cherish them.  If they’re not completely out of your grasp, but just distant, why not pull them back for a while?  Make those calls.  Write those texts. Visit those places. Go back through your photographs.  

There’s a kind of rejuvenating effect in reliving memories, a sort of unique emotional calibration.  And in our own small way, we are preserving at least a sliver of what was, and perhaps adjusting our forward trajectory just a little.
