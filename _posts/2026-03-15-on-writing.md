Writing is sort of a lonely existence, and for me, it feels that way at the moment, but it’s a fleeting and tolerable experience. My wife is being a mensch and living with a friend who just lost her husband. We are a unique pair, since we prefer *shared* solitude over continuous chatter, but the time apart has made us more talkative when we do connect.

Meanwhile, I am free to design my days as I see fit, and while life without a boss has tremendous flexibility, such as eating ice cream at midnight, there is a pattern of regular writing that’s emerging. As a writer, I could not be considered a plodder,a plotter, nor a pantser. Type-casting would be very difficult in my case.

I have a peculiar gravity around the ideas I pursue. Long and comprehensive drafts are not difficult, owing in part to the huge storehouse of blogs and journal entries in my backlog. I never have to look that far to find an excellent seed or a poetic phrasing. So my habit is to write a complete draft, design a cover, and sit with it a great while to find the ill-fitting parts, ambiguous positions, and rough transitions. 

Eventually, I will be able to collapse the wrongness into a simple critique of the gestalt of the last effort. From this, I start again, usually keeping the best ideas from the previous draft, but blended with a new direction.

In short, the message refines but the presentation and positioning morph until I finally have a story that’s worth telling.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YUYc!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fee0aea86-7285-4171-917b-478e4956d020_1024x1536.heic)

With my current work in progress, “Zero Defects: Better Living through Rocket Science,” I’ve found a stable formula and the bones of a publishable draft are emerging. Originally, this material lived as a “rocket science for everyday use” manifesto, but the geek factor was far too high.

Late yesterday afternoon, I stumbled over the idea of embedding heartfelt, engaging fiction to make the material more accessible. Enter Maria Domenguez, a New Orleans housewife trying to survive the frontal boundary that is Thanksgiving for too many real households. I opened the book with a compressed, day-of before-and-after involving a good Catholic family, feuding cousins, missing ingredients, a critical mother, and a new, kosher-keeping girlfriend wading into her first exposure to a traditional ham-on-thanksgiving family.

And one undefrosted turkey.

When I re-read my scene draft, I cried. And then I laughed harder than I expected. Success. I found a way to make the magic of engineering real, personable, and useful to everyone. I daresay that getting through a complex day now feels like the kind of problem engineering can handle, with the right amount of love and compassion mixed in.
