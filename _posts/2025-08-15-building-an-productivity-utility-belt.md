I’m an INFP; that’s Myers-Briggs shorthand for Introverted, intuitive, Feeling, Perceiving. In practical terms, it means I thrive on ideas, possibilities, and personal meaning, but I’m not naturally wired for rigid structure or detailed planning. My superpower is vision and adaptability; my kryptonite is repetitive routines, follow-through on boring details, and anything that feels overly mechanical.

An ESTJ, on the other hand, is Extroverted, Sensing, Thinking, Judging, the opposite on every letter. They’re the commanders, organizers, and rule-keepers of the personality world. They build structure, enforce schedules, and get things done with impressive consistency. It’s not my native style… but to navigate the world well,  it’s exactly the structure I need.

Why should you care about this? Because for people like me, adopting the tools of our opposite type doesn’t erase who we are, it just shores up the weak spots that cost us time, peace of mind, credibility, and opportunities. I’m not trying to become an ESTJ. I’m building a utility belt of their best habits and systems so I can still think, feel, and imagine like an INFP, but execute like someone with a clipboard, a plan, and a laser focus..

## The Goal: A Clear Head, Less Stress, More Flow

When my head is cluttered with “all the things,” I lose the joy of creating and the space to think clearly. The ESTJ Utility Belt is my way to:

- Make things easier, so my energy goes to the work that matters, not remembering milk or chasing forgotten, overdue tasks.

- Free up mental space; by offloading ideas and commitments into a system I trust, I'm actually freeing up creativity and bandwidth, because INFPs never "put anything down" unless it's written down somewhere that they know for sure they'll look.

- Reduce decision fatigue, by setting up habits, priorities, and review cycles ahead of time.

- Give myself a reliable launchpad, so I can focus on what inspires me without constantly losing momentum on side-quests and the wonderful network of ideas that wants to occupy my head.

The rest of this write-up is a tour of the five “gadgets” I’m adding to my belt in this first phase. Each one a is focused, practical tool borrowed from productivity masters, adapted for an INFP brain, and implemented in org-mode so it runs almost on autopilot.

In short, this project is my personal productivity arsenal: a carefully chosen set of tools, each inspired by a trusted productivity method, mapped to an Emacs/org-mode workflow. Think of it like a Batman belt, but every gadget is designed to help me capture, focus, act, and review automatically, without draining mental energy or forcing me to be something I'm just not.

I’m building it in five core “modules,” each with its own mission, inspiration, and measurable outcome:

### 1. Quick-Capture Grappling Hook

Inspiration: Getting Things Done by David Allen

Why It’s in the Belt: GTD’s “capture” principle is the rope that pulls stray thoughts, tasks, and ideas out of my head before they slip away.

Focus: I’m concentrating on the “Capture” and “Clarify” chapters — the blueprint for emptying my mind into a trusted system.

Goal: By the end of this week, I want my reflex to be: have an idea → drop it instantly into inbox.org. No hesitation. No half-memory.

### 2. Agenda Batcomputer

Inspiration: Make Time by Jake Knapp & John Zeratsky

Why It’s in the Belt: The “Highlight” method turns a cluttered day into a focused mission. My org-mode agenda is perfect for this — it just needs the right targeting system.

Focus: Studying “Highlight” and time-blocking techniques to give each day a main objective.

Goal: Every morning, pick one highlight and bake it into my agenda view so I can’t ignore it.

### 3. Habit Grappler

Inspiration: Atomic Habits by James Clear

Why It’s in the Belt: Habits are the grappling lines that keep me anchored to consistent progress. If I can make the environment work for me, I don’t need to fight myself.

Focus: Habit stacking, identity-based habits, and environment design.

Goal: Add at least one habit to org-habit and arrange my surroundings so it’s easier to do the habit than skip it.

### 4. Project Utility Pouches

Inspiration: The One Thing by Gary Keller & Jay Papasan

Why It’s in the Belt: These pouches hold the mission-critical items — the project or subtask that moves the needle most.

Focus: The “focusing question”: What’s the ONE thing I can do such that by doing it everything else will be easier or unnecessary?

Goal: Tag and track my most impactful project in projects.org, keep it in my daily view, and make sure every day I know the “one thing” I’m moving forward.

### 5. Review & Reset Smoke Bomb

Inspiration: The 12 Week Year by Brian P. Moran & Michael Lennington

Why It’s in the Belt: Weekly reviews are my way of throwing down a smoke bomb on distractions — giving me space to reassess and reposition before I’m ambushed by drift.

Focus: Planning in 12-week sprints, plus the rhythm of weekly reviews.

Goal: Run my first real weekly review in review.org and use it to adjust my next week’s plan with precision.
