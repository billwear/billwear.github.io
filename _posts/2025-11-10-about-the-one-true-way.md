We have proximity, but no patience;  connection with no context. We live in feeds built to flatter us, where “like-ish minds” drift together until the friction’s gone. It feels like belonging, but it’s only shallow, paper-thin agreement.

Clumping is easy now. Political, intellectual, social, even financial tribes form faster than thought. We merge by reaction, not reflection. One post offends, another defends, and soon the echo becomes identity, and we choose sides so fiercely that we hardly recognize ourselves. What should have been dialogue becomes derivative society — a culture of copies of copies, driven by the thrill of “us vs. them” and the desire for the perfect shortcut.

Every tribe has its “one true way,” forming and re-forming with the latest meme. Forget religion, profession, class, ideology, and political party. We are loose collections of warring tribes.

Each claims to have the right kind of flame. But the truth is smaller, quieter, and far more dispersed. There is no single light that redeems the rest. The human story has never been about finding the one right way; it’s always been about learning to live morally among many partial ones, without hating each other.

Unless I'm sadly mistaken, those are the principles upon which America was founded. Shame on US.

There is no genie. No get-rich-quick. No ladder through the Ponzi of modern fame. No secret door that trades devotion for dopamine. No man sent from God to destroy all the other tribes and bend them to our current (and easily changed) way of thinking.

It doesn't work like that. It never has. Every shortcut burns the same fuel — hope without craft — and it always runs out.

Rare value still wins, but only at fair prices. Skill refined in silence. Focus held through fatigue. Work that outlasts applause. You don’t gain customers by chasing after them.  You serve them by becoming indispensable in the one thing you do best. You improve it continuously, because mastery isn’t a Facebook status, it’s a lifelong practice.

And that practice needs judicious goals. Choose them with care. Ratchet them upward as you grow, not as you envy. Build contingency plans that honor reality, not fantasy. Leave room for pause. When distraction whispers, remember that every “yes” costs a dozen “maybes.”

The Global Village will keep shouting. Let it. Your work will speak longer.

## For you

1. Focus on one craft. Pick the work where skill compounds, and make it your signature. Read about it, study it, practice it. Overnight success takes about fifteen years. The years from fifteen to thirty are perfect for this.

2. Price for fairness, not fame. Charge enough to stay in the game, not to feed comparison. Give free samples and help others solve their own problems, free of charge. The world will beat a path to your door when they need you,

3. Iterate in public silence. Improve daily, but don’t announce every step — let quality reveal itself. A good magician never reveals their secrets mostly because they know you actually want to be amazed. Give them that moment of wonder.

4. Define your ratchet. Write down one metric of progress you can raise each quarter. The correct ratio should be  "three steps forward, one step back."  If not, you need more practice.

5. Plan contingencies, not miracles. What’s your next move if the current one fails? Write it out. Save money. Buy supplies on sale. Find ways to use what you have. Don't exceed your means. Every life has high and low tides, so plan to take lots of pictures on the sandbar.

6. Cull distractions by category. Drop one app, one habit, one person who drains focus. It's not cruelty or  self-discipline, just good sense.

7. Set your pace to your purpose. The slower you move with intent, the faster you grow in quality, and quality will ratchet you up faster than any "seven-step e-mail system."

Every tribe and nation responds favorably to a job well done. Do your part to help us join hands in the global village by doing quality work that forms a point of universal agreement.

Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.
