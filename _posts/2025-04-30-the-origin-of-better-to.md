I think it began as a quiet rebellion against perfectionism.

I was sick of never getting anything done — of spending so much time overthinking, overengineering, overplanning, and overcommitting that even the simplest task collapsed under its own weight. There was always another angle, always one more way to optimize. But then, one day — I’m not even sure what triggered it — I was staring down a problem that just needed solving. No time for the usual dithering. I had to act.

There were a few plausible options on the table, and I asked myself, maybe for the first time out loud:  

**“Which one is better than the others?”**

Not *perfect*. Just *better*.

That question became a turning point.

It started small — a way to break logjams when choices were hard or time was tight. But soon, it began to creep into my day-to-day thinking. I caught myself applying it to moments that didn’t even feel like decisions. 

“There’s a better way to do this,” I’d say, even when I was just brushing my teeth or making coffee. I remember one particular moment, walking up the stone path to my house, slouched over, dragging my feet like I was auditioning for the part of "tired old man" in a low-budget drama. Suddenly, without thinking, I muttered,  

**“Better to stand up straight and walk with purpose, than drag myself like a dead animal.”**

And just like that, I was hooked.

Over the next few days, I started applying the *Better To* rule to everything. Whether or not I was consciously choosing between alternatives, the mindset made me *look* for them. It nudged me to notice what I was doing, and ask if there might be a better way — even a slightly better one. Often, there was. And bit by bit, things actually began to improve.

It couldn’t have come at a better time. We were deep into renovating my wife’s grandmother’s house — a 70-year-old structure full of character and chaos, worn down by two decades of rotating relatives and deferred maintenance. The renovation had already taken a year, with several more months still ahead. We’d just moved in, which meant we were juggling a mortgage (after nearly 20 years without one), double utility bills, and two broadband accounts — all while trying to live among the dust and drywall.

Then life threw its usual wrench party: a root canal, a sick cat, a car breakdown. One $300–$400 emergency after another. The budget didn’t just feel tight — it felt predatory. Like it might bite back at any moment.

I could’ve panicked. Maybe I did for a second. But then I remembered the rule:  

“**Is there a better way to deal with this?**”

Turns out, there was. I got precise. Laser-focused. I tracked our budget down to the penny. We still had to negotiate payment plans, cut back on a few luxuries, and make some hard calls — but we got through it. We didn’t go broke. And we didn’t lose the house. That simple, persistent question carried us through.

## “Better To” as a Mindfulness Hack

I’ve never really been a mindfulness guy. I tried some of the books. The language was nice. The idea of being present appealed to me — in theory. But it always felt like I was being told to tune in *so hard* to the present moment that the future became an afterthought. That’s fine until reality kicks down the door.

But the *Better To* mindset didn’t work that way.

It kept me present — not by insisting I savor every breath like it was a rare vintage, but by asking, again and again:  

**“Is this what I actually want to be doing right now?”**

**“Is there a slightly better way to handle this moment?”**

**“What will help, not just now, but next?”**

And that question — that lens — sharpened my awareness. It didn’t ask me to escape the future. It just helped me steer toward it with intention, rather than fantasy.

*Better To* isn't about always choosing the best option. It’s about moving from stuck to better. From default to deliberate. From dragging your feet to walking with purpose.

That’s where this story begins — not with a *doctrine*, but with a *mindset*. A habit of asking. A bias for motion. A compass, not a map.

Because perfection is paralyzing. But *better*?  

Better is doable.
