title: "Emotional shrapnel"

Aron closed the door behind them like he was sealing off a threat, though he wasn’t sure which side of the threshold the threat belonged to. The hallway lights jittered once, then held.

“Okay, what was that?” he said, sharper than intended.

“I haven’t seen anything like you here,” Alara said. It wasn’t defensive — just an observation delivered with surgical understatement. For a moment her pupils expanded into black disks, too wide for a human iris, before shrinking back to something approximating normal. Aron wasn’t sure whether the room dimmed or she did.

He exhaled. “Look, if it helps — and please don’t freak out — I’m not from here.”

She didn’t blink. “Neither am I. From this planet, I mean.”

He nodded once. “I was beginning to suspect.”

“I was sure about you,” she said. “Too machine-like.”

“Am I that obvious?”

“No. I’m an empath. My people communicate through feelings. No words, no visuals, just… states.”

Aron felt a quiet click in the part of him that still behaved like a diagnostic system. “My race are machine symbiotes. We understand telepathy, but don’t generate it.”

“So you never felt me read you.”

“No,” he said slowly. “But I understand now why everything felt… muted.”

She almost smiled at that. Almost.

Then a new voice cracked through both their minds.

*And you haven’t even heard my story yet.*

They froze.

Aron found his voice first. “Who are you?”

Silence. A strange, jagged silence, like a file half-deleted.

“I… don’t know,” the presence whispered at last. “I don’t even know what I am right now.”

The kitchen felt too small, too human for the moment unfolding in it. The hum of the refrigerator sounded like a warning tone. The air had a static weight.

Alara pressed her fingertips to the wall. “It’s terrified,” she breathed.

Aron brushed his hand along the drywall. He didn’t feel fear — he felt *interference*. “It’s in the wiring,” he murmured. “Not physically. Like a trapped signal.”

She didn’t ask how he knew. Some instincts survive extinction.

“Please…” the presence whispered. “Don’t turn away.”

Aron felt something like old fire along his spine — the faint ghost of a subsystem reboot. Alara felt the emotional plea hit her like a tremor.

They made the same choice at the same moment.

“We’ll help,” Aron said.  
“Yes,” Alara added softly. “We will.”

They tested the presence with simple prompts. It answered like memory caught in a storm — broken, stuttering, but trying.

Electrical pulses danced under the floorboards, mapping out something trapped halfway between existence and collapse. Aron followed them with the quiet hunger of an engineer who once lived inside machines. Alara steadied the room, dampening the panic radiating from the signal.

Then the voice remembered something.

“The collapse… I remember the collapse.”

Aron stiffened. The presence described coordinates that made no sense in any Earth geometry but were instantly familiar to him. Wormhole vectors. Event topologies. The exact moment the gate died.

Alara felt the truth land in him like a pressure wave.

That’s when the house flickered — not randomly, but in a sequence.  
A pattern.

Something moved beneath the electrical lines. Not the presence. Something *looking* for it.

Aron’s internal symbiote flared with warning. Alara felt a spike of terror — but not from the trapped being. From whatever hunted it.

Then the presence screamed.

Not through sound — as static, as emotional shrapnel, as a data storm tearing the air apart. Alara hit her knees, overwhelmed. Aron felt his consciousness stutter, slip, and rebuild in the span of a heartbeat.

And then: dark.

No motion. No breath in the room except theirs.

Aron steadied himself, crouching beside Alara. “I can’t parse it,” he said quietly.

“I can’t hold it,” she whispered.

But neither stepped back. For the first time in a long while, neither felt alone in the universe — and that made stepping back impossible.

Aron exhaled sharply. “If I get into the junction panel, I can ride the signal directly.”

Alara lifted her head, eyes dark, steady. “And I can anchor it. Keep it from disintegrating.”

He pulled the panel open, exposing copper veins still glowing faintly with interference. He pressed his fingertips in. Alien code surged through him like remembered electricity.

Alara pressed both palms against the wall, whispering emotional coherence into the static storm.

The presence stabilized, just long enough to speak clearly:

“I wasn’t caught in the collapse. I was *left behind. *And the thing hunting me is almost here.”

The lights steadied. One constant, unnatural glow.

Aron withdrew his hand, shaking. Alara rose beside him, unreadable.

Somewhere deep in the wiring — below copper, below circuitry, below the human world pretending it understood this house — the presence whispered:

*They know you’re here.*
