*Save everything.*

---

Her given name was “Birdie,” and she had the kind of hidden strength that stacks lumber under a tarp.  

---

A year and a half ago, wife and I bought her grandmother’s old home place, a legacy home that’s pushing 70.  One entire year of clearing, cleaning, and fixing before we could move in.

A farm full of remembrance — a time-capsule and savings account and memory box all in one.

We’ve got piles of wood pulled from decades-old cabinets; antique hardware sorted into antique Mason jars; farm tools leaned carefully in the corner. This isn’t junk, it’s *legacy.* 

Things here have been touched by sunlight from another century. They were made when time was slower, hands steadier, and resources weren’t disposable.

When we first started to tackle this place, I grabbed a notepad and wrote:

> 

Motto for Birdie’s Nest: “Save everything.”

And so far, we have done that: the screws, the hinges, the half-can of stain. We clean and sharpen the old instead of buying new.

We don’t hoard; we *honor*. We **preserve the good we have, because that *****is***** progress**. 

In a world obsessed with new, there’s quiet rebellion in keeping old things working, in making them sing again.

The homestead we’re building isn’t flashy, but every nail, every salvaged plank, every stubborn bolt we unstick and put to use again — it all adds up. It tells the truth: **We are not wasteful. We are not helpless. We are builders, even now.**

### Generational sunlight

Birdie’s Nest has transformed us more than we could ever have changed it.  Once obsessive members of the TV Generation, we’ve suddenly become managers and conservators, meting out money and time and energy carefully. Dollars are stretched, effort is carefully talked through, existing parts and tools are repurposed.  

No decision is taken lightly,

When you save what others discard, you’re doing more than managing money. You’re saying: *This still matters.* You’re saying: *What came before me wasn’t useless.* You’re saying: *I see the stored fire in this, and I won’t let it go out.*

There’s a sacredness in preservation: not as a museum, but as a *foundation*. As sunlight stored in oak grain, in rusted tools, in hands that once held them.

And there’s inner fire in that, too.

Not the kind that burns fast, but the kind that stays. The kind that warms the next room over, and with any luck at all, the next generation. The next weary soul who thought maybe everything good had already been thrown away may one day find safety and comfort here.

Know better. Build better.

Save everything. Tend what still works. And when the time comes, make something beautiful out of the margin you’ve made.
