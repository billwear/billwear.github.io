Some problems don’t have obvious answers.

You ask yourself “what would help?” and nothing clear shows up. Or you get three conflicting ideas at once. Or the problem is so tangled — relationship issues, career decisions, creative blocks — that a single action won’t solve it.

This is where most people get stuck. They sit there trying to *think* their way through it, wrestling with a problem that has dozens of moving parts. It’s like trying to catch a swarm of bees with tweezers.

Here’s a technique I use when I’m stuck: mind mapping. It’s deceptively simple, and it works because it lets your brain dump associations faster than your conscious mind can organize or critique them.

**The Method**

**1. Get physical materials.** Paper and pen. Envelope and crayon. Napkin and mascara. Whatever you have within arm’s reach. Not your phone. Not your laptop. Actual physical materials you can touch.

**2. Put the problem in the center** of the page. Draw a circle or box around it. Not the solution, not the goal — the actual problem. “Stuck on whether to take this job.” “Can’t figure out the book’s structure.” “Still angry at my brother.”

**3. Set a timer for 5 minutes.** Then start dumping.

Draw a line out from the center. Write one word or short phrase. Draw another line. Write the next thing that comes up. Keep going. Each branch gets just one word or phrase.

Don’t organize. Don’t connect things to each other yet. Don’t judge whether something is “relevant.” Just capture whatever surfaces.

The mess is the point.

When the timer goes off, stop.

**4. Look at what you’ve got.** Scan your branches. Which words or phrases pull your attention? Circle them.

**5. Extend the interesting ones.** Pick one circled item that feels most alive, most urgent, most *interesting*. Draw new lines out from that word. Set the timer for another 3 minutes. Keep dumping associations from that node.

Maybe “angry at my brother” branches to “he never apologizes” which branches to “I always have to fix things” which branches to “just like with Dad” and suddenly you’re looking at a pattern you didn’t see when you were just trying to think about the argument.

**6. Follow what draws you.** When one branch goes cold, jump to another circled item and extend from there.

After 15-20 minutes total, you’ll have a messy, sprawling map that looks nothing like an outline or a to-do list.

**7. Look for the pattern.** Step back. What keeps showing up? What surprised you? What branch went somewhere unexpected?

Often the solution isn’t in the center — it’s three or four associations out, in a connection your conscious mind would never have made because it was too busy being logical.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_05p!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6cc2328b-60b6-48b1-aa84-87b82b05d716_1221x827.heic)

**What To Do Next**

**If one area seems important but still fuzzy:** Get another napkin. Put that specific node in the center and map *that*.

**If you have an idea what to do next:** Start a list on a separate sheet. “Possible next steps.” Don’t build a master plan, just capture concrete actions that could break the logjam.

**If you have an “aha” moment:** Put a concrete action related to that insight into your to-do list or calendar right now. Lock it down. Commit to it.

The goal isn’t perfect clarity or a complete solution. The goal is **movement**. You came into this stuck. Now you’ve got associations, patterns, possible next steps, maybe even one concrete action you can take.

That’s enough.

In my book “No Off Switch: Creating Rare Value with Your Wild-Child Mind,” I’ll demonstrate a much more detailed mind-mapping method that drills down to the core issue. But this snapshot still gives you the core skill: learning to query your Edge Ego for details and solutions.

Just remember that the mind map *itself* isn’t the solution. It’s the bridge between “I’m stuck” and “I know what to try next.”

Try this the next time you’re stuck on something that won’t yield to normal thinking. Grab whatever’s nearby and give yourself 15 minutes. See what shows up.
