*Are you flying the plan or flying the plane?*

There’s a favorite scene I carry from *Airplane*. A ground controller panics: “They could be miles off course!” Another insists, “That’s impossible—they’re on instruments!”

Cut to the cockpit, where everyone’s riffing jazz, horns and reeds everywhere, smoke curling, chaos swinging in 4/4 time.

It’s funny, but it’s also the perfect metaphor for life. We look for tools we can trust — systems that promise to keep us level — and then we hand them the controls. We call it safety, or discipline, or productivity. But it’s often surrender.

Don’t get me wrong: life isn’t VFR. We can’t see that far ahead, no matter how smart we are. We need instruments — habits, checklists, calendars, goals, projects — that help us sense and guide what’s coming. We need skills that keep us alive. But those tools can’t fly the plane. You can’t outsource the feel of the air.

The thing about instruments is that they don’t care. They only measure conditions: altitude, pressure, speed, attitude. They don’t read intent.

That’s what makes them safe, but also what makes them dangerous. Safe, because they free you from guessing. Dangerous, because they free you from feeling.

For most of my life, I let the instruments run the show. The plan, the schedule, the sequence. The rules that said when to climb and when to turn. They were supposed to make everything easier. But they also made me a little deaf. I stopped listening to what the work wanted. I started flying the *plan* instead of flying the *plane*.

That single letter — the “e” at the end— is everything.

The “e” stands for *experience*. For *engagement*. For *embodiment*.

Flying the plane means being there in the cockpit. Feeling the stick. Reading the air. Listening to the hum of your own engines. The “e” is where the human enters the loop. It’s the part no autopilot can replace.

When you let instruments dictate, the horizon becomes abstract — just another number in a window. You can keep the wings level, but you can’t tell if you’re headed home or out to sea.

At some point, every maker, writer, or human hits that quiet turbulence, where the gauges look fine but your gut says something’s off. That’s when you realize you’ve been holding the stick too tight. You’re correcting for corrections. You’re no longer steering; you’re maintaining.

That’s where I was. Retirement gave me the first clear air in decades. No tower, no flight plan. Just the sound of my own engines and a question: can I trust my internal guidance?

Turns out, I can.

The feeling isn’t heroic, but steady. I wake up, and the vector feels obvious. I move from one thing to the next because momentum wants to continue, not because a checklist demands it. The sequence isn’t perfect, but the direction is true.

That’s the secret the ground crew never understands. You don’t navigate by eliminating drift; you navigate by *using* it. Every correction teaches the system what balance feels like. Every wobble is data.

When you start trusting that feedback loop, life gets simpler. You stop needing so many readouts. You sense the horizon again.

That’s not rebellion; it’s calibration.

The irony is, once you start flying this way, you appreciate your instruments more. They become companions instead of controllers. You glance at them for confirmation and trim, not permission.

That’s what my planning tool could be for me — not a microcontroller, but an amplifier. A way to record and learn the resonance, not dictate the rhythm. The same goes for any system, any tool. If it can help us hear our own frequency more clearly, it belongs on the dashboard. If it mutes us, it’s out.

We learn to filter the noise. We learn that perfection is not the goal; *alignment* is.

And one morning, you notice something new: you’re no longer waiting for the world to tell you what comes next. You’re already halfway there, and the work feels like flying by your own lights.

The signal is strong. The path is clear. You’re on instruments, sure, but they’re the ones you built yourself.

## For you

1. Capture the current. When you feel energy rising, jot what sparked it before it fades. That’s your new flight plan.

2. Do one large, right thing. Skip perfection; fix direction. A clean vector beats a polished checklist.

3. Reduce the dashboard. Keep only the tools that amplify clarity. If it adds friction, ground it.

4. Trust drift. When focus slips, notice what you drift toward. It may be where the true course lies.

5. Rebuild feedback. Check results, not rituals. The goal is sensing improvement, not logging hours.

6. Read ravenously, but borrow, don’t copy. Other people’s frameworks are radar echoes — useful for position, but they can’t squawk your identity.

7. Stay embodied. The “e” at the end is the point. Feel the air, hear the hum, stay in the seat.
