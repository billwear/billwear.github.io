This morning, I’m leaning into the sluggish mood that’s been with me since I retired on Halloween last year, but we’ve come to the Rubicon: it’s time I parted ways with old man half-ass.  I’ve worked for West Point graduates, so I know that particular energy isn’t for me at all, but I also know that I need real goals.  And by that, I mean that I want goals that fill my soul and cover the bills. Tall order. 

When we chase a bus, we’re pulled from in front, not pushed from behind, because the bus represents a destination. Usually we don’t choose the bus with our whole soul; it’s more like “the boss’ way or the highway,” and the highway is scary and low-income.  Today, I think I’ve discovered that my way *is* the highway.  I’m all about the journey, and it has to feed my soul just as effectively as it feeds my stomach.  In fact, I’ve been on this quest since I was nine years old, when I read a then-forty-year-old theology book about personal integration.

I learned in that reading, and have confirmed since in my own life, that the average person has as many faces as there are social groups they want to match.  Work; church; sports; hunting buddies.  I developed mad skills for that mode, and it’s automatic in the worst way.  Now the lines are converging; that zeitgeist is fading in the rear-view.  Like Thoreau, I have more lives to live, and I have no more time (or patience) for this one.  

How do I start?  Is it as easy as just walking out the door and leaving the cabin?  After all, I’m already a *journey* person.  From the time I was nine or ten, walking fifteen or twenty miles has been my Saturday thing. I would leave the house mid-morning and just walk: neighborhoods; dirt roads; dead ends; woods; swamps. Where didn’t matter. Discovery and the journey were the thing.

Then I grew up and responsibilities pushed me out of the habit of walking, though I’ve spent years preparing to travel. I have several large crates filled with backpacks. I keep a camping kit behind my bedroom door, backpack and all: everything you’d need to walk out the door and never come back. I guess I never realized that I was just acting out my life philosophy, or in a more picturesque sense, living my Prime Directive.

I live to go places. In my youth and childhood, I chose places that nurtured my soul and let me discover the world. In my adulthood, I leaned toward places that fed me. Now, I’m learning to seek places that do both. I don’t quite know where they are yet, but I know how to find them: I must become a discoverer again.

I’m a discoverer who goes from one place to the next not because I’m flighty, but because I like to fly.  The story is rich and powerful, the destinations are interesting stopovers, and the road is the network that connects them.  I just didn’t realize until now that it’s meant to be a one-way journey.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Lw0E!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2ebfce11-ee4d-4c8a-93c6-c585a75e6210_1024x1536.heic)

The landings are all about memories and learning, and along the way, I pick up what’s true and carry it with me.  Every layover has something to offer, and each one changes me.  I know somehow that acknowledging this will make the trip easier and more enjoyable. And I have picked up quite a lot: memories; experience; embarrassing moments to flog myself when I think I’m so smart, but then later to appreciate as real-time learning.  

On this long and winding road (my favorite song,  by the way), I’ve also learned seventeen principles that, more or less, seem about as universal as anything I’ve encountered. Keep it simple. Do one thing well (at a time). Don’t stick your nose in other people’s business. Distrust all claims for the one true way.  

I guess it’s a moral code of sorts; at the very least, it’s an effective way of living when the road is foggy and there’s no clear map: 
- 

Start small and build a little at a time; a mosaic is more beautiful than the finest concrete, and way less likely to get you sued for improper construction.
- 

Say what you mean; because telepathy is still in beta testing.
- 

Network; because who else will laugh at your “I’m not a robot” jokes?
- 

Divide and conquer; because life is essentially a giant game of strategy board games.
- 

Keep it simple; complex plans involve running, and who’s got the energy for that?
- 

Do one thing well; remember, unicorns are famous for just one horn.
- 

Be who you are; even a bent wire can carry a great light, especially in a modern art exhibit.
- 

Build for strength, not just speed; the tortoise won the race, but the hare had more Instagram followers.
- 

Speak clearly, listen carefully, pay close attention; otherwise, you’ll end up in a conversation about quantum physics when you just asked for the time.
- 

Underpromise and overdeliver; because everyone loves a surprise, especially when it’s not another birthday card.
- 

Practice the Prime Directive; unless you’re in a sci-fi movie, then totally ignore it.
- 

Hack; just remember, “try it and see” doesn’t apply to skydiving or lion taming.
- 

Use what you have; unless it’s a floppy disk, then maybe it’s time to upgrade.
- 

Use levers, not people; because people are terrible at being levers, they keep asking for coffee breaks.
- 

Release early, release often; otherwise, you’re just hoarding half-baked ideas, and there’s no market for those.
- 

Distrust all claims for the one true way — except for pizza, pizza is always the answer.
- 

Think ahead, but don’t worship your plans; remember, not all who wander are lost — they might just be looking for their keys.

So now that I know a lot more about the meaning of *my* life, how does this come together? Can i build the journey as I go, walking my principles? That would be logically satisfying, but it seems an unlikely route for the average person.

Fortunately, I am *not* the average person. In fact, I’m so mismatched that I’ve often wondered if I was thrown out of a flying saucer as they passed.  After all, Earth *is* the boonies of our galaxy.

I’m at least exceptional enough, though, to realize that these principles *are* the journey. They precipitated out of every swamp, park, forest, neighborhood, career, zeitgeist, philosophy, computer system and gestalt I frequented. They are not just ideas; they are the secrets of the psyche, the cosmic principles of the conscious mind.

In essence, I beat Star Trek at its own game: I’ve boldly gone where no one’s gone before and left a map. Hey, Spock, this way.
