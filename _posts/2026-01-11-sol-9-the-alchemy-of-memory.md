I still remember an embarrassing moment from high school. There was one girl I dated mostly because neither of us really had dates. We were both academically inclined, and I was also involved in athletics, which didn’t leave much time for socializing. And even if it had, high school social life is strange enough on its own.

We were, essentially, each other’s backup dates. We went to prom together. I remember taking her home afterward, walking her up to the stoop, and in a moment of poor judgment, I leaned in and awkwardly attempted to kiss her. She recoiled with a flash of horror, then recovered gracefully, said good night, and that was that.

It’s an embarrassing memory, but what’s always struck me is how specific it feels. It’s as if it has its own DNA. The combination of emotions wrapped around that moment — embarrassment, regret, surprise, self-awareness — is unlike any other memory I have. It brings up a feeling I’ve never experienced before or since. That was my first experience of what I think of as fingerprinting emotions.

I don’t know how many experiences like that we have in a lifetime. It’s tempting to jump to a neat conclusion and say that every memory is simply a unique combination of events and emotions, rolled together and stored away. But that doesn’t quite fit. We also have routine memories: the muscle memory of getting ready in the morning; the automatic recognition of a friend’s face; the sudden mental adjustment when you realize they’ve aged four years and you hadn’t noticed because you hadn’t seen them.

And then there are what I think of as dopamine fingerprints, the moments when you do something precisely right and feel it immediately.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Dr-m!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffa222d89-7756-452e-b6c8-afee4aa9a8ba_5712x4284.jpeg)

That happened to me last night.

I was prototyping a medicine pouch, what some might call a medicine bag. I decided to take a bit of a risk and use the actual materials I intend to use for the final version, just to see how they behaved. 

The problem was that I hadn’t anticipated all the seam allowances I would need. The design required turning pieces inside out and then sewing those reversed pieces into others. I’ve said before that I try to avoid reversing pockets, but this pouch needed a lining, and it also needed pockets with linings. The geometry got complicated very quickly.

Eventually, I stopped partway through and instead made two small, clean bags, which replaced the awkward, amateur pouch I’d been using. In the process, I learned a great deal about planning, lessons that wouldn’t have occurred to me if I hadn’t attempted something that complex without fully designing it first.

What I do know is this: when I finished ironing the outer piece with the flap, it was perfect. The corners were precise and identical. The seams were straight and landed exactly where they were supposed to. Everything lay flat and clean, sandwiched naturally between the layers. There were no loose threads, no distortions, no visible mistakes. The fabric combination—a dark navy antique map print paired with black polished cotton—worked beautifully. It looked like something you could pick up off a shelf in a jewelry store.

That moment lit me up.

Even though the overall prototype wasn’t successful, that single piece was even, smooth, flat, and elegant. The satisfaction made all the effort worthwhile. And, like that moment in high school, it carried a very specific and unmistakable emotional signature.

That made me wonder: do all experiences have unique emotional fingerprints? Is this how we recall them, and why some moments stand out so vividly? Does memory perform a kind of alchemy on experience, giving certain moments a unique DNA that makes them endure?

When I think back to last Sunday — only seven days ago, when I taught Sunday school — it feels like a hundred lifetimes ago. Not because so much time has passed, but because the first week of this 60-day challenge has imprinted so much new experience.

Maybe this is why vacations feel like they run on a different clock. When you travel, visit someone, go to a theme park, or stand at the edge of the Grand Canyon, everything is new. Every day carries its own texture. Memories are recorded differently. And when you come home, it feels as if you’ve been gone for a month, even if it was only a few days.

Time hasn’t stretched. Memory has.

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
