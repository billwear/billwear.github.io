You can choose your friends, but you can’t choose your family

You’ve heard the saying. It’s one of those uncomfortable truths that stares back from the bottom of the soup bowl at Christmas dinner. Aunt Martha redeems herself with her unbeatable pies, but the rest? Quirks, quirks everywhere: the conspiracy-theory cousin, the aunt who collects grievances like commemorative stamps, the insurance agent who’s never off-duty.

And of course, the habitual offender: the one who simply refuses to be on time.

## My Arthurian legend

In my family, that was Great Uncle Arthur.

Arthur wasn’t making a statement. He wasn’t resisting the schedule. He was just… Arthur. Always a little behind. Missed weddings. Showed up after dessert. Once arrived forty-five minutes late to his own surprise party. Always halfway through a story when the rest of us were already packing up to leave.

Naturally, someone joked that he’d probably be late to his own funeral.

And, as legend has it, they were right.

It was the late 1800s. The coach carrying Uncle Arthur’s body seventy-five miles to the cemetery broke down en route. The family, already assembled graveside in stiff collars and Sunday gloves, had to wait until the next day for Arthur to show up one final time.

Then again, that’s how legends are made.

## Keeping time

Some said they should’ve sent him by rail.

In those days, no one took time more seriously than the railroad. Trains ran on tight timetables. Whole towns set their clocks by the whistle. A late train wasn’t just inconvenient, it was dangerous. In a single-track system, a five-minute error could cause a head-on collision.

How did they keep it all coordinated? With watches.

Not just any watches; high-precision railroad chronometers, synchronized religiously. The whole network depended on each conductor’s ability to keep exact time. For watchmakers, landing a railroad contract was the ultimate validation.

But under that pressure, mistakes could happen.

One watch company lost their contract after “fixing” a design flaw. One gear in their top model wore out after three years. A young engineer recommended replacing it with a stronger alloy, one that would last ten years.

They did.

And the watches immediately stopped keeping time.

## Flaw or feature?

The gear wasn’t broken. It was brilliant.

The original, softer alloy was designed to wear down in a specific way. Its tiny sacrifices balanced out the imperceptible wear in the rest of the system. It wasn’t a weakness, it was a counterweight. A built-in correction.

When they “improved” it, they broke the whole design.

## Our people are like that

Every tribe — chosen or inherited — has its own strange rhythm. Family will sometimes wonder aloud why you married that person, or hang out with those people. Some folks might stop inviting you, just because you always bring that one friend.

And that’s okay.

You can’t live your life running someone else’s train, on someone else’s time, with a watch that doesn’t keep your time.

## The people who keep time with you

*Inner Fire* orbits around the ideas of personal autonomy and unbiased thinking. But thinking for yourself isn’t just about autonomy and independence. Yes, it takes introspection, humility, and discernment. But it also takes something more foundational: interdependence.

The best lives are lived among people whose presence makes your weirdness work.

We’re not perfect, but in the right crowd, our imperfections fit perfectly:
- 

The friend with the furrowed brow doesn’t smile often, but when things get serious, they take the lead.
- 

The one who cries at the drop of a hat reminds everyone to stay soft.
- 

The overbearing refund-demanding friend? They’ll fight for you when you’re too tired to fight for yourself.
- 

And then there’s the funny one, who turns every comment into something golden.

When one of them’s out sick, the group feels it. When someone’s in the hospital, they don’t need to worry who’s showing up. Someone will.

Family sometimes doesn’t fit, not because there’s something wrong, but because you were all thrown into a crucible together, each of you trying to stand out, often in the wrong ways. Perfect families are rare.

But perfectly imperfect groups of friends? They’re everywhere.

Find yours, not because they’re flawless, but because they wear out in the exact way that keeps you in balance.
