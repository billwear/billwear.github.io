*What it means to stay transparent, creative, and alive—without setting yourself on fire.*

For as long as I’ve had a terminal window open (52 years last month), I’ve been drawn to the ideals of open source: transparency, community-driven evolution, permissionless innovation. Those values didn’t just shape how I learned to write code; they rewired how I thought about work, creativity, and even relationships.

So when I joined Canonical, I felt like I’d landed in the right dimension. This was a company that *stood out* for trying to do something nearly impossible in tech: keep “open” in open source, while building a commercially sustainable business that could support hundreds of people around the world. 

And honestly? They pull it off in ways that most companies wouldn’t even attempt. Canonical walks a line that would buckle under lesser leadership. They sponsor the upstream. They fight for standards. They give away tools the industry depends on, and do it *with* intent, not as an afterthought.

But as anyone who lives in the open will tell you, the challenge isn’t the code. It’s the culture.

Because the world around open source is still very much closed.

We live in a time of walled gardens, opaque decision-making, performance reviews behind digital curtains. And most days, that’s fine, maybe even necessary. 

But if you’re someone like me, who lives a little too close to the light, who processes publicly, who documents everything in a Markdown file and keeps their thoughts forkable, you start to feel the tension.

You’re not mad at the castle. But you still wish the drawbridge lowered a little more often.

---

## what it means to live an open-source life

It’s not about your GitHub graph. It’s not even about software.

To live an open-source life is to move through the world with a kind of principled transparency. To document your process. To iterate in public. To default to “share.” To build in such a way that your future self — or some curious stranger — can fork your methods, rebase your habits, and maybe even submit a patch.

But here’s the catch: That kind of living is *costly*.

It takes time to document your life. It takes effort to choose truth over optics. It takes an absurd amount of inner strength to hit "Publish" on a half-finished idea and trust the world not to crush you for your vulnerabilities.

And when you do all that within the walls of a modern job? You run up against friction fast. Even at a company like Canonical, where the open-source DNA runs deep, you learn quickly that internal clarity doesn’t always mean public transparency. Some conversations stay inside the firewall. Some strategy needs protective silence to survive and grow.

And that’s not hypocrisy. That’s wisdom and balance.

The truth is, I’ve never seen another company do more *work* to keep open source alive in the marketplace. It would be far easier for Canonical to shut things down, protect their turf, and act like a platform. Instead, they fight upstream battles and defend the commons *and* keep the lights on for hundreds of contributors, engineers, writers, and designers like me.

But that doesn’t mean it’s always easy to live openly while surviving inside that tension.

So I had to find a way to make peace with the duality.

---

## the system i built to keep my sanity

I don’t always get it right. I’m prone to idea churn, tactical rabbit holes, networks of side-quests, and radical rethinks of my own system every few weeks. But what’s slowly taken shape is a quiet little toolkit—an *emacs-shaped life raft* that lets me live out loud without capsizing.
- 

**A journal** that captures my daily emotional telemetry (*emologent*)—so I know where I’ve been, why I felt the way I did, and how I really feel right now.
- 

**An org-mode dashboard** that shows me my actual life at a glance: project progress, personal blockers, even metabolic goals.
- 

**A capture tree** for every weird fragment, marketing spark, errant research trail, or sewing pattern idea, so it gets swept out of the main path, with an air-tag to come back later.
- 

**A commitment to single-handling**: Finish what I’m doing or go to bed trying. Then get back on it tomorrow. Stay on task till the task is done. 

This isn’t *lifehacking*. This is self-debugging.

Some days, it feels like a fragile miracle. Other days, it’s just the grind of showing up. But over time, it’s how I’ve learned to build trust with myself.

---

## what i’m learning from all this

Living an open-source life means more than just building in public; it means **becoming forkable**.

It means organizing your thoughts so someone else can use them. It means building habits that are auditable. It means being the kind of person whose methods *can be cloned* because you’ve actually made them portable.

This isn’t about branding. It’s about *living in such a way that your progress helps someone else move forward*.

That’s the point of the Inner Fire. I’m trying to chronicle this process — not from the mountaintop, but from the switchbacks. It’s a messy trail. There are false starts. There’s the ever-present fear of looking foolish. But I think it’s worth it. Because if I can figure out how to live a creative, open-source life *without giving up security or soul*, then maybe someone else can too.

That’s the experiment.
