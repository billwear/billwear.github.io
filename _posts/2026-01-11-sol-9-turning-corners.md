There are some blind spots in my basic sewing skill. I’m trying to turn ideas not *just* into cloth, but into functional things that you’ll see for their beauty and usefulness, and *forget* that they’re cloth. Fancy mug and phone rugs. Fashionable tote bags and purses. Other small things that have tended to sell well for me in the past. 

Turning pieces with pockets is currently my big challenge.  How do you make several pieces turnable with a single seam and still have it come out clean? How do you sandwich fabric, lining, lined pocket and gussets into one seam, with seam allowances that work instead of fighting you?

## Postponing the YouTubes

I could study this like a General and plan this out forever, or I could take scraps and see what happens. I have plenty of canvas, so I start there, but I have even more muslin, so I’ll probably switch to that — or just scraps — until I get this right. The goal is simple: get a pocket into a turnable piece using a single seam, with stiffening, and see what breaks.

The trick for a pocket turns out to be a sandwich. The pocket piece goes between the facing right sides of the outer layer and lining. Wrong sides of the pocket against the right side of the piece it belongs to. The other piece goes on top of that, then the stiffener. Sew the perimeter like normal, leaving a turning opening. Turn it, and there it is. An un-hemmed pocket, cleanly captured with one seam:[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1_NG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8b548b6c-389f-48da-b74b-415c0b52eba5.heic)

It works — but it’s not good yet.

The pocket top needs to be doubled and stitched along the exposed edge, then flipped. The corners are messy. They bunch up, stiff and blunt, like they don’t know where to go. That tells me something about the seam allowances: There’s too much material trying to occupy too little space.

I trim a little. Not enough to weaken the seam, just enough to release pressure.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QO89!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F41f383a6-7a6e-4cb7-bf4b-7c44f97f9a7f.heic)

> 

*Watching corners misbehave like this reminds me why I’m comfortable practicing with scraps.*

*In college, I once wrote an essay that a professor encouraged me to submit to a national literary criticism journal. It wasn’t because the idea was clever. It was because I stayed with a single work long enough to understand how it moved. I read The Seagull by Anton Chekhov something like eighty-five times. I drafted the essay through twenty-five progressive drafts, each one cutting closer to what mattered.*

*At some point, I stopped trying to explain the play and started listening to it. The characters began to feel like instruments in an orchestra. Each carried a different weight, a different register. I treated Nina’s story as a solo line — a flute — moving through a larger score. Chekhov wasn’t showing off. He was weaving. He let the meaning emerge by how the lines crossed and resolved.*

*I didn’t think of it as discipline then. I just knew that rushing ruined things, and staying made them clearer. And that’s what I need to do with these little pocketed pencil coasters I’m apparently building.*

Research confirms what the fabric is already telling me: sharp ninety-degree corners trap bulk. If you want a clean turn, the seam allowance has to echo the curve you want on the outside. So I angle the cuts more deliberately this time. Still a little blunt, but better.

I turn the piece again, using a telescoping antenna to push out the corners. Improvised tools have a way of revealing what matters, and since I’m working hard to blend my three favorite hobbies — sewing, electronics, and writing — that irony is unmistakeable.  

The fabric responds. The pocket top, now flipped and stitched, behaves itself. The corners are cleaner. Not elegant yet, but serviceable. An interim skill is starting to take shape, literally.

I don’t love dog-bone corners, but that’s tomorrow’s problem. For now, the important thing is that I can feel the difference between cutting for speed and cutting for shape.[](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zdfi!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c1b210f-e500-4087-91ab-8780a746e6ac.heic)

At this point, the temptation is to keep experimenting, to chase ever-more-clever constructions. But there’s a reason to take a step back. The corners aren’t pristine. I need to take the pocket out of the equation for a while and see if I can just build turned table “rugs.” Mug rugs. Phone carpets. Pencil command centers.

In other words, it’s time to slow down and make a *lot* of nicely paired mug rugs and phone rugs. Repetition will teach me more than novelty here. Perfect corners don’t come from thinking harder — they come from doing the same thing carefully, over and over. That’s what Sol 10 is about. That’s tomorrow. In the meantime,

**Burn slow. Build deep. Be the proof.**
