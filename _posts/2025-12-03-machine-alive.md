There was a silence after her hand left the machine — not awkward, just loaded.  
The kind of silence where both people know something happened but neither of them can name it without changing the shape of the room.

She stepped back half a pace, pretending to study the machine’s stitch selector.  
He pretended to give her space.

Neither pretense held.

“You moved like you felt something,” she said finally, a little too casual to be accidental.

Not accusing. Not suspicious. Just… witnessing.

He kept his focus on the machine’s housing. The metal was cool, grounded, a physical anchor he could hold to keep the internal circuitry from showing through his face.

“It’s been a long time since I used anything like this,” he said.

She didn’t buy it. Not entirely. Her head tilted the smallest amount, the way someone adjusts their angle to get a better read on a person who isn’t quite translating cleanly.

He could feel her recognition forming — not the truth, but the pattern: that he reacted like someone who’d used *different* tools, under *different* conditions, with *different* instincts.

She stepped closer again, slower this time, like she was approaching the boundary they both felt redraw itself a few minutes earlier.

“Can I…” She paused, searching for the right phrasing. “Can I try threading it? I want to feel how it moves.”

That was new. Not a helpful neighbor thing. Not a curious hobbyist thing. It was someone responding to **the energy in the room**, not the machine.

He nodded.

She leaned over the worktable. Her hair brushed his arm — accidental, but not neutral. She reached for the machine again, but when her fingers touched metal this time, she hesitated. Not from fear. From **recognition**.

“Huh,” she murmured. “It’s… warm.”

“It’s off,” he said quickly.

“No — not heat.” Her eyes lifted to his, sharp and searching. “More like it’s… waiting for something.”

He froze. Only for a fraction of a breath, but she caught it. She caught everything tonight.

“That’s weird, right?” she asked softly.

He wanted to lie. He also knew he couldn’t—not with her, not tonight. The fire inside him was too active, too directional, too exposed by the act of choosing a tool that wasn’t just a tool.

“It feels alive,” she whispered, fingertips tracing the edge of the bobbin case. “Not machine-alive. Something else.”

He swallowed. The air in the room felt denser again, like the moment was narrowing into a corridor he couldn’t step out of.

“What makes you say that?” he asked.

She didn’t look away.

“Because you touched it,” she said, “and then everything about you changed.”

The fire in his chest tightened, not in alarm — in **confirmation**. She wasn’t seeing the truth of him but she had found the outline around it.

It was enough.

She stepped back, exhaling slowly, as though recalibrating her own internal readouts.

“You don’t have to tell me anything,” she said. “I just… want you to know I notice things. And I don’t scare easy.”

It wasn’t flirtation. It wasn’t interrogation. It was a boundary shifting again — narrowing, but still not quite breaching.

He looked at the machine, then at her, then at the space between them that had began to behave like its own kind of field.

“Okay,” he said quietly. “Good to know.”

She nodded once, like they’d sealed something without naming it. Then she placed her hand on the machine a second time.

This time, it wasn’t his reaction she noticed. It was her own.
