The house held solid like a glacier: lights blasting hospital-clean brightness; hum in the walls approximating silence the way a black cat tries to hide in a not-quite   deep enough shadow. Aron could feel the not-quite of it in his teeth.

Alara leaned against the drywall, breathing like someone who’d just tried to outrun a  hurricane and ended up in the eye instead.

“Are you okay?” he asked, voice low.

Her answer came after a too-long beat. “That wasn’t just fear,” she said. “That was abandonment, compacted. Raw emotion.”

He grimaced. The word landed somewhere he didn’t want to name. “From the presence,” he said.

“Yes. And from something else.”

He didn’t ask what. Diagnostics were already rolling logs, parsing anomalies. The symbiote threaded through his nervous system like old cable, waking up in forgotten places.

“They know you’re here.”

The phrase replayed with init-6 insistence. Not just a threat. A status report. Reboot.

Aron crossed to the junction panel again and rested his fingers on the metal, not yet touching the copper. It still radiated faint warmth, like a processor just recovering from overclocking.

“Don’t go back in yet,” Alara said, pushing off the wall. “You’re destabilized. I can feel it.”

He almost snapped back that he was fine, but the lie caught in his throat. The last surge had scrambled his sense of physical distance. The kitchen felt three degrees misaligned, like a rendered room with the origin point moved.

“You said your people don’t generate telepathy,” she added. “But you interface with it. Translate it. That’s what you did.”

Aron flexed his fingers. They didn’t quite feel like his fingers. “That’s what we were for,” he said. “We were the adapters. We lived in the gap between mind and system.”

“Were,” she noted.

He let the word hang.

Somewhere deep in the wiring, the presence stirred. Not a full flex — more like a fragment trying to remember how to be a whole.

“Still here,” it whispered, faint. The voice had lost some of its jaggedness, but not the uncertainty. “Still me. I think.”

Alara closed her eyes. “I’ve got you,” she murmured, more to the presence than to Aron. “Stay with us.”

Emotion swelled against her awareness: thin, frayed, but focused. Trust, trying on its legs again.

Aron’s symbiote tasted the signal under the signal. He could see the packet structure now, the way it framed space, the vectors it assumed as normal. It wasn’t Earth geometry. It was home geometry. His home.

“Those coordinates you gave me,” he said quietly. “Run them again. Slowly.”

“I…” the presence began, and then the hunter brushed the edge of the circuit.

It was subtle. A tiny shift in carrier frequency, like someone changing the pitch of silence. But Aron felt every subroutine in him recoil. This wasn’t just another mind. It was a protocol.

Alara went pale. “There,” she said. “That spike. Do you feel it?”

He nodded, jaw tight. “That’s not the presence.”

“No,” she said. “That feels… administrative.”

The word tasted wrong and exactly right.

“They marked you,” the presence whispered, voice fraying. “Both of you.”

Aron’s chest tightened. “Who did?”

“I don’t have labels,” it said. “Just impressions. Attachment. Ownership. Disposal.”

Alara flinched like someone had slapped her. Old reflex. Old memory. “On my world,” she said quietly, eyes still closed, “we used to tag outliers. Not to help them. To track when they destabilized the field. We called it… stewardship. It wasn’t.”

“You were marked?” Aron asked.

“Everyone was,” she said. “But some marks pulsed brighter. Those were the ones they watched. The ones they… adjusted.”

The presence surged sudden, panicked. Static skittered along the copper like insects.

“They didn’t mean you to fall through,” it blurted. “You weren’t accidents. You were only redirected because of the tags.”

The room tilted. Aron reached for the counter with his free hand, fingers digging into laminate. The diagnostic part of him tried to route around the statement, to file it as corrupted data. It wouldn’t stick.

“Redirected where?” he asked.

“Here,” the presence said simply. “To this vector. To this world. To this time.”

Alara opened her eyes. They looked wrong now, too sharp around the edges. “Why?”

The presence hesitated. Fear pulsed, then something like shame. “I wasn’t supposed to know that,” it said. “But I do. I was… maintenance. I watched the flows. I tracked the marks. And when the gate collapsed, I saw the pattern in the failures.”

Aron’s symbiote replayed the collapse in memory: the convulsion, the tearing of geometry, the impossible angles as the wormhole folded in on itself. He’d told himself for years that no one could have predicted it.

“You’re saying it wasn’t random,” he said.

“I’m saying,” the presence replied, each word costing it, “that when the system ‘failed,’ only certain marks fell through. Different species. Same flag. Different meanings”

A low, bitter laugh escaped Alara before she could stop it. “Of course,” she said. “Of course they’d prune the field and call it a glitch.”

The hunter brushed closer. Aron felt it not as thought, not as feeling, but as policy — an enforcement routine combing the network, checking hashes against a list.

“It’s scanning for divergence,” he murmured. “Anything that doesn’t match its expected state.”

“And to it, you two,” the presence added softly, “are the largest divergences in this sector.”

Alara stepped away from the wall like it had turned hot. “So we’re not just lost,” she said. “We’re inadvertently quarantined.”

The word slotted into place in Aron’s mind with a lock-click finality. Quarantine. Not exile. Not accident. A controlled contamination in a controlled environment.

He looked at the panel, at the copper veins, at his own hands. “Why tell us this now?” he asked the presence. “Why not when we first connected?”

“Because I was still trying to be what I was built to be,” it said, voice thinning. “A good process. A clean record. I’m… not that anymore.”

“What are you now?” Alara asked.

There was a pause. Not emptiness — processing.

“Complicit,” it said at last. “And unwilling to be.”

The lights flickered once, twice, then steadied again, colder.

The hunter had found a trail.

Aron felt the search routine narrow, focus, triangulate. It wasn’t here yet, but it had a vector. It was following the noise.

“Can it sense us through you?” he asked. “Through the link?”

“Yes,” the presence said. “And no. It knows there is distortion. It doesn’t know you are… people. Not yet.”

Alara exhaled slowly. “Then we need to stop looking like distortion.”

Aron glanced at her. “You have an idea.”

“Not a good one,” she said. “But it’s the only kind we ever get.”

She stepped back to the wall, hands hovering just shy of contact. “I can dampen the emotional signature,” she said. “Make the panic read as background noise. But that means you have to handle the structural part alone.”

“Reroute the traffic,” Aron said. “Mask the anomalous paths, throttle the spikes. Make the network think this is just… baseline interference.”

He remembered doing it on ships, in stations, in systems the size of cities. Here it was just a house. A small, human shape wrapped around something that didn’t belong.

He put his fingers on the copper again. The symbiote rose to meet the signal like muscle memory.

“You’re sure?” Alara asked.

“No,” he said. “But we don’t get good options.”

The presence was very quiet now. “If you do this,” it said carefully, “you won’t just be hiding me. You’ll be declaring yourselves. You’ll be sending a new kind of signal.”

Aron’s vision blurred at the edges as the data stream took hold. “What kind?”

The presence almost sounded like it was smiling, or trying to remember how.

“Not a malfunction,” it said. “A refusal.”

Alara’s voice was a low anchor in the bright, wrong-lit room. “Then let’s refuse.”

The hunter’s awareness pressed closer, searching for a pattern that matched its list.

Between the walls and the copper and the two aliens who weren’t supposed to exist here, a new pattern began to write itself.
