To tend a flame, you must stand near it — not so close you get burned, but not so far that it gets out of hand. 

To tend the mind, you must learn to do the same thing. It isn’t about force or declaration, but presence — and careful, precise actions.  You must be both watchful and intentional.

Remember those rare moments — maybe it’s that quiet space just before dawn, maybe in the wreckage of another emotional hangover — when you “come to” and you realize that the fire hasn’t stopped?  

It’s roaring  on, burning through thought after thought with little or no meaningful evaluation. But it will listen.

In that next golden moment when, like the prodigal son, you “come to yourself,” actively recognize that tiny space between thoughts where you can actually *think about what you’re thinking about*. 
- 

*“I like this.”*
- 

*“I don’t like this.”*
- 

“*I hate this.”*

The mind responds to attention like a flame responds to oxygen.  

Calendars, goals, “powering through,” military discipline?  They only take you so far, and they don’t fully leverage the nuclear furnace of the managed mind.

But here’s the hard part.

You don’t manage it by going with the flow, or yelling at yourself, or getting depressed.  Step back. Don’t judge, just watch where it’s going, and then gently nudge it in a new direction.  

Maybe you have to pour a little water here or there, move the ideas around, or flick embers back into the fireplace.

This is the work, the stewardship of our minds. 

Notice the spark before it becomes a blaze. Shape the flame with tiny moves, not sweeping changes. Redirect it with breath, pause, and practiced stillness.  

Think about managing the bonfire: It seems ready to burn out of the pit, but you’re not sure.  You stand stock still, at the ready, hose in one hand, shovel in the other, watching. *Paying very close attention*.

You’d be surprised how quickly wayward thoughts vanish when you notice them, and then just say to yourself, “I hate thinking this way.” 

You won’t be a monk overnight.  
But you might be a better version of yourself by noon.

Learn how to tend the flame of insatiable thought. Then you won’t have a brush fire, you’ll have a *forge*. And with a reliable forge, you can make useful, rare and valuable things.
